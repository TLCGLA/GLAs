WHAT IS A THESIS STATEMENT?
  • A thesis statement is a one or two sentence summary of your argument placed near the beginning of the essay.
3
WHY SHOULD YOUR ESSAY HAVE A THESIS STATEMENT?

  • It defines the purpose of your essay for yourself and the reader.
  • It improves the organization of ideas in your essay.
4
THE THESIS STATEMENT IS THE GUIDE FOR YOUR ESSAY

  • A strong thesis will guide you to writing the information necessary to proving your point.  All information in the essay should contribute to proving the thesis.  If the information does not help support the thesis, it should be removed from the essay.


  • As you work your way through a draft, refer back to the thesis frequently.  Ask yourself:
  • What does my reader need to learn to accept my thesis statement?
  • What is the best order for presenting my information?
5
HOW TO GENERATE A THESIS STATEMENT IF THE TOPIC IS ASSIGNED
  • Step 1:  Turn the assignment into a question.  For example:
  • Assignment:  Write an essay on the effects of cell phone use on modern society.
  • Question:  How have cell phones affected modern society?


  • Step 2:  Compose one or two sentences answering that question.  For example:
  • Answer:  Face-to-face interaction has been replaced by interaction with phones, making social relations more impersonal.
6
HOW TO GENERATE A THESIS STATEMENT IF THE TOPIC IS NOT ASSIGNED
  • Step 1:  Think of a question you would like to answer in your essay.
  • Step 2:   Write a one or two sentence answer for the question.  This is your thesis statement.


  • Keep in mind the following:
  • Choose a topic that could be debated.  If you pick a topic about which no one disagrees, you dont have anything to defend in your essay.
  • Stick to only one main idea
  • State your position on the subject by ensuring that you answer the question that you asked.
7
THESIS STATEMENT CRITERIA:
  • A strong thesis statement accomplishes the following:
  • It summarizes the whole essay in one sentence
  • The thesis should provide a preview for the essay.  Let readers know what they will read about.
  • It predicts, controls, and obligates
  • The thesis is a guide.  Use it to help you construct a logical argument.
  • It makes a unified claim that is debatable (readers may disagree) or that needs to be proved (readers need convincing).
  • The thesis should have some controversy.  If all readers agree about a topic, there is little point in writing about it.
  • It should be narrow and specific.
  • The thesis should provide a clear picture of the point you plan to make in an essay.
8
HOW TO CHECK IF YOU HAVE A STRONG THESIS STATEMENT

  • Does the thesis provide a preview for the essay?
  • Can it be used to guide your ideas?
  • Does it make a controversial claim?
  • Is it narrow and specific?
9
DEVELOP A THESIS
  • Topic:  Studying foreign language in a community college


  • Attempt 1:  This paper will discuss foreign language studies in community college.
  • Doesnt give a clear picture of what the paper will be about
  • Too broad and vague to support effectively


  • Attempt 2:  There are many benefits and drawbacks to studying a foreign language in community college.
  • Doesnt make a controversial claim about the topic
  • Doesnt give a clear picture of what the paper will be about
10
EFFECTIVE THESIS
  • Community college students should be required to study a foreign language for at least one year because it will prepare them to compete in a global marketplace.


  • Evaluation:
  • This thesis gives readers a clear picture of what the paper will be about.
  • The writer can use the thesis to guide him/her while constructing the paper.
  • The thesis makes a debatable claim; not everyone will agree that students should be required to study a foreign language.
11
UNIFYING CLAIM
  • A good thesis includes a unifying claim and mayor may notinclude subtopics.


  • A unifying claim is the overall assertion or idea of your thesis and of your paper, which youll back up with support and specific evidence in the body of your essay.
12
RELATING YOUR CLAIM TO YOUR SUBTOPICS
  • Depending on the nature of the writing assignment, you may be asked to include specific subtopics in your thesis statement.


  • Subtopics are smaller topics within your overall focus.  If you are writing a thesis statement with subtopics, most of the time limiting them to three works best.  However, including subtopics does not mean you do not need a claim. It must be clear to the reader how your subtopics are related.
13
EXAMPLE OF THESIS WITH SUBTOPICS
BUT MISSING A UNIFYING CLAIM

  • Twenty-somethings today like to eat, shop, and play video games.


  • (Saying merely that twenty-somethings like certain activities is not a strong enough idea to make the relationship between those activities clear).
14
EVALUATION
  • This is a weak thesis statement  because if the subtopics were taken away all that remains is the idea of twenty-somethings liking something!  This is not a claim it is possible to disagree with, nor is it one that would need proof, since most people like something.
15
EXAMPLE OF THESIS WITH SUBTOPICS RELATED BY A UNIFYING CLAIM
  • Because of the medias influence, twenty-somethings of today are sophisticated consumers of fashion, food, and technology.


  • (The unifying claim here is that the media has made twenty-somethings sophisticated consumers.)
16
EVALUATION:
  • The second thesis could stand on its own without the subtopics if necessary or desired.  Readers would also be able to disagree and argue that teens have always been more willing to try new things simply because they are young and adaptable, and that we should not give so much power to the media in forming theories about people.
17
SUBTOPICS SHOULD PREDICT YOUR TOPIC SENTENCES

  • If you do specify subtopics in your thesis statement, youll most likely need to use them in each topic sentence of your essay.  A topic sentence is usually the first or second sentence of one of your body paragraphs.  A strong topic sentence will repeat the claim made by the thesis and relate it to the appropriate subtopic, thereby stating the main idea of the paragraph.
18
EXAMPLE OF TOPIC SENTENCE THAT REPEATS THE THESIS CLAIM AND RELATES IT TO THE SUBTOPIC:


  • Television and the movies have made sushi a surprisingly coveted meal for young adults with disposable income.


  • (This topic sentence relates to the overall claim of the successful example thesis because it states the medias influence and addresses the first subtopic, which is food.  The paragraph could then be developed with examples of television and movie references to sushi.)
19
A SAMPLE THESIS STRUCTURE:
ALTHOUGHBECAUSE

  • One way to structure a thesis is to use an Althoughbecause structure.
  • Begin with Although, which is a counterargument for your topic
  • Then, state your claim
  • Finally, add because to explain your reasons

A THESIS CANNOT BE A FRAGMENT
  • It must be expressed in a complete sentence.


  • NO:  How life is in the Salem Housing project.


  • YES:  The Salem Housing project damages the residents psychological well-being through the bars on the windows, thin walls, and crowded conditions.
22
A THESIS TYPICALLY SHOULD NOT BE IN THE FORM OF A QUESTION

  • Often the answer to that question may function as the thesis.


  • NO:  Should eighteen year olds have the right to drink alcohol?
  • YES:  Eighteen year olds should have the right to drink alcohol; they have similar rights, like voting and enlisting in the military, that shows societys confidence in their maturity.
23
A THESIS SHOULD NOT INCLUDE PHRASES THAT INDICATE PERSONAL OPINION SUCH AS I THINK


  • They weaken the statement.


  • NO:  In my opinion, advertising sugary cereal to children contributes to childhood obesity.


  • YES:  Advertising sugary cereal to children contributes to childhood obesity.
24
A THESIS SHOULD NOT SIMPLY ANNOUNCE A TOPIC

  • Instead, you should share your argument about that topic.


  • NO:  This paper will discuss shootings on college campuses.


  • YES:  In order to prevent shootings, college campuses should implement random searches to stop students from bringing firearms to campus.
25
A THESIS SHOULD NOT BE A STATEMENT OF FACT.

  • Instead, it should make a debatable argument.


  • NO:  Facebook is a highly popular social media platform.


  • YES:  Facebook should take decisive action to prevent bullying among its users under 18.
26
A THESIS SHOULD NOT CONTAIN ELEMENTS THAT ARE NOT CLEARLY RELATED

  • NO:  All novelists seek the truth; therefore, some novelists appear to be good psychologists.


  • YES:  In their attempt to probe human nature, many novelists appear to be good psychologists.
27
A THESIS SHOULD NOT BE EXPRESSED IN VAGUE LANGUAGE.

  • NO:  Bad things have resulted from religion being taught in the classroom.


  • YES:  Religion as a part of the school curriculum should be avoided because a persons religious beliefs are highly personal and require individual commitment.
28
A THESIS SHOULD NOT BE EXPRESSED IN MUDDLED OR INCOHERENT LANGUAGE

  • NO:  Because body dysmorphia, anorexia nervosa, bulimia, and fat shaming are common among adolescents, the global media is complicit by glorifying enhanced (airbrushed and photoshopped) images of emaciated models.


  • YES:  With their reliance on perfect, skinny models, the global media bear some responsibility for body image issues among adolescents.
29
A THESIS SHOULD NOT BE WRITTEN IN OVERLY FIGURATIVE LANGUAGE.

  • NO:  Obstetricians should be trained to cut the Gordian knot when deciding to perform a Caesarian section.


  • YES:  Obstetricians should be trained to make a swift decision when deciding to perform a Caesarian section.
