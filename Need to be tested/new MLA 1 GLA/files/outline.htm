WHAT IS MLA?
  • MLA stands for Modern Language Association, which is a professional association for scholars of language and literature.
  • The MLA sets guidelines for proper formatting and citation style.
  • Most classes in the Humanities, like English, will use MLA formatting.
5
WHY DO WE USE MLA?
  • Avoid plagiarism:  Using MLA citation techniques properly ensures that you will not have plagiarized material in your assignment.


  • Build credibility:  By using MLA citation and formatting correctly, you show that you can meet academic standards.  Your readers are more likely to accept your assertions if they appear in an academically-correct format.


  • Be part of a scholarly community:  When citing in MLA style, you provide your readers with valuable information about your source material.  As fellow scholars, they may choose to learn more about the information you provide.  Your Works Cited page will become their guide to finding that material.

PART 1:
DOCUMENT FORMAT
8
FORMATTING REQUIREMENTS
  • Some MLA formatting requirements apply to the entire document.  These are:
  • Margins:  One inch on all four sides
  • Spacing:  Use double spacing throughout the entire document.  Do not add extra spaces between paragraphs or sections
  • Font:  12 point professional font (Times New Roman or Calibri)
  • Header:  Your last name and page number should appear on every page in the top right hand corner.  The header should be half an inch from the top of the page.


  • Note:  Your instructor may have specific formatting requirements.  Be sure to review the syllabus or assignment instructions.
9
FORMATTING THE FIRST PAGE
  • Heading:  In the top left corner, include the following:
    • Your Name
    • Instructor’s Name
    • Class
    • Date

  • Title:  Include the title in the center of the page.  Do not use italics, bold, underline, or increase the font of the title.
  • Spacing:  Do not add extra spaces between the heading and title, or between the title and text.

PART 2:
IN-TEXT CITATION
16
GOALS
  • The goal of the in-text citation is to direct your readers to your sources briefly and clearly.
  • Your Works Cited page (part 3) will include detailed information about your sources; the in-text citations should give enough information to direct readers to the corresponding entry on the Works Cited page.
17
CITING IN MLA STYLE
  • To cite a quote using MLA style, you will need to include four different elements:
  • Quotation marks
  • Signal phrase
  • In-text citation
  • Works Cited page (Discussed in Part 3)
18
STEP 1:  “QUOTATION MARKS”
  • Quotation marks appear around any text that you did not write.
  • They tell the reader that another author (not you) is speaking.


  • Example:
  • “The whole concept of genetic giftedness turns out to be wildly off the mark—tragically kept afloat for decades by a cascade of misunderstandings and misleading metaphors.”
19
STEP 2:  SIGNAL PHRASE
  • Once the original text is in “quotation marks,” add a signal phrase.
  • A signal phrase indicates who is speaking in the quote.
  • It improves flow and gives important background information for the reader.
  • The signal phrase should provide the element that comes first in the entry on the Works Cited page (usually the author’s name).
  • Example:
  • According to David Shenk, “the whole concept of genetic giftedness turns out to be wildly off the mark—tragically kept afloat for decades by a cascade of misunderstandings and misleading metaphors.”
20
POSSIBLE SIGNAL PHRASES
  • You can use a wide variety of signal phrases.  The most common is probably “according to,” but you can also use any verb that means “says.”


  • For example:
  • David Shenk states
  • David Shenk argues
  • David Shenk emphasizes
  • David Shenk describes
  • David Shenk illustrates
  • David Shenk notes
  • David Shenk suggests
21
STEP 3:  IN-TEXT CITATION
  • After the quote, include the page number in parentheses.
  • This allows your reader to locate your source and learn more.


  • Example:
  • According to David Shenk, “the whole concept of genetic giftedness turns out to be wildly off the mark—tragically kept afloat for decades by a cascade of misunderstandings and misleading metaphors” (10).
22
OTHER POSSIBILITIES FOR IN-TEXT CITATION
  • If you do not name the author in the signal phrase, include his/her name in the in-text citation.
    • Example:  (Shenk 10).

  • If your work does not include page numbers (like many online sources), do not provide a page number.
    • Example:  If the author is named in a signal phrase…
    • Laura Moser describes the possible structure of a four-day school week, stating, “Friday attendance will be optional, and instead of regular classes, struggling students can go to tutorials and get more individualized attention from teachers.”


    • Example:  If the author is not named in a signal phrase…
    • One proposed structure for a four-day school week is, “Friday attendance will be optional, and instead of regular classes, struggling students can go to tutorials and get more individualized attention from teachers” (Moser).

PART 3:
WORKS CITED PAGE
25
WORKS CITED
  • The final step to properly citing sources in MLA format is a Works Cited page.
  • This page is an alphabetized list of all the sources you used to complete your assignment.
  • It is also a scholarly courtesy because it helps your readers locate the sources you used so they can learn more about your topic.
26
THE PHILOSOPHY BEHIND THE 8TH EDITION
  • In previous editions of MLA style, students would first determine the type of publication they were citing (book, online article, TV show, etc.), then find the correct citation style for that source.
  • This system became problematic because technology moved more swiftly than MLA citation updates.  Whenever a new technology became available (like Twitter), MLA had to devise a new kind of citation.
  • The 8th Edition corrects this problem.  It provides a template of core elements that most published sources will have.  Writers can use the template for any work they plan to cite.
27
CORE ELEMENTS
28
1.  AUTHOR.
  • The author is the person primarily responsible for producing the work.
  • Begin your citation with the author’s name.
  • Use last name, first name (for the first author only)
  • Two authors
    • Follow the same order that is used in the published work
    • Example:  Dorris, Michael, and Louise Erdrich.
  • Three or more authors
    • Use the last name, first name for the author listed first
    • Then, add “et al.” (meaning “and others”)
    • Example:  Burdick, Anne, et al.
  • Pseudonyms and User Names
    • Use the name the way it is published
    • Example:  @nerdist
  • No Author name:  skip directly to the work’s title
29
2.  TITLE OF SOURCE.
  • Provide the title in full exactly as it is found in the source.
  • However, you should follow standard capitalization rules.  You can learn more about capitalization here.
  • Use italics or “quotation marks” depending on the type of source you’re using.  Use italics if the source is self-contained, and use quotation marks if the source is part of a larger work.  (See page 2 of your worksheet for more specific detail).
  • For short, untitled messages (like a tweet), reproduce the full text instead of a title.
30
3. TITLE OF CONTAINER,
  • If the source is part of a larger whole, that larger whole is the container
    • Examples: a journal is a container for an article and a TV show is a container for a TV episode
  • The container is crucial for locating the source; in other words, readers need the name of the container in order to access your information.
  • The title of the container is usually italicized.
  • It is followed by a comma because the following information describes the container.
  • A source may have more than one container.  For example, a journal article from an online database will have two containers (the journal and the database).  Or a television show on a streaming service (like Netflix) will have two containers (the show title and the streaming service).
  • If there is more than one container, complete steps 3-9 for the first container.  Then, repeat steps 3-9 for the second container.
31
4.  OTHER CONTRIBUTORS,
  • This section gives you the opportunity to give credit to the other people (aside from the author) who participated in creating the source.
  • Name these contributors if their participation is important to your research.
    • Editors of scholarly editions/anthologies and translators should always be recorded because their contribution is key.
    • If the source has many contributors (like a film), select the ones most pertinent to your discussion of that source.
  • Precede each name with a description of the role, such as:
    • Adapted by Introduction by
    • Directed by Narrated by
    • Edited by Performance by
    • Illustrated by Translated by
32
5.  VERSION,
  • If the source indicates that is a version released in more than one form, identify the version in your citation.
  • Books:  note the edition
  • Other media:  note any special version information, such as the Director’s Cut for a film
33
6.  NUMBER,
  • The source may be part of a numbered sequence; provide that number to help readers locate your source.
  • Examples:
    • Multivolume book.  Indicate the volume number like this:  vol. 2
    • Journals may have volume and issue numbers.  Cite like this:  vol. 12, no. 4
    • Television series have seasons and episodes.  Cite like this:  season 4, episode 10
34
7.  PUBLISHER,
  • The publisher is the organization primarily responsible for producing a source or making it available to the public.
  • Book:  Look on the title or copyright page for the publisher.
  • Film and television series:  Select the company that has primary responsibility for releasing the source.  Often,  Amazon has this information available in Product Details.
  • Websites:  A website may be published by many types of organizations.  Look at the bottom of the page for a copyright notice to find the name of the publisher.
  • Omit the publisher’s name in the following sources:  periodicals (journal / magazine / newspaper), a self-published work, a website whose title is the same as the publisher, a website not involved in producing the works it makes available (like YouTube).  In the case of websites like YouTube, name the site as the container, not the publisher.
35
PUBLICATION DATE,
  • Provide the date your source was published.
  • If the date was published on more than one date, use the date most meaningful for your use of the source.
  • Write as much of the date as is available (day / month / year).
  • Some online sources also include a time.  Include that information if it is available, like this:  25 Apr. 2013, 10:30 p.m.
36
9.  LOCATION.
  • The location helps your readers find where a text is located.
  • Print sources (for articles in an anthology, for example):  Use p. for a single page or pp. for a range of pages.
  • Online work:  Use the URL if your instructor requires it.
  • Physical object that you experienced firsthand (like a painting in a museum):  Use the name of the place and its city.
37
ONE LAST NOTE ABOUT THE CORE ELEMENTS
  • You may not have information for all nine steps of the core elements.  For example, not every work has other contributors (element 4).
  • Skip any element that does not pertain to the source you are citing.  So, if you cite a work without other contributors (element 4), move directly from the container (element 3) to the version (element 5).

TO FORMAT YOUR WORKS CITED PAGE
  • You should still have a header that includes your last name and page number.
  • Title the page Works Cited; do not use bold, underline, italics, or “quotation marks.”
  • List all entries in alphabetical order.  Do not number entries or use bullet points.
  • Double space; do not include extra space between entries.
  • Use hanging indentation (indent all lines after the first line of an entry).

HOW TO DO HANGING INDENTATION IN WORD
  • Highlight the citations.
  • Click on the box next to Paragraph.
  • Under Indentation, look for a box labeled Special.
  • Select Hanging Indentation and click OK.

CAUTIONS
  • MLA format does have revisions and updates every few years.  Make sure you’re using the most recent version.
