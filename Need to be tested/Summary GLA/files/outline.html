Part 1:  Preparing to Write a Summary
4
What is a summary?
  • A summary is your explanation of what a text is about.  You will write the main points of the text.


  • Note:  the word “text” here means anything you could read (like a book, an article, or a website) or see (a movie or a speech).
5
Imagine this…
  • You arrive to a class ten minutes before class begins.  There is a quiz based on a chapter that was assigned for homework.


  • Your friend in class didn’t read the chapter.  When you arrive, he asks you what the chapter was about.


  • When you respond, you tell him the most important points in the chapter—the information that is most likely to be on a quiz.


  • You have just summarized the chapter for your friend.
6
Why do we summarize?
  • Professors ask students to summarize to make sure the students understand what they have been reading.


  • At work, your supervisors may ask you to summarize so you can share information with them.  They may not have time to read the text themselves, and they will rely on you to teach them what they need to know.
7
Summary Guidelines
  • Summaries should be shorter than the original text.
  • Summaries will cover the main points of the original text.  When you write a summary, you will leave out details.
  • Summaries should be objective.  This means they will tell readers what the text is about, not what you think or feel about the text.
8
How do we prepare to summarize?
  • There are three steps that will help us prepare. They are:
  • Read carefully
  • Note the 6 W’s
  • Find the main points


  • We will go over each step individually.
9
Step 1:  Read carefully
  • If you know you will have to summarize an article, you should read carefully by:
  • Underlining or highlighting important phrases
  • Writing important words or phrases in the margins
  • Asking yourself questions to make sure you understand
  • Looking up any words you don’t know
  • Reading the article more than once
  • Note:  if you are summarizing a visual text (like a video), you can still do these steps.  Keep your notes on a separate piece of paper.
10
Sample:  Here’s what “reading carefully” looks like.
  • Read the notes on the first three paragraphs of the article below.
  • Then, watch this video for more help learning to read carefully.
11
Step 2:  Note the 6 W’s
  • The 6 W’s are:  who, what, when, where, why, and how (we include “how” on this list even though it doesn’t start with a “w”).


  • After carefully reading an article, make a note of the 6 W’s.  This will help you make sure you understand the text.
12
Sample:  The 6 W’s of the Bee Article
  • Who:  Humans who use pesticides, Dennis vanEngelstorp (University of Maryland scientist)
  • What:  Bees, Crops (Fruits, nuts, and berries)
  • When:  2013 (drop in bee population, California almond crop)
  • Where:  United States, California specifically
  • Why:  Humans cause some of the drop in bee population
  • How:  Humans use pesticides and fungicides (chemicals to kill pests and fungi that harm plants)
13
Step 3:  Find the Main Points
  • Once you have read the text carefully, review any notes you have made.  Then, develop a list of main points and key examples that you could use to explain the article.


  • Main points are the pieces of information readers need to understand about the article.
  • Key examples help show what the main points mean.  Although summaries don’t use many details, you may want to include one or two key examples.
14
Find the Thesis
  • After you’ve made your list of main points, try to find the author’s thesis.  In other words, what is the overall main point of the text?


  • One way to help find the thesis is to think about writing the text in one sentence.  If you only had one sentence to explain what the text is about, what would you say?
15
Sample:  Finding the Main Points
  • Here is the my list of main points of the honeybee article:
  • Reader’s Notes
  • Main Points:
  • Honeybee population is dropping
  • Humans cause the drop
  • Bees pollinate fruits, nuts, and berries
  • Loss of bees will affect food supply
  • Soon, we may not have enough bees
  • Key Example:
  • California Almond Crop, March 2013
  • Thesis:
  • The drop in the number of honeybees will cause problems for our food supply.
16
Part 2:  Drafting Your Summary
17
What is a draft?
  • A draft is an attempt at writing a text.  In this case, it is your first try at writing a summary.


  • Keep in mind that drafts don’t have to be perfect.  You can always fix them later.  The most important thing is to write your ideas down as complete sentences.  Once they are on paper, it is easier to correct them.
18
Helpful Hint
  • Before drafting a summary, talk about the text.  Explain what the text is about to a friend.  Your friend can ask questions to make sure that you know the text well.  This will also help you organize your ideas in an order that makes sense to you.
19
What do I need to write a draft?
  • To write your draft, you will need:
  • The original text with your notes
  • Your list of main points
  • A pen/pencil and paper or computer where you can write
20
How do I begin?
  • The first sentence of your summary should include the following information:
  • The title of the text
  • The name of the author
  • The thesis of the text


  • Think of the first sentence as your chance to give the readers the most important information about the text.  If they only read the first sentence, they will have a good idea what the text is about.
21
Sample First Sentence
  • Here is the first sentence of a summary of the bee article we’ve been reading:


  • Brandon Keim’s article “One-Third of U.S. Honeybee Colonies Died Last Winter, Threatening Food Supply” shows that the drop in in the number of honeybees will cause problems for our food supply.


  • Note:  The first sentence uses the thesis I wrote when making a list of the main points of the bee article.  It is much easier to write a good summary if you take good notes when reading.
22
Additional Sentences
  • After the first sentence, write the other main points of the text.  Remember, the reader wants the most important information from the article.


  • You can use a few key examples, but most of the time, don’t include details.


  • Use your notes on the article and your list of main points to help you write the rest of the summary.
23
Sample Additional Sentences
  • Bees pollinate fruit, nuts, and berries, providing food for humans.  If the bee population falls, humans will have less access to the food that bees provide.  For example, in March 2013, California’s almond crop was barely pollinated.  Scientists believe that humans are partially responsible for the loss of honeybees because we use pesticides and fungicides that kill the bees.


  • Note:   When drafting these sentences, I relied heavily on my notes.  I’ve only included one specific example (the almond crop).  I left out many of the details in the article, such as the percentage of bees that have died.
24
Concluding Sentence
  • Your final sentence of a summary should be a conclusion.  Tell your readers why the author thinks his or her information is important.  In other words, why should readers care about the text?
25
Sample Concluding Sentence
  • Keim concludes that the loss of bees could have a serious effect on humans’ food supply, causing a shortage of many crops that we rely on.


  • Note:  I focus on Keim’s conclusion here.  This is the idea that he emphasizes in his article.
26
Let’s look at the whole draft together!
  • Brandon Keim’s article “One-Third of U.S. Honeybee Colonies Died Last Winter, Threatening Food Supply” shows that the drop in in the number of honeybees will cause problems for our food supply. Bees pollinate fruit, nuts, and berries, providing food for humans.  If the bee population falls, humans will have less access to the food that bees provide.  For example, in March 2013, California’s almond crop was barely pollinated.  Scientists believe that humans are partially responsible for the loss of honeybees because we use pesticides and fungicides that kill the bees. Keim concludes that the loss of bees could have a serious effect on humans’ food supply, causing a shortage of many crops that we rely on.
27
Does the draft meet summary guidelines?
  • Does our draft meet these guidelines?
  • Shorter:  The original article is 253 words.  The draft is 117 words.  It is shorter than the original.
  • Main Points:  The draft includes the most important points from the article.  It leaves out details.
  • Objective:  The draft explains what the author, Brandon Keim, writes about.  It doesn’t say what I thought or felt about Keim’s article.
  • In an earlier slide, we read about the guidelines for creating a good summary.  They are:
  • Summaries should be shorter than the original text.
  • Summaries will cover the main points of the original text.  When you write a summary, you will leave out details.
  • Summaries should be objective.  This means they will tell readers what the text is about, not what you think or feel about the text.
28
Where do we go from here?
  • Once you have all your ideas written in a draft, ask yourself these questions:
  • Do I include all the main points?  (If not, add the other important points.)
  • Do I include too many details?  (If so, decide which details to delete.)
  • Are the sentences in an order that makes sense?  (If not, try to put the sentences in a different order.)

Part 3:  Citing in Your Summary
30
Important Note
  • This section uses MLA (Modern Language Association) rules to explain how to cite.  If your instructor does not use MLA, be sure to ask him/her how to cite properly in the format they follow.


  • If you aren’t sure which format your instructor follows, be sure to ask him/her.
31
Tip 1:
  • Write your summary in your own way.  “Your own way” means using your own words and sentence structure.
  • This is why it is important to talk about the text with a friend.  You’ve already explained the ideas in your own way to your friend.  Now, you just have to write them down!
  • Write the first draft of your summary without looking at the original text.  This also forces you to write the ideas in your own way.  You can always check your facts against the article after you write your draft.
32
Tip 2:
  • Be sure to quote any phrases you didn’t write yourself.


  • Example:
  • Brandon Keim describes the current loss of bees as “an unsustainable decline that threatens the nation’s food supply.”


  • Use very few quotes in a summary.  Most of the summary should be written in your own way.
  • Note:  To learn more about quoting, take the MLA1 or APA1 workshop.  Or, you can ask TLC tutors for help.
33
Tip 3:
  • Be sure to use the author’s name and the title of the text in the first sentence.  This tells readers where your information comes from and gives credit to the original writer.


  • Correct: Brandon Keim’s article “One-Third of U.S. Honeybee Colonies Died Last Winter, Threatening Food Supply” shows that the drop in in the number of honeybees will cause problems for our food supply.


  • Incorrect: The drop in in the number of honeybees will cause problems for our food supply.
34
Tip 4:
  • If your summary comes from a text that uses page numbers (like a book chapter or magazine article), include the page number in parentheses at the end of the summary.


  • Example:  Let’s imagine that Keim’s article was printed on page 45 of a magazine.  Here’s what the last sentence would look like:
  • Keim concludes that the loss of bees could have a serious effect on humans’ food supply, causing a shortage of many crops that we rely on (45).
35
Tip 5:
  • Include a citation for the original text.  This may appear on a Works Cited or Reference page.  Or, it may appear after your summary.  Ask your instructor where s/he would like you to include your citation.


  • Example:  This example is in MLA style for an article published online.  Ask TLC tutors for help citing.


  • Keim, Brandon.  “One-Third of U.S. Honeybee Colonies
  • Died Last Winter, Threatening Food Supply.” Wired.  Condé Nast, 8 May 2013.  Web.  23 July 2014.
36
Part 4:  What NOT to Do in a Summary
37
Don’t Turn in a Summary without Proofreading
  • After you finish drafting your summary, be sure to proofread carefully.  Fix any spelling or grammar mistakes you may find.  You can also ask a TLC tutor to help you find and correct errors.


  • Note:  For help learning to proofread, take the Proofreading workshop.  You can also take a wide variety of grammar workshops to improve your skills.
38
Don’t Copy the Original Text
  • Don’t copy large amounts of the original text, even if you cite it properly.  (You can include brief quotes in a summary.)


  • Remember, a summary should be written in your own way.  Use your words and sentences to explain the original text.


  • Your professor wants to see that you understand what the text was about.  The best way to show that you understand is to write the summary in your own way.
39
Don’t Rewrite the Original
  • When writing the summary, don’t just rewrite the original text word for word.  Remember, the summary should be shorter than the original and it should leave out details.


  • Instead of rewriting the text word for word, imagine explaining the text to a friend who didn’t read it.
40
Don’t Include Your Opinion
  • When writing a summary, focus on what the text says.  Do not tell readers what you think or feel about the text.


  • Here are some examples of what you SHOULD NOT do:
  • Brandon Keim wrote a really interesting article about bees.  (“Interesting” tells us what you think about the article.)
  • Brandon Keim’s article on bees was repetitive.  (This sentence criticizes the article; a summary should just tell readers what the article is about.)
  • Brandon Keim’s article about dying bees made me sad.  (This sentence explains how you feel about the bees.)
41
Conclusion
  • Writing a summary may seem tricky at first.  But, as you practice, you will get better and better!


  • As you begin writing summaries, be sure to focus on explaining what the text is about.


  • If you need help writing summaries, work with a TLC tutor.  They are happy to help you!
