U.S. College Classroom Expectations
4
American School System at a Glance
  • Nursery School / Pre-K (3-4 years old)
  • Kindergarten and Elementary School (5-10 years old)
  • Middle School (11-13 years old)
  • High School (14-17 years old)
  • Community College / Undergraduate Programs (18 years & older)
  • Master’s Programs or Professional Schools
  • Doctoral Programs
5
Classrooms are Diverse

  • American college classrooms are highly diverse in terms of age, gender, race, language, ethnicity, and culture.
  • For example, at COC in 2013-2014, we had 18,514 students.
    • 48% Female and 52% Male
    • 43.4% Latino, 38.1% White, 5% Asian, and 4.8% African American
    • 34.2% of students are over 25
  • Although this may be uncomfortable at first, you have the benefit of interacting with a wide variety of people.
6
Classes Are Uniform in Learning Levels
  • Although your class is diverse in terms of age, gender, race, etc., the students are at very similar learning levels.
  • They are placed in the class either through placement tests or performance in previous classes.
  • Since you are all at the same level, the professor can meet the students’ needs.
  • The students can work as a team to understand the material better.
  • Note:  Many disciplines, like ESL or math, have multiple levels for their classes.  Be sure to talk to your counselor to find out what your next class will be.
7
Personal Responsibility and Initiative
  • In an American classroom, you are expected to take personal responsibility for your work.  This means:
    • Reading and studying extensively outside of the classroom.
      • To earn C’s, plan to spend six hours a week studying for a three unit class.
      • To earn A’s, plan to spend nine hours a week studying for a three unit class.
    • Coming to class prepared to work.  Bring your notebook, pen/pencil, any required readings for the day, and any other materials your professor requires.
    • Knowing and following the policies in the syllabus.
    • Asking the instructor questions when you don’t understand the material.
  • You should take initiative to ensure that you get the best education possible.  If you need help, ask for it (more on Seeking Help in Part 5).
8
Syllabus

  • On the first day of class, you will receive a Syllabus, which is an outline for the class.  The syllabus is a vital document that explains exactly what is expected of you over the course of the semester.
  • You can expect to see the following components in most syllabi:
  • Required Books
  • List of Assignments
  • Grading Policy
  • Attendance/Drop policy
  • Academic Integrity Policy
  • Office Hours/Location
  • Instructor’s contact information
  • Disability accommodations
  • Student Learning Outcomes
  • Assignment/Reading Calendar (this could also be a separate handout)
9
Using the Syllabus
  • Instructors expect students to be familiar with the policies listed in the syllabus.  They often do a great deal of work to make sure all their policies are clear to their students.
  • Bring your syllabus to class.
  • Review the syllabus periodically.
  • Check the syllabus before asking a question about course policies.
10
Academic Integrity in the Classroom
  • Your syllabus may include information on cheating and plagiarism.  It is vital that you understand what counts as cheating and plagiarism.  American colleges take plagiarism very seriously; you do not want to have a plagiarism incident on your academic record.
  • Different countries or schools may have a different understanding of plagiarism.
  • In the U.S. college system, we avoid plagiarism by accurately citing all of our sources.
  • Be sure to review COC’s Academic Integrity policy (in your handout).
  • You may also want to take a workshop on Avoiding Plagiarism or one of the citation techniques (MLA or APA).
  • If you are confused about plagiarism, cheating, or academic integrity, ask your instructor or a TLC tutor for help.

Part 2:
Role of the Professor
12
What Can You Expect from Your Professor?
  • In many countries, professors are respected because they keep a distance from their students.  Students are expected to listen to a professor and speak very little.  The practice of asking questions may be discouraged.
  • The same is not necessarily true in American college classrooms.  While some professors are very formal, many prefer a more casual, interactive relationship with their students.
  • You can expect to see a variety of teaching techniques, including lectures, class discussions, team activities, PowerPoint presentations, etc.
  • Professors will expect you to participate in student-centered learning activities (more information later in the workshop).
  • Professors will expect you to ask questions if you do not understand a topic.  In the United States, asking questions is not a sign of disrespect.
13
Class Discussions
  • Although professors may lead a class discussion, they will usually not dominate the discussion.
  • They will expect students to participate by voicing their own opinion and responding to their classmates.
  • Throughout this process, it is important to be respectful to all the students.  You do not have to agree with your classmates, but you do need to reply to them politely.
  • To prepare for a class discussion, be sure to read any assigned readings.  Come to class with questions or important points that you want to discuss.
  • For many instructors, discussions are an important part of class participation.  In order to get a good participation grade, you should be prepared to participate.
14
Student-Centered Learning
  • Most professors in the United States will use some aspect of student-centered learning.
  • This means that they will focus on actively engaging the students in the lesson.
  • Students should not expect to passively listen to a professor lecture.
  • Instead, be prepared to participate in activities such as debates, problem analyses, team work, etc.
  • These activities are a vital part of an American education.  They allow you to work with the material, your professor, and your classmates.
  • This will help you remember and use the material later in life (either on a test, in a paper, or in your career).
15
Communicating with Your Professor
  • Your professors will expect you to communicate with them, especially if you are struggling in the class.  Learn to be an advocate for your own education by communicating with professors.
  • Usually, your professor will let you know what to call him or her.  Don’t be surprised if a professor asks you to use their first name.  If the professor doesn’t tell you what to call him/her, use the title Professor and his/her last name.
  • Find out which communication channel your professor prefers.  Some professors prefer to use email, and others like to meet with students in person.
  • Visiting a professor’s office hours is a wonderful way to get to know your professor.  You can get answers to your specific questions or advice on your assignments.  Knowing your professor on a one-on-one basis can help in the future if you need to ask for a letter of recommendation.
16
Professor’s Role in Grading
  • Most instructors in American colleges will agree that grades are earned, not given.
  • This means that you as a student have a great deal of control over your grade—the better work you do, the better a grade you will get.
  • When it comes to grading, think of your professor as an evaluator.  She or he will judge your work based on certain criteria (like your mastery of the course material) and assign you an appropriate grade.
  • Do not think of grades as an opportunity for negotiation.  Professors won’t just give you a higher grade.
  • On the other hand, if you don’t understand your grade or believe your professor may have made a mistake, you should contact them.

Part 3:
Grades
18
Grades
  • Getting good grades is vital to student success.  Get a good start from the beginning of the semester!
  • Participation counts. Students often receive grades for participating in class discussions. Failing to do so could imply that you are not interested. So participate!
  • Review the comments on your papers closely.  Make sure you understand what they mean.  Then, learn how to correct any problems.  For example, if your instructor writes “cs” on your paper, it means that you have a comma splice.  It is your responsibility to learn how to correct a comma splice.
  • A few basic tips for improving grades – plan, practice, revise, & proofread. Ask for help when you need it!
19
Understanding Grades
  • Grade Chart









  • Your syllabus will show how your grades are distributed among assignments.  Review this section of your syllabus frequently.
  • Keep track of your grades so you know where you stand in the class.
  • Some instructors will use a point system.  This means each assignment is worth a certain amount of points.  At the end of the semester, the instructor will add up all of your points and find your percentage based on the total points for the class.
  • Other instructors will use a weighted system.  Each assignment will be worth a certain percentage of your final grade.
  • If you have questions about how your instructor calculates your grade, be sure to contact him or her.

Part 4: 
Active Participation
21
Expectations
  • Professors in American colleges expect you to be an active participant in class.
  •  This means speaking frequently.
  • You may participate in class discussions, team activities, or peer review.
  • Don’t be too shy to speak.  The more you practice engaging in these activities, the more comfortable you will feel.
22
Opportunities to Participate
  • Class activities are done in pairs, in groups, or alone, so you ought to be confident while sharing your views.
  • In a full-class discussion, be sure to raise your hand to indicate that you want to speak.
  • When you work with a study group, you have the opportunity to discuss the material with your peers and prepare for exams.  This can be a very helpful strategy.
  • The Learning Center (TLC) provides many opportunities for working on team projects, such as study rooms and tutors.
23
Finding Good Team Members
  • One way to improve your chances of success is to find good team members to work with.  Consider the following:
  • Find a diverse group (different sexes, ethnicities, and ages) because each member will bring his/her own perspective to the team.
  • Find at least one team member who speaks a different native language than you.  This will give you the opportunity to practice speaking English together.
  • Find team members who can do things that you can’t.  This will allow you to broaden your skill set.  You can also teach them the skills that you know.
  • Find one responsible person to be the team leader (this could be you!).  That person should make sure that the team is on track to completing the assignment successfully.
  • Avoid working with your close friends.  Sometimes, working with friends can be distracting.  Instead, work with people you get along with that will stay focused on the assignment.

Part 5:  Seeking Help
25
Resources
  • American colleges expect their students to take initiative when they need help.  In other words, you are responsible for seeking out help.
  • The good news is that College of the Canyons provides a wide variety of resources to help you succeed in your classes.  Be sure to take advantage of these resources!
  • Your handout includes web links for the services discussed in this section.
26
Office Hours
  • College professors hold office hours.  Office hours are times outside of class when students can meet with their professors one-on-one.
  • Use this time to ask individual questions, seek help on an assignment, or discuss personal situations with a professor (like making arrangements to take an exam in TLC instead of the classroom).
  • Your professor may want you to make an appointment before visiting office hours.  Be sure to email or speak to him/her after class regarding appointments.
27
The Learning Center
  • The Learning Center (TLC) provides a variety of resources for students, including:
  • Tutoring in writing, math, computers and some subjects
  • Computer lab
  • Test proctoring services
  • Group study rooms
  • To learn more about TLC, watch this video.
28
Library
  • The library provides a variety of research resources to help you.  These include:
  • Wide selection of books
  • Online databases and e-books
  • Study spaces
  • Citation resources for MLA and APA style
29
ASG Computer Lab

Counseling
  • The Counseling department can help you develop your Educational Plan and prepare for your goals (career certificate, Associate’s Degree, or transfer).
  • Counseling takes place in a one-on-one session and may involve a discussion of goals, self awareness, or college success skills.

International Students Program

Disabled Students Program and Services (DSPS)

  • Sign Language Interpreter
  • Recorded Lectures

Student Health and Wellness Center
