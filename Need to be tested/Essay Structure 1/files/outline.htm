Part 1:
Overview of Essay Structure
4
Survey Question 1
  • Write your answer on page 1 of the handout.
  • How many paragraphs do essays have?
  • A.  3
  • B.  5
  • C.  7
  • D.  As many as they need
5
Survey Question 1 Explanation
  • The correct answer is D: As many as they need.
  • Essays can be written in a wide variety of formats and lengths.  While the five paragraph essay is a common form, it doesn’t work for every situation.  In most cases, it is best to plan to write as many paragraphs as you need to prove your point and meet your page length requirement.
6
What is essay structure?
  • The term “essay structure” describes the standard way that essays are organized.  The main components of an essay are:
  • 1.  Introduction
  • 2.  Body Paragraphs
  • 3.  Conclusion


  • Most academic essays will also include a title and a Works Cited or Reference page.
7
What is the purpose of using essay structure?
    • Essay structure helps us organize our ideas.
    • It provides a useful format for us to present our ideas in an academic setting.
    • The structure is easy for readers to follow.
    • It matches other communication types, like reports and emails.
8
What is the role of the writer in an academic essay?
  • Academic readers expect writers to thoroughly explain their ideas in an organized fashion.


  • When you’re writing an essay, it may help to think of yourself as a tour guide.
  • First, tell your readers what you are going to tell them.
  • Then, tell readers the information they need.
  • Finally, remind readers what you told them.
9
Will I ever need to do this after college is over?
  • After college is over, you may or may not find yourself writing academic essays.
  • What you will be doing is communicating with other people.  The fundamental essay structure of introduction, body, conclusion is a clear, well-organized way to communicate.
  • You will use intro/body/conclusion when you…
    • Write an email
    • Make a presentation
    • Write a letter of application for a job, a transfer admission, or a scholarship

Part 2:
Introductions
11
Survey Question 2
  • Write your answer on page 2 of your handout.
  • Why do essays include introductions?
  • A.  Because English teachers said so.
  • B.  To familiarize readers with the essay topic.
  • C.  To make sure essays have enough paragraphs.
  • D.  Tradition (we’ve always written essays with introductions).
12
Survey Question 2 Explanation
  • The correct answer is B:  To familiarize readers with the essay topic.
  • The introduction has a very specific purpose.  It helps readers learn the basic background of a topic before being introduced to your argument.  When writing the introduction, imagine that you’re writing to an intelligent but uninformed audience.  What do they need to know in order to understand your point?
13
Why write an introduction?
  • The introduction of an essay introduces readers to the ideas you’re going to share.  Readers need some background information from you to help them understand your topic.
  • The introduction fulfills several purposes:
    • Identifying your topic
    • Providing background information
    • Encouraging readers to be interested in your topic
    • Sharing your argument about the topic
    • Providing readers an idea of who you are as a writer


  • Note:  You don’t have to write the introduction first.  Sometimes, it works better to write the body of the essay first and then go back to the introduction.
14
Tone
  • Imagine walking into a party where you don’t know anyone.  When you walk in, do you say, “Hi, my name is X, and we should stop teaching Physical Education in elementary schools?”
  • Probably not.
  • We understand that we can’t present our argument so blatantly immediately after meeting someone.  Instead, we work up to the argument by getting to know people first.
  • The same is true in an essay.  Generally, it works well to begin an essay with a gentle tone.  This helps readers trust you more and get used to your ideas.  Your tone can grow stronger the further you progress in your paper.
15
What goes in an introduction?
  • Typically, academic essay introductions include:
  • 1.  Attention Grabber (optional)
  • 2.  Background Information
  • 3.  Thesis
  • Of course, this may change based on assignment and the subject you’re studying.  (Scientific lab reports, for example, won’t include an attention grabber.)  If you have questions about your particular assignment, talk to your instructor or to a TLC tutor.
16
Attention Grabber
  • An attention grabber, or hook, is designed to capture your reader’s attention.  You should make your readers excited about reading your work and increase their interest in your topic.
  • Attention grabbers are usually not a required part of a writing assignment.  However, they can make your (and your readers’) experience of the essay more fun!

19
Background Information
  • Use the introduction as an opportunity to educate your readers.  Give them any background information they will need to understand your ideas.
  • It may help to imagine that you’re writing to an intelligent but uninformed person—perhaps a college student who is not in class with you.  What would that person need to know to understand your topic?
20
What information might appear in the introduction?
  • Definitions of Key Concepts
  • Theoretical Foundations
  • Historical Background
  • Plot Summary (for a literature or film analysis)
  • An overall narrowing of the topic from a general perspective to a more specific one
  • Answers to the reporter’s questions, which are:  Who?, What?, When?, Where?, Why?, and How?
21
How much is too much?
  • Avoid providing too much background information because you risk losing your readers’ attention.
  • Save specific facts, statistics, and details for the body of the essay where you can discuss them in depth.
22
Thesis
  • Often, your thesis will appear at the end of the introduction.  This placement gives you a chance to gently introduce readers to your topic before sharing your argument with them.
  • The thesis is the main argument you plan to make in your paper.  It is the most important sentence or two in the essay.
  • A good thesis will:
    • Answer the question on the essay prompt
    • Be debatable
    • Be clear
    • Be specific
    • Provide a preview of the rest of the essay


    • Note:  For more information on thesis statements, consider attending the Thesis Development workshop or Guided Learning Activity.

Part 3:
Body Paragraphs
25
Survey Question 3
  • Write your answer on page 5 of your handout.
  • What is the purpose of body paragraphs?
  • A.  To explain the writer’s ideas in detail
  • B.  To be exactly eight sentences long
  • C.  To take up space
  • D.  To fill with researched information from outside sources
26
Survey Question 3 Explanation
  • The correct answer is A: To explain the writer’s ideas in detail.
  • The purpose of body paragraphs is to help readers learn about the writer’s ideas.  They should provide specific details and evidence to convince the readers.
27
What are body paragraphs?
  • Body paragraphs make up the middle of a writing project.  They explain the writer’s ideas in detail and support those ideas with specific details and evidence.


  • How many body paragraphs do you need?
  • As many as it takes to explain your ideas thoroughly and meet the page length requirement of your assignment.  Focus more on the quality of your writing than on the number of paragraphs required.
28
Body Paragraph Considerations
  • Coherence:  All the paragraphs should relate to each other and to your thesis statement.  They should stick (cohere) together.
  • Organization: Put your body paragraphs in an order that makes sense to your readers.
  • Readability: Readers often have trouble focusing on long paragraphs.  Consider breaking apart long paragraphs so readers can follow your ideas better.
29
Criteria for a Strong Body Paragraph
  • A strong body paragraph will do the following:
    • Support your thesis
    • Discuss one main idea
    • Use specific, detailed language
30
Some Types of Body Paragraphs
  • Body Paragraphs can fulfill several different functions, including:
    • Thoroughly explaining a reason that supports the writer’s thesis
    • Presenting a counterargument and/or refuting a counterargument
    • Comparing or contrasting ideas
    • Providing relevant background information
    • Explaining a step in a process
    • Analyzing a key example that supports the thesis


  • For more information, consider attending Essay Structure 2:  Making the Modes of Writing Work for You.
31
What can a body paragraph include?
  • A body paragraph may include some (or all) of the below elements.  Your paragraph may or may not need these elements depending on the type of assignment you’re working on or the subject you’re studying.  If you have questions, be sure to ask your instructor or a TLC tutor for help.


    • Topic Sentence that connects to the thesis and reflects the subject of the entire paragraph.
    • Topic sentence development:  additional details or description about the topic sentence.
    • Evidence:  quotes, statistics, examples, illustrations, etc. from reputable sources.
    • Commentary on the Evidence:  show readers how the evidence supports your argument.
    • Connections back to the thesis statement:  show readers how the entire paragraph supports your argument.
    • Transitional words or phrases:  help readers make connections between your ideas.
32
Transitional Words and Phrases
  • Transitions are words and phrases that show how two ideas are connected.  Using transitions will help readers follow your ideas better.
  • You can use transitions within or between paragraphs.  Be careful not to over-use transitions.  You don’t need one in every sentence.
  • Some common transitions include:
    • In addition
    • For example
    • Although
    • In fact
  • For more examples, follow the link on your handout.
33
A quick note about citation
  • Body paragraphs frequently use outside sources to help support their points.  Be sure to cite those sources properly using the format required by your instructor.
  • TLC offers workshops and/or handouts on a variety of citation styles.
  • We at TLC are happy to help you work on accurate citations!  Don’t underestimate the importance of citation; failure to cite properly is considered plagiarism.

Part 4:  Conclusions
36
Survey Question 4
  • Write your answer on page 7 of your handout.
  • How similar should the introduction and conclusion be?
  • A.  100% similar:  Just copy the introduction and paste it at the end of the essay.
  • B.  90% similar:  Reword some of the introduction, but it’s OK to keep many key phrases.
  • C.  20% similar:  While there may be some similarities with the introduction, the conclusion is mostly original material and tone.
  • D.  0% similar:  The conclusion and introduction have nothing to do with each other.
37
Survey Question 4 Explanation
  • The correct answer is C:  20% similar.
  • The introduction and conclusions should share some ideas—after all, they are from the same paper.  However, the tone of the conclusion is quite different.  Savvy writers use the conclusion to urge action and remind readers why the topic is important.
  • Avoid copying or rewriting the introduction.  Instead, use the conclusion to make a strong final impression on your readers.
38
What is the purpose of the conclusion?
  • The conclusion is your final word on a topic.  It gives you the opportunity to remind readers about key points in your argument AND emphasize the significance of your argument.
  • The conclusion should answer the question, “so what?”  In other words, tell readers why your topic is important.
  • Audiences remember the last thing they read best.  This means that it is very important to have a strong conclusion so you make a positive, lasting impression on the reader.
39
Tone
  • If we’ve done a good job in our essays, by the conclusion, the reader has learned about our topic and trusts our credibility.
  • This means that the conclusion can be stronger in tone than the introduction.
  • You can also use the conclusion to urge action.
40
Avoid the following
  • Too much summary
  • Repeating word-for-word from the intro
  • Phrases like “in conclusion” or “to sum up”
  • Adding new information that isn’t discussed in the body

Part 5:  Developing Academic Discretion

Survey Question 5 Explanation

  • Every writing assignment is a little bit different.  In order to meet the needs of different assignments, students should learn to alter their strategies.
46
What is Academic Discretion?
  • As a college student, you should develop your academic discretion.
  • Academic Discretion will allow you to critically think about writing assignments and determine the best strategies for approaching that assignment.
  • As you work on your assignment, think about your purpose, the assignment requirements, the message you plan to convey, and the readers’ needs.  Use these factors to help you structure and organize your work.
47
Beyond Formulas
  • You may be used to writing within specific formulas for your school assignments.  One of the most common is the Five Paragraph Essay (also known as the Jane Schaffer method).
  • These formulas may be helpful for beginning writers.  However, as you progress and your assignments grow more complex (and longer), these formulas are not as effective.
  • Eventually, you can start working outside of these formulas.
  • Work on meeting the assignment requirements rather than counting paragraphs and sentences.
48
Assignment Requirements
  • When working on your paper, review the assignment requirements frequently.  Use them to help make sure that your paper is structured effectively.
  • Examples:
    • If you are writing a 10 page paper, you should know that you need more than five paragraphs.
    • If you are writing a compare/contrast paper, you should know that you need to discuss at least two main subjects.
    • If you are writing a formal lab report for your chemistry class, you should know that your language should be clear, specific, and scientifically appropriate.
  • Your assignment will give you clues as to how to approach the writing.  If you need help interpreting those clues, talk to your instructor or a TLC tutor.

What you should know about Essay Structure now
  • The Introduction / Body / Conclusion format is useful in many communication situations.
  • Introductions should ease your readers into your topic and argument.
  • Body paragraphs should explain your ideas in detail and support them with evidence.
  • Conclusions should remind readers of important ideas and answer the question “so what?”
  • As a college student, you should develop your academic discretion to make sure you are communicating effectively.
