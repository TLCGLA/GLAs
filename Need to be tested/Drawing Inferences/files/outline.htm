What is an inference?
  • You draw an inference when you make a plausible (likely, reasonable) guess about ideas that are not directly stated in a text.
13
To draw an inference, you combine two ingredients:
  • The facts you observe in a text
  • Your previous knowledge and experience
14
In order to draw inferences,
we . . .
  • Ask questions
    • I wonder who she is?
    • Where is this taking place?
  • Make connections
    • What do I know about this topic or situation?
15
The tricky part
  • Note, however, that inferences are not facts—they are interpretations.
  • They have degrees of plausibility, and their accuracy or validity is not entirely guaranteed.
    • The animals could start playing too roughly
    • The dog could be showing submission to the cheetah
16
Make sure your inferences are justified by the facts in the text
  • Avoid inferences that contradict the facts:  “The animals are eating.”
  • Avoid inferences that stretch the facts too far:  “The animals will be best friends forever.”
  • These are not reasonable inferences!

Written Texts
  • So far, we have discussed some visual texts.  Now let’s discuss how we can make inferences when we read.
  • We often need to “read between the lines” to interpret written texts. Again, we can use clues in the text to make reasonable inferences.
23
Examples of Inferences in Written Texts
  • We make inferences about people, settings, and situations.
  • We make inferences about what will occur next.
  • We make inferences about the larger ideas or themes within a text, as well as the text’s purpose or message.
  • We make inferences about the meaning of individual words and phrases, using clues from the surrounding context.
24
Inferences about Unfamiliar Words or Phrases
  • You can use clues in the surrounding text to draw inferences about the meaning of unfamiliar words and phrases.


  • Example:
  • The house was haunted by a malevolent ghost that moved furniture, hid precious objects, and wailed angrily all night long.

Crossing the Rubicon
  • In ancient Rome, it was illegal for a Roman general to lead an army into Rome.  He was supposed to disband the army before returning home.
  • In 49 CE, Julius Caesar illegally and intentionally crossed the Rubicon (the river on the Roman border) while in command of an army.
  • This was the beginning of his civil war.
  • Today, “crossing the Rubicon” means “passing the point of no return.”

Predictions:  Making inferences about what will occur next
  • When you make a prediction, you guess what might happen, based on information in the text and from your own experience.
  • You can make predictions about actions, events, and outcomes.  (“What will happen next?  What is the result of these actions?”)
  • After you make such predictions, you can confirm, revise, or discard them as you continue reading.

Making inferences about the larger ideas, themes, or purpose of a text
  • When you read essays, articles, stories, and other college texts, your instructor often asks you to identify the author’s main idea (thesis or theme).  You might also need to identify the author’s purpose.
  • Thesis, theme, and purpose are not always directly stated in the text, so you need to infer them yourself, and read between the lines!
