AGENDA
  • This workshop will cover the six different uses of the comma:
  • To create a list
  • To join two sentences with a conjunction
  • To introduce information and quotations
  • To interrupt the flow of a sentence
  • To use with some adjectives
  • To use with dates, numbers, titles, etc.
3
MYTH BUSTING
  • You may have been told to add a comma to a sentence whenever you pause.  This technique is not the most effective.
  • Different people pause in different places when they speak out loud.  Does this mean we all have our own comma rules?
  • Of course not!
  • Instead of trying to find the places you pause, learn the correct rules for comma use.  This will help you properly place a comma every time!

USE 1:  TO CREATE A LIST
5
WHEN TO USE A COMMA IN A LIST
  • Use a comma when listing three or more things in a series.
  • Examples:
  • Maddie made a quick to-do list in her head.  She has to go to school, print out her paper, and read a chapter in the next fifteen minutes!
  • Jerry just signed his first record deal.  He’s already thinking about his future projects: touring Asia with his band, making a multimillion dollar music video, and performing a duet with Taylor Swift at the Grammys.
6
DO NOT USE A COMMA IF YOUR LIST ONLY HAS TWO ITEMS
  • Example:
  • Chris ordered his pizza with mushrooms and extra cheese.
7
OXFORD COMMA
  • Grammar enthusiasts debate whether or not we should use the Oxford Comma (the comma before “and” in a list).
  • Often, the Oxford Comma ensures that a sentence’s meaning is clear.
  • Example:
  • Without Oxford Comma:  Highlights of Cara’s global tour include meeting the Queen’s head butler, a world class cliff diver and an enthusiastic interpretive dancer.
  • With Oxford Comma:  Highlights of Cara’s global tour include meeting the Queen’s head butler, a world class cliff diver, and an enthusiastic interpretive dancer.
  • Without the Oxford comma, it isn’t clear that we’re discussing three different people.  Instead, it sounds like the butler is also a cliff diver and dancer.
8
USE 2:  TO JOIN TWO SENTENCES WITH A CONJUNCTION
9
TO USE A COMMA AS A JOINER
  • Use a comma and a coordinating conjunction to join two simple sentences.
  • Each of the sentences must have a subject and a verb.
  • Each of the sentences must make sense on its own
  • The coordinating conjunctions are:
    • For
    • And
    • Nor
    • But
    • Or
      Yet
    • So
  • You can remember them with the acronym FANBOYS
10
EXAMPLES
  • Jorge was convinced the house was haunted, but no one else ever saw the ghost.
  • Mei’s mother thought she should study abroad for a semester, but Mei was nervous about being away from home for so long.
11
THE COMMA SPLICE
  • Comma splices occur when writers use only a comma to connect two sentences.  This is a grammatical error.  You must use both a comma and a FANBOYS to connect the sentences.


  • Incorrect:  Walter was concerned about his health care, he was worried about what would happen to his family if he got sick.
  • Correct:  Walter was concerned about his health care, and he was worried about what would happen to his family if he got sick.

USE 3:  TO INTRODUCE INFORMATION AND QUOTATIONS
14
USE A COMMA FOR INTRODUCTORY CLAUSES
  • Use a comma after an introductory clause.  An introductory clause is a dependent clause that provide background information for the rest of the sentence.
  • Introductory clauses start with adverbs like: after, although, as, because, before, if, since, though, until, when, etc.
  • Examples:
  • Before going to The Learning Center, you must get a student ID card.
  • If you are a vegetarian, you can make the sauce without meat.
  • Note:  If the dependent clause comes at the end of the sentence, you don’t need a comma.
  • Example:  You must getting a student ID card before going to The Learning Center.
15
USE A COMMA FOR INTRODUCTORY PHRASES
  • Use a comma after an introductory phrase.  An introductory phrase is not a complete clause, but still provides background information for the rest of the sentence.
  • Introductory phrases do not have a subject or verb that are different from the main sentence.


  • Examples:
  • To prepare for a speech, it is important to practice in front of friends or family.
  • A curious child who loves staring at the stars, Mario dreams of becoming an astronaut when he grows up.
16
USE A COMMA FOR INTRODUCTORY WORDS
  • Sometimes, we use single words to help make the transition from one sentence to the next.  Use a comma after those words
  • Introductory words include:  however, still, furthermore, and meanwhile
  • Examples:
  • Meanwhile, the police were already tracking the bank robber’s getaway vehicle.
  • Furthermore, changing to native, drought-tolerant landscaping may attract birds and butterflies to your garden.
17
USE A COMMA TO INTRODUCE QUOTATIONS
  • Use a comma between a signal phrase and a quote
  • Examples:
  • A famous ad campaign that began in the 1990’s asked, “Got milk?”
  • “Brevity is the soul of wit,” declared Polonius.


  • Don’t use a comma if the quotation is introduced with “that.”
  • Example:  The driver the officer pulled over claimed that “the speed limit wasn’t posted anywhere.”


  • Don’t use a comma if the quote ends with a question mark or explanation point.
  • Example:  “We’re getting married!” Bethany squealed with glee.
18
USE 4:  TO INTERRUPT THE FLOW OF THE SENTENCE
19
A MID-SENTENCE INTERRUPTION
  • Use TWO commas with words and phrases that interrupt the flow of the sentence or provide additional information.


  • Examples:
  • Gilroy, located in central California, holds a garlic festival every year.
  • Lorena, a strong writer, gets nervous when she has to read her work out loud.
20
AN INTERRUPTER AT THE END OF A SENTENCE
  • When the interruption comes at the end of the sentence, use only one comma.


  • Examples:
  • Roman couldn’t wait to visit Sarah, his best friend from elementary school.
  • Leila visited the zoo to see the California condors, an endangered species.
21
WHEN NOT TO USE A COMMA
  • Don’t use commas if the sentence loses important meaning when you take out the interrupter.
  • Incorrect:  The drunk driver, who hit me, should lose his license.
  • Correct:  The drunk driver who hit me should lose his license.


  • Don’t use a comma to separate the subject from its verb.
  • Incorrect:  The girl who always wears blue, has chicken pox and will be absent all week.
  • Correct:  The girl who always wears blue has chicken pox and will be absent all week.

USE 5:  TO USE WITH SOME ADJECTIVES
24
WHEN ADJECTIVES CAN SWITCH
  • If you can switch the order of two adjectives without changing the meaning, you need to use a comma.


  • Examples:
  • The brown recluse spider is a terrifying, deadly arachnid.
  • The brown recluse spider is a deadly, terrifying arachnid.
25
WHEN ADJECTIVES CAN’T SWITCH
  • If you cannot switch the order of the adjectives, you do not need a comma.


  • Incorrect:  The hot, pink legwarmers were the perfect final touch for Shelly’s ‘80’s party costume.
  • Correct:  The hot pink legwarmers were the perfect final touch for Shelly’s ‘80’s party costume.
26
USE 6:  TO USE WITH DATES, NUMBERS, TITLES, ETC.
27
IN DATES
  • Use a comma between the day of the month and the year.


  • Example:
  • February 14, 2010
28
IN NUMBERS
  • Use a comma between every three digits before the decimal point.
  • Example:  I’d like to sell my car for $10,403.


  • Note:  You don’t have to use a comma for years.
  • Example:  I bought the car in 2009.
29
IN PLACE NAMES
  • Use a comma between the city and state
  • Example:  College of the Canyons is located in Valencia, CA.


  • Use a comma between the city and country
  • Example:  Iris plans to study in Rome, Italy this summer.
30
IN TITLES
  • Use a comma between a person’s name and their title (if the title appears at the end of his/her name).


  • Examples:
  • My favorite TV doctor is Doogie Howser, MD.
  • I look forward to the day I can sign my name Cameron Jones, PhD.
