Preview Activity Discussion
  • There are many different ways a teacher can be good.  In addition, students’ own opinions about teaching, teachers, and education will affect their answers.
  • For example, one student may say a good teacher is one who explains things clearly while another student may say a good teacher is one who can relate the material to real life.
  • When we write sentences like “I had a good teacher,” we are leaving the readers to make their own meaning.  They may interpret the sentence differently than we did.
  • Instead, we will work on adding detail to our sentences to make sure our readers understand exactly what we mean.

Part 1: Writing Clearer, More Specific Sentences
7
Section Overview
  • In this section, we will examine ways to add detail to individual sentences.  This additional detail will help you express your ideas more clearly to your readers.
  • We’ll go over particular strategies for writing more specific sentences, look at examples, and give you some practice revising sentences.
8
Avoid Vague Language
  • Words like “good” and “bad” can mean so many things that they have lost their meaning.  They don’t communicate the writer’s ideas clearly to readers.
  • Here are some examples of other vague words:
    • Things / stuff
    • “To be” verbs (was / is / are / am / etc.)
    • Very / really
    • Got
    • Went
    • Important
    • Big
    • Small
9
Let’s look at a sample
  • Sample Sentence:  Eating breakfast is good for you.


  • Discussion:  The sentence doesn’t make the meaning of “good” completely clear.  Does “good” mean healthy?  Morally good?  Effective?


  • Revision:  Eating breakfast is a healthy choice because it provides important nutrients for the rest of the day.
10
Your turn
  • Read the following sentence:
  • Education is a big issue in the world today.
  • In box 2 of your worksheet, rewrite the sentence to remove the vague word “big.”  Consider ways to make the sentence clearer and more specific.
  • Note:  Don’t replace the word “big” with “important”—that’s another vague word.
11
Use Specific Language
  • Use details to let readers know exactly who or what you are talking about.  For example, instead of saying “class,” you can say, “English 89 at 9:30 a.m.”  This lets your readers know which class you’re discussing.
12
Let’s look at a sample
  • Sample Sentence:  You have to turn in your assignment soon.
  • Discussion:  There are two places where this sentence can be more specific.  First, let readers know which assignment you’re referring to.  Second, let readers know what you mean by “soon.”  When exactly is the assignment due?


  • Revision:  You have to turn in the final draft of your second essay by 5:00 p.m. tomorrow.
13
Your turn
  • Read the following sentence:
  • The doctor can help with it.
  • In box 3 of your worksheet, rewrite the sentence to make it more specific.  Focus your attention on the words “doctor” and “it.”
14
Describe Your Topic
  • Help your readers understand your topic better by describing it thoroughly.  If you are writing about a problem on your high school campus, for example, describe the campus to your readers.
  • In college-level writing, readers rely on the writer to provide all the details they need to understand a topic thoroughly.  You can’t assume that your reader knows what you know (even if you covered the material in class together).  Instead, show the reader how well you understand the subject by describing it thoroughly.
15
Let’s look at a sample
  • You’re writing a paper about overcoming obstacles to education.  You’ve decided to write about procrastination.
  • Sample description:  Procrastination occurs when people knowingly put off tasks for later that they should be completing now.  One key characteristic of procrastination is a feeling of guilt; the procrastinator understands that his/her time would be better spent doing something else.  Procrastinators may be putting off tasks for a number of reasons, including a fear of failure or disliking the tasks or the topic.
  • Discussion:  In this example, the writer explained exactly what procrastination is.  The writer ensures that readers understand the definition of procrastination.  Readers can better understand the point the writer will make in the rest of the paper.
16
Your turn

17
Use your senses
  • Tell your readers how your subject looks, smells, sounds, tastes, and feels.  Help the readers experience that subject for themselves.
  • Carefully select which senses to use for which topic.  If you are writing about garbage, for example, you may not want to tell readers how the subject smells or tastes.
  • Sensory descriptions can capture the readers’ attention and make them more interested in your topic.  There are a few really great places to use sensory descriptions, like the introduction of an essay and in explaining examples.  (Of course, you can use sensory descriptions elsewhere, too!)
18
Let’s look at a sample
  • Sample Sentence:  The man baked cookies.
  • Discussion:  The sentence provides basic information, but it leaves out a lot of details.  What does the man look like?  What kind of cookies is he baking?
  • Revision:  The tall, handsome man baked buttery chocolate chip cookies.
19
Your turn
  • Read the following sentence.
  • The dog sat next to me.
  • In box 5 on your handout, rewrite the sentence to add sensory details.  Remember, your senses tell you how something looks, smells, sounds, tastes, and feels.

Show and Tell
  • When we tell someone information, we give them the information directly without giving them the opportunity to interpret it.
  • Example:  The professor was upset.


  • When we show our information, we provide details and let the readers interpret them for themselves.
  • Example:  The professor raised his voice and slammed his books on the table.


  • We can combine both options, which gives readers a thorough picture of the information the we want to present.
  • Example:  The professor raised his voice and slammed his books on the table; in short, he was upset.
21
Let’s look at another example
  • Tell:  The girl is a future scientist.
  • Show:  The girl loves observing birds and insects, looking at the stars, and playing with magnets.
  • Both:  The girl is a future scientist; she loves observing birds and insects, looking at the stars, and playing with magnets.
22
Your turn
  • Read the following “tell” sentence.
  • The artist worked hard.
  • In box 6 on your handout, write a “show” sentence.  Then, combine the two sentences so they show AND tell.

Part 2:  Writing about Visuals
24
Section Overview
  • For some assignments, instructors will ask you to write about visual media (like pictures, videos, ads, etc.).  Your reader may or may not have access to the original visual.
  • As a writer, it is your responsibility to help the reader imagine what the visual media looks like for him/herself.  You can achieve this goal by using details and being organized.
25
Details (Part 1)
  • You should include a great deal of detail in your description.  Assume that the readers have never seen the visual before.  Then, ask yourself, how can you help them imagine the visual?
  • Use some of the following elements to help you:
    • Foreground and Background (What is in the front of the image?  What is in the back?)
    • Size and Proportion (How big are objects?  How big are they in comparison to each other?)
    • Camera Angle (Which way is the camera looking?)
26
Let’s look at a sample
  • What details do we see in the picture?
    • What is in the foreground?  What is in the background?
    • How big are the objects in the picture?  How big are they in relation to one another?
    • Which way is the camera looking?

27
Details (Part 2)
  • Here are some other details we can consider in our description:
    • Color (What colors are used in the image?  What is the significance of the colors?)
    • Pose (What are the model(s) doing with their bodies?)
    • Relationship between Objects (How are the different objects in the image connected?)
    • Movement (Which way are the objects moving?)
28
Let’s look at a sample
  • What details do we see in the picture?
    • What colors are in the picture?
    • What is the model doing with his body?
    • What is the relationship between the objects in the picture?
    • What kind(s) of movement is occurring in the picture?

Organization
  • After identifying the different details you want to present, develop a system for organizing those details.  If you give your readers details without organizing them, the reader will have trouble imagining the picture.


  • Consider some of the following organizational strategies:
    • Vertical:  Start at the top of the visual and work your way down
    • Horizontal:  Start at the left of the visual and work your way right
    • General to specific:  Give an overview of the image, then provide specific details

Part 3:  Elaborating on Ideas
33
Section Overview
  • This section will help you elaborate on your ideas to make sure you’ve explained them fully.  This will help readers understand the ideas better.  Also, ideas that are elaborated are more interesting to readers, so you’ll capture their attention.
  • Bonus:  well-elaborated ideas take up more space in your paper with quality material.  You’ll be able to meet page length requirements better!
34
Provide Examples to Support Ideas
  • Sometimes, student writers present their ideas in general terms.  They may move onto another idea without fully explaining the first.  When they do this, they miss an opportunity to engage readers and ensure that they understand the idea.
  • One way to solve this problem is to include a detailed example.  Select an example that illustrates the idea you’re writing about.  Then, share that example with readers as a brief story.  Have a beginning, middle, and end.  Be sure to explain how the example illustrates the idea.
35
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Parents can encourage their children by praising their hard work.
  • Example:  My child has been taking ballet lessons for six months now.  A few weeks ago, he performed in his first recital on stage.  He was so worried that he would forget the steps, so he made a commitment to practice at home every day for half an hour.  When he performed in the recital, he remembered every single step.  Afterwards, I complimented him on the dedication he showed by practicing so hard.  He’s already excited to prepare for his next recital.  By praising him for his effort, I’ve helped encourage him to continue working hard in the future.
36
Provide Facts to Support Ideas
  • Another way to make your idea more concrete and understandable is to provide facts.  These facts show the readers that they can believe your ideas.
  • Facts can also help readers understand your idea.  They can grasp the specific details of the facts, which will help them follow the ideas better.
  • By including outside facts, you build your credibility.  You show readers that you aren’t just making stuff up—you have evidence.
  • As with the examples we discussed earlier, be sure to interpret your facts.  Show readers how they support your idea.
37
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Low income parents face challenges in getting involved at their child’s school.
  • Fact:  They often cannot afford to take time off work in order to attend events like Back to School night.
  • Interpretation:  This means that they have fewer opportunities to discuss their child’s progress with his/her teacher.

Ask the question, “how?”
  • After making a statement in your essay, ask the question, “how?”
  • This will encourage you to provide information about methods.  You will have to explain in what manner an event occurred or by what means an event occurred.
  • You can also use the question “how?” to show causes from the past and changes in the future.
40
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Failure can be beneficial.
  • “How” Questions:
    • How can failure benefit people?
    • How do people fail?
    • How do people learn from their failures?
    • How can they avoid failing in the future?


    • By answering one or more of these questions, you can enhance your discussion of the idea that failure is beneficial.  Readers will have a better idea of what the idea means.
41
Ask the question, “why?”
  • After making a statement in your essay, ask the question, “why?”
  • The question “why” encourages us to find the reasons for our idea.  It also helps us find the purpose or the causes for our idea.
42
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Many of today’s employers are looking for workers who can think creatively.
  • “Why” Questions:
    • Why are employers looking for creative thinkers?
    • Why are creative thinkers valuable to employers?
    • Why do employers prefer creative thinkers over more conventional thinkers?
  • By answering one or more of these questions, you provide important information to your readers.  They will be able to understand the roots of your ideas better.
43
Your Turn

  • Four year universities are looking for students who are involved on campus.
  • In box 10 on your handout, brainstorm at least three “how” or “why” questions you could ask about the idea.
44
Ask the question, “so what?”
  • After making a statement in your paper, consider the question, “so what?”  This question asks you to consider the significance of your topic.  In other words, why should readers consider the topic important?
  • By answering the question, “so what?” you can engage your readers and (hopefully) encourage them to adopt your ideas.
  • The question “so what?” is an extremely valuable tool to help you succeed in your college classes.  Keep asking “so what?” to push yourself further.  You’ll come up with more complex, interesting ideas.
45
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Many of today’s employers are looking for workers who can think creatively.
  • “So What” Questions:
    • Why is it important that employers want creative thinkers?
    • How can I use this information as I finish my education?
    • How can I use this information as I conduct a job search?
    • Why is creative thinking so important?
  • Note:  You might find yourself asking a chain of “so what?” questions to help you dig deeper and deeper into a topic.
46
Look for Counterarguments
  • After making a statement in your paper, ask yourself, “why not?”  In other words, think about the reasons that people might disagree with you.
  • Consider your readers’ possible counterarguments carefully.  Then, you can reply to their counterarguments.  How would you respond to people who disagree with you?
47
Let’s look at a sample
  • Idea:  Many of today’s employers are looking for workers who can think creatively.
  • Counterarguments:
    • Not all industries/jobs need creative thinkers
    • Some employers prefer other skills (like critical thinking)
  • Respond to Counterarguments:
    • How will you respond to the idea that not all industries or jobs need creative thinkers?
    • How will you respond to the idea that some employers prefer other skills?
