PART 1:  DEFINING STRESS
5
What is Stress?
  • Stress is the body’s reaction to the different challenges we face in life.
  • It developed through evolution as a way to protect our ancestors from predators and other threats.
  • Some stress is beneficial; it helps us get things done.
  • Too much stress can be damaging to our health.  According to the American Psychological Association, “sudden emotional stresses…can trigger heart attacks, arrhythmias, and even sudden death.”
  • When stress becomes chronic (interfering with your life for an extended period of time), its effects can be even more damaging.
6
Stress Facts
  • In 2014, the American Psychological Association (APA) conducted a survey called “Stress in America:  Paying with Our Health.”  They found that:
  • On average, Americans rate their stress at 4.9 on a 10 point scale (1=little to no stress and 10=a great deal of stress)
  • 42% of adults say they are not doing enough—or are not sure whether they are doing enough—to manage their stress
  • Common sources of stress are money (64%), work (60%), the economy (49%), family responsibilities (47%), and personal health concerns (46%).
7
Function of Stress
  • Stress is meant to prime our “fight or flight” instincts and keep us alive.
  • This was a great benefit to our distant ancestors whose stressors were immediate threats that could kill them (like attack by a predator)
  • Today, our stressors are different.  For example, we worry about upcoming challenges.  This means that stress hormones persist in our bodies over a longer period of time.
8
Eustress
  • “Eustress” is good stress; it motivates you to continue working hard.
  • Eustress keeps people happy, motivated, challenged, and productive.
  • We need eustress; it is good for us.
9
Distress
  • “Distress” is bad stress; it occurs when the eustress becomes too much to bear.
  • We know we are experiencing distress when our tension builds, there is no longer any fun in the challenges we face, and there seems to be no relief from the stress.
  • Distress can lead to overeating, loss of appetite, drinking, smoking, and other negative coping mechanisms.
10
Effects of Chronic Stress
  • Chronic, persistent stress damages almost every body system we have, including:  digestion, reproduction, growth, tissue repair, and immunity.
  • It can lead to mental health problems like depression and anxiety.
  • It can also lead to cardiovascular disease, including heart disease, high blood pressure, heart attacks, and stroke.
  • The National Institute for Mental Health estimates that 70-80% of doctor visits are for stress-related illnesses.
  • Stress has a negative impact on memory, making it more difficult to prepare for exams.
  • Stress also hinders other mental skills like creativity, concentration, and attention to details.

PART 2:  COMMON SIGNS OF STRESS
12
Pre-Reflection #1
  • On page 3 of your handout, answer the following question:
  • How do you know when you are feeling stress?  What signs does your body give you?
13
Physical Symptoms
  • Your body changes when under stress.  You may notice the following physical symptoms:
  • Increased heart rate or blood pressure
  • Headaches and migraines
  • Constant feelings of tiredness
  • Nervousness and shaking
  • Clenched jaw and grinding teeth
  • Tight muscles and joint pain
  • Lowered immune system:  frequent colds, sinus infections, and flu symptoms
  • Upset stomach, indigestion, heartburn, and ulcers
14
Psychological / Cognitive Symptoms
  • You may also experience psychological / cognitive symptoms, such as:
  • Difficulty in ability to think clearly:  confusion, trouble concentrating, inability to remember things, and poor problem solving
  • Constant worrying
  • Racing thoughts
  • Forgetfulness or disorganization
  • Being pessimistic (only seeing the negative side)
  • Insomnia or difficulty sleeping
15
Emotional Symptoms
  • You may experience emotional symptoms like:
  • Fear, anxiety, depression, irritability, anger, and frustration
  • Becoming easily agitated, frustrated, and moody
  • Feeling overwhelmed
  • Having difficulty relaxing
  • Feeling bad about yourself
  • Avoiding others
  • Taking out stress on others (lashing out or angry outbursts)

19
What is Test Anxiety?
  • Test anxiety is the adrenaline rush of nerves we feel before a test.
  • Some test anxiety, like some stress, is beneficial because it keeps us alert and focused.
  • Too much test anxiety can work against students, causing feelings of intense pressure, fainting spells, and stomach pains.  Test anxiety can prevent students from recalling information they studied.
20
Symptoms of Test Anxiety
  • Cognitive:  Nonproductive, negative thoughts run through your head before, during, and after a test.
  • Emotional:  Negative feelings related to the exam (disappointment, frustration, etc.).
  • Behavioral:  Observable indications of stress (fidgeting, drumming your fingers, walking quickly, etc.).
  • Physiological:  Your body’s counterproductive reactions (dry mouth, butterflies in the stomach, tension headache, etc.).
21
Math Anxiety
  • Math anxiety is a learned fear that prevents otherwise intelligent people from performing well on math exams.
  • Math anxiety uses up our working memory, which is our mental scratch paper that we use to hold numbers in our heads as we work through math problems.
  • So, the more anxiety you feel, the less attention you can pay to math problems.
  • To overcome math anxiety, practice completing problems under pressure.  For example, you can use a timer.
  • For more info, consider taking our Math Anxiety workshop or GLA.
22
Addressing Test Anxiety
  • Here are some strategies to help you address test anxiety:
  • Cognitive:  Don’t catastrophize!  Stop yourself from engaging in negative self-talk.
  • Emotional:  Leave your baggage at the door.  During a test, leave behind any personal problems you may be facing in order to focus on the exam.
  • Behavioral:  Go outside and engage in physical activity.
  • Physiological:  Take deep, relaxing breaths.

PART 4: COPING WITH STRESS
24
Pre-Reflection #2
  • On page 6 of your handout, answer the following questions:
  • Make a short list of techniques people use to cope with stress.  Which techniques are healthy?  Which are unhealthy?
25
Self-sabotage
  • Overall, unhealthy stress reduction techniques have us engage in self-sabotage.  In other words, we do not act in our own best interest.  In fact, they may cause more problems in the long run.
  • These techniques include:
  • Drinking alcohol excessively
  • Watching TV / using the computer unproductively for long periods of time
  • Working obsessively
  • Fighting
  • Abusing drugs
  • Unhealthy eating
26
Healthy Techniques
  • Rather than sabotaging ourselves, we must find healthy options for coping with our stress.
  • The following slides provide some suggestions of healthy techniques for coping with stress.
27
If you feel overwhelmed, try:
  • Separate from external stressor.  For example, if your neighbors are loud, find a quiet place to study at school.
  • Manage your time well.  Focus your attention on high-priority activities and eliminate time wasting activities.
  • Put your problems into context.  Ask yourself, “how much will this problem matter in one year?”
  • Take a mental vacation.  Picture a beautiful, relaxing place and spend a few minutes thinking about that place.
28
If you feel angry, try:
  • Writing in a journal to help you honestly express your opinions.
  • Channeling your anger into positive actions.  For example, volunteer with an organization that addresses your concerns.
  • Shifting your attention to pleasant topics and away from the issue that caused the anger.
29
If you feel anxious, try:
  • Preparing thoroughly for upcoming challenges.  For example, if you are anxious about a test, be sure to study carefully for that test.
  • Breathe deeply.  Fear causes the body to constrict; deep breaths will help loosen the body.
  • Imagine yourself succeeding at your goals.
  • Reframe your thoughts.  Ask yourself, “if the worse happens, can I live with it?”
30
If you feel sad, try:
  • Doing any small task that moves you towards achieving your goals.
  • Socialize with friends and loved ones.  We often associate sadness with isolation; seeing people you care about can help re-energize you.
  • Focus on the positive by identifying your successes and the different things you’re thankful for.
31
Overall, focus on positive coping mechanisms
  • When working your way through a stressful situation or a stressful time in your life, be sure to focus on healthy stress relief techniques.  These will help you overcome your challenges successfully.

PART 5:  STRESS PREVENTION
34
Stress Prevention
  • In the previous section, we looked at ways students can cope with stress once they find themselves in a stressful situation.
  • In this section, we will look at ways students can prevent stress from occurring in the first place (or at least keep stress at manageable levels).
35
Pre-Reflection #3
  • On page 8 of your handout, answer the following questions:
  • What positive skills can students develop to prevent stress from becoming overwhelming?
36
Strategy #1:  Manage Time Well
  • Often, we find ourselves under stress because we have too much to do and not enough time to do it.
  • In order to combat this feeling, we should manage time well by:
    • Entering important deadlines on a calendar
    • Breaking large assignments and projects into manageable pieces
    • Prioritizing tasks:  completing the most important tasks first
    • Avoiding procrastination
  • For more information, take our Time Management GLA!
37
Strategy #2:  Learn to Say No
  • It can be difficult to say no to people or opportunities that seem fun and exciting.
  • But, taking on too much can increase our stress.
  • When you are offered a new opportunity, think about your goals.  Will this opportunity help you meet your goals?  If not, you may have to say no to that opportunity.
  • When saying no, offer an alternative or an explanation (as in, “I can’t take on a new project at this time.  Can we work on it in the future?”).
38
Strategy #3:  Manage Your Finances
  • Money is one of the top stressors for adults.  In fact, according to the APA’s “Stress in America” report, “Seventy-two percent of adults report feeling stressed about money at least some of the time.”
  • To avoid this stress, work on managing your money well by:
    • Creating a budget
    • Distinguishing between wants and needs
    • Tracking your expenses
    • Paying bills on time
    • Applying for scholarships to avoid student loan debt
39
Strategy #4:  Exercise
  • Exercise releases hormones like endorphins which block pain and create a feeling of euphoria.  In other words, exercise can help you blow off steam!
  • As little as 20 minutes of exercise a day can decrease stress levels.
  • Find a type of exercise you enjoy, like yoga, hiking, dancing, or playing team sports.
  • Exercise can also help you sleep better at night.
  • Note:  Check with your doctor before altering your exercise routine.
40
Strategy #5:  Sleep
  • Lack of sleep can lead us to be irritable and cranky, reducing our ability to deal with the day’s stressors.
  • Most adults need 7-9 hours of sleep per night.  Be sure to get adequate sleep so you are well-rested for the day ahead.
  • Spend time winding down before going to bed.  Don’t work late into the night or use the phone before bed.  Instead, try reading or listening to music.
  • Try to maintain a regular sleep pattern (going to bed and waking up around the same time of day everyday).
  • Avoid large meals before bed.
41
Practice Stress Reduction Strategies Regularly
  • By practicing stress reduction strategies, you will feel more calm and in control.
  • Remember, these strategies are:
    • Manage time well
    • Learn to say no
    • Manage your finances
    • Exercise
    • Sleep

Resource Overview
  • College of the Canyons cares about your success and well-being!  We provide the following resources to help you manage your stress effectively:


  • Student Health and Wellness Center
  • Counseling Department
  • The Learning Center
  • Body Mind Wellness Initiative
  • Kinesiology Department
  • Gardens of the Canyons
44
Student Health and Wellness Center
  • The Student Health and Wellness Center provides free or low cost, confidential services for students.
  • The staff includes licensed clinical psychologists for individual, couples, or group mental health counseling.  Counseling visits are free!  Each student is allowed 6 visits per semester.
  • The Center is also an LGBTQ Safe Zone.
45
Counseling Department
  • The Counseling Department provides educational, career, and personal counseling and program advisement.
  • It aims to empower students by helping them establish goals, evaluate options, develop an educational plan, and learn to study effectively.
  • Students can meet with counseling faculty privately to discuss personal and academic goals and challenges.
46
The Learning Center
  • The Learning Center provides one-on-one peer tutoring to help you in your classes.
  • TLC also provides workshops and Guided Learning Activities to help you improve your reading, writing, math, and study skills.
  • TLC has a variety of study spaces and resources where you can work alone or in a group.
47
Body Mind Wellness Initiative
  • The Body Mind Wellness Initiative promotes wellness-related events, groups, and resources.
  • These events are usually available on campus, for free.
  • Events include the BMW Resource Fair.
    • The Fall Fair focuses on challenges to Body Mind Wellness
    • The Spring Fair focuses on healthy lifestyle behaviors
  • The BMW Initiative also connects students to clubs and support groups like LETS, an organization dedicated to erasing the stigma associated with mental illness.
48
Kinesiology Department
  • The Kinesiology Department provides one unit physical education classes.
  • These classes give students the opportunity to exercise.
  • Class titles include:  Physical Fitness Lab, Weight Training, Stretch for Flexibility and Relaxation, and Basketball.
  • Be sure to consult with your doctor before changing your exercise routine.
49
Gardens of the Canyons
  • Recent studies have shown that being outside can help reduce stress.
  • In fact, a 2010 study in Environmental Science & Technology showed that we only need five minutes outside to feel a mood-boosting effect (qtd. in Main).
  • College of the Canyons has several on-campus gardens to give you a beautiful space to walk or relax outdoors.
  • See the website (link below) for a map of gardens at both the Valencia and Canyon Country campuses.
