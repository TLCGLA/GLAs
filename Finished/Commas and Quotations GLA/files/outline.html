The Dreaded Comma Splice
Never use a comma ALONE to connect two
sentences. Always use a fanboys conjunction.
  •     CORRECT:
  • Studio executives wanted Chewbacca to wear shorts, but George Lucas vetoed the idea.
  • WRONG:
  • Studio executives wanted Chewbacca to wear shorts, George Lucas vetoed the idea.

Comma Use #2: An Introducer
  • Use a comma after introductory phrases and clauses.
  • From 2000 to 2002, the Lakers won three
  • titles consecutively.


  • The comma separates the introductory phrase from the subject and the verb, making the sentence easier to read.
23
"Frankly"
  • Frankly, Keith could live without
  • food before he could live without
  • basketball.


  • Susan loves the Lakers. However,
  • she rarely has time to watch their
  • games.


  • Because Bill was a huge
  • Lakers fan, he never missed  a
  • game.
24
"Note that when the dependent..."
  • Note that when the dependent clause comes second, a comma is unnecessary.
  • Because Bill was a huge Lakers fan, he never missed a game.
  • Bill never missed a Lakers game because he was huge fan.


  • Some words that begin dependent clauses are if, before, although, when, whenever, because, before, and unless.

Comma Use #3: A Lister
  • Use a comma when listing 3 or more things in a series.
  • The Lakers won three titles consecutively
  • with the help of Shaquille O'Neal, Kobe Bryant,
  • and Hall of Fame coach Phil Jackson.
30
Use a comma when listing 3 or more things in a series.
  • Susan, Tiffany, and Amber got together to watch the
  • game on TV. They snacked on nachos, pizza, and
  • cut up carrots.
31
Do NOT use commas when you only list two things!
  • Susan and Amber snacked on nachos and salsa.


  • The Lakers won three titles consecutively
  • with the help of Shaquille O'Neal and Kobe Bryant.

"Comma Use #4: An..."
  • Comma Use #4: An Interrupter


  • Use TWO commas with words or phrases
  • that interrupt the flow of the sentence or add
  • additional information.


  • Persimmons,
  • a delicious fruit,
  • are available in
  • the fall.
37
"The Staples Center"

  • The Staples Center, which is located in downtown
  • Los Angeles, is current home to the Lakers.



  • My friend gave me a DVD of Toy Story, one of my
  • favorite movies, to cheer me up.
38
"Note"



  • Note: Sometimes the interrupter comes at the
  • end of the sentence.


  • The Lakers current home is the Staples Center,
  • which is located in downtown Los Angeles.
39
"Exception"

  • Exception: Dont use commas if the sentence
  • loses important meaning when you take out
  • the interrupter.


  • The man who grabbed my wallet lived in my
  • neighborhood.


  • The people who did the damage should pay for it.

Comma Use #5: With Adjectives
  • If you can switch the order of two adjectives without changing the meaning, you need to use a comma.
  • The comma butterfly is a magical, fascinating insect.
  • The comma butterfly is a fascinating, magical insect.
49
But if you cannot switch the adjectives,
you do NOT need a comma.

  • Ten red balloons fell from the ceiling.
50
If you can switch the adjectives, use a comma.
If you CANNOT switch them, no comma.

Other ways to use the comma
  • In dates: October 16, 1955
  • In place names: Memphis, Tennessee
  • 1234 Ivy Lane, Memphis, TN
  • In titles: Amy Jones, MD, is a good
  • doctor.
  • In numbers: Id love to win $1,000,000.

Quotation Marks and Commas
  •  If you quote someone and use a signal phrase
  • (such as he says, she claims, according to
  • Lincoln), put a comma after the signal phrase.


  • Yogi Berra, the famous New York Yankees
  • player, had a colorful way of expressing
  • himself. He once said, Always go to other
  • peoples funerals, or else they wont go to yours.
55
Quotation Marks and Commas
If the signal phrase comes last, put the comma after the quote, BEFORE the quotation mark.
  •  I'm not going to buy
  • my kids an
  • encyclopedia,
  • Berra said. Let them
  • walk to school like I did.
56
Quotation Marks and Commas
Dont use a comma if the quote ends with a question mark or exclamation point.
  • How can you think
  • and bat at the same
  • time? asked Berra.
57
Quotation Marks and Commas
Dont use a comma if the quotation is introduced
with that.
  • Yogi Berra once claimed that a nickel aint worth
  • a dime anymore.
