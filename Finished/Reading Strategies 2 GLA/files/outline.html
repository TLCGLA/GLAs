What is active reading?
  • Active reading occurs when we read with purpose.  In other words, our goal is to learn the material (not just finish the pages) and connect that material to our needs.


  • We know that were reading actively when we are:
  • Interacting with the reading (previewing the material, taking notes, predicting, etc.)
  • Critically thinking about the authors ideas
  • Considering our own ideas as we read
4
Why should students read actively?
  • When students read actively, they
  • Are better prepared for college-level work
  • Retain information longer
  • Find it easier to use/review the information
  • Use their time well and avoid the reading coma

Strategy 1:  What it Says / What it Means / What it Does
6
What it Says / What it Means / What it Does
  • This strategy asks you to approach a reading from three different angles.  When reading, try to figure out:
  • What it Says:  Summarize the reading
  • What it Means:  Analyze the reading
  • What it Does:  Explain the function of different parts of a reading
7
What it Says
  • After each paragraph, determine what a reading says.  In other words, you should be able to explain what the paragraph is about to someone who hasnt read it.
  • This step will improve your comprehension of the reading.
8
Sample:  What it Says
  • College athletics use too much of a schools budget.  Critics of college athletic programs believe that the quality of the colleges academic programs have suffered to help pay for the athletic programs.
  • Summary of the Text
  • College athletics programs have become more harmful than beneficial to colleges and universities, critics say. The increasing diversion of resources and attention to such programs, they contend, has come at the expense of the academic mission of most schools, which have gradually lowered admissions standards and tolerated poor academic achievement for the sake of successful athletics programs.
  • Source:  Do College Athletics Play a Valuable Role in Higher Education?
  • Original Text
9
What it Means
  • After summarizing the paragraph (figuring out what it says), determine what the paragraph means.  In other words, you should analyze the text.
  • To figure out what it means, try the following:
  • Suspend judgment.  Dont immediately react to the writers ideas.  Instead, work on comprehending those ideas first.
  • Divide the text into parts and determine how those parts are related.  To do this well, you must pay attention to detail.
  • Determine the writers values, assumptions, and unstated ideas.  What does the writer think or imply without stating it directly?
  • Look for patterns, such as the repetition of ideas, contrasting ideas, and anomalies (ideas that dont seem to fit).
  • Experiment.  You will have to ask yourself questions and try out different ideas as you work on your analysis.  Your analysis will evolve as you continue to work on the text.
10
Sample:  What it Means
  • Athletics and academics are set up as enemies
  • Writer privileges academic performance over athletics
  • Assumes that money should be spent on the academic mission of the school
  • Assumes that spending money on the academic mission will ensure that high academic standards will be achieved
  • Overall, the writer of the passage assumes that academics and athletics cannot exist in harmony; to spend money on one is to take away money from the other.
  • Analysis of the Text
  • College athletics programs have become more harmful than beneficial to colleges and universities, critics say. The increasing diversion of resources and attention to such programs, they contend, has come at the expense of the academic mission of most schools, which have gradually lowered admissions standards and tolerated poor academic achievement for the sake of successful athletics programs.
  • Source:  Do College Athletics Play a Valuable Role in Higher Education?
  • Original Text
11
What it Does
  • After analyzing the paragraph (figuring out what it means), determine what the paragraphs role in the entire reading is.  Why might the writer have included this paragraph?  How does it contribute to his/her argument?
  • To figure out what it does, try the following:
  • Think of the paragraph as part of a whole text.  How does it relate to the rest of the reading?
  • Think about structure.  Why is the paragraph placed where it is in the whole reading?
  • Examine the writers strategies.  How does s/he make an argument?
  • Evaluate the quality of the argument.  Is it logically sound?
12
Sample:  What it Does
  • This paragraph is at the beginning of a section in the article called Critics Say Athletics Conflicts with Academic Goals
  • Introduces the arguments that critics of athletics programs make
  • Cause/Effect argument:  Spending less money on academic leads the school to admitting less qualified students and lower academic achievement standards.
  • This paragraph doesnt include evidence, but it is at the beginning of a section.  If the writer doesnt include evidence soon, I will be able to question the validity of his/her argument.
  • Function of the Text
  • College athletics programs have become more harmful than beneficial to colleges and universities, critics say. The increasing diversion of resources and attention to such programs, they contend, has come at the expense of the academic mission of most schools, which have gradually lowered admissions standards and tolerated poor academic achievement for the sake of successful athletics programs.
  • Source:  Do College Athletics Play a Valuable Role in Higher Education?
  • Original Text
13
Use What it Says / What it Means / What it Does Strategically
  • By examining a text from all three angles, you will have a very strong understanding of the reading.  Youll be well prepared to argue for or against the authors ideas.  You may even be able to use your notes from this strategy to start building a paper.
  • Do keep in mind that the sample used in the previous slides was very detailed.  You may not have to be as detailed when reading each paragraph in an assigned text.
  • This strategy works well for a text that you have to read very closely and respond to in detail.

Strategy 2:  CAASE:  Comprehend, Apply, Analyze, Synthesize, Evaluate
16
What is CAASE?
  • CAASE is an active reading strategy that asks you to use the information from a reading in a variety of ways.  Each of the steps is more cognitively challenging than the next; by using the information in challenging ways, you will learn it better and be able to retain it longer.
  • You may see some similarities with What it Says / What it Means / What it Does.  Most of the time, you wont need to use both strategies for the same reading.  Instead, choose the strategy that works best for you and fits the assignment.
17
What does CAASE stand for?
  • Comprehend what you read
  • Apply the knowledge from the reading
  • Analyze what you read for accuracy and credibility
  • Synthesize what you read from multiple sources
  • Evaluate what you read so you can make good decisions


  • Lets look at each step separately!
18
Comprehend
  • When you comprehend a text, you have grasped the meaning of the material and can explain it to someone else.  Ask yourself the following questions to make sure that you comprehend a text:
  • What is the text about?
  • Why is it relevant (to your assignment, your class, your future career, or your life)?
19
Sample:  Comprehend
  • What is the text about?
  • The text is about social anxiety disorder, which people suffer if they are afraid of talking or being around other people.  They dont like the idea that others can be judging them.
  • Why is it relevant?
  • It is relevant because the entire chapter is on psychological disorders, which well be tested on next week.
  • Comprehending the Text
  • Social anxiety disorder involves a fear of interacting with others or being in a social situation and is one of the most common phobias people experience.  People with social anxiety disorder are afraid of being evaluated in some negative way by others, so they tend to avoid situations that could lead to something embarrassing or humiliating.  They are very self-conscious as a result (547).


  • Source:
  • Ciccarelli, Saundra and J. Noland White.
  • Psychology.  4th Custom Ed for COC.  Upper Saddle River:  Pearson, 2014.  Print.
  • Original Text
20
Apply
  • To apply the information in the text, you should be able to use the material in new situations.  For example, you might apply a rule (like a traffic law) to a particular situation (a driver that is pulled over by a police officer).
  • You may ask yourself some of the following questions to show you can apply the information in a text:
  • Can I think of an example of the concept in the text?
  • How might I use this concept in my education, life, or career?
  • In the case of our psychology example, how might I diagnose a patient with social anxiety disorder?
21
Sample:  Apply
  • Can I think of an example of someone with social anxiety disorder?
  • One of my classmates had to give a speech in psychology.  She was so nervous that she could barely express her ideas to the class.  She finished quickly and almost ran back to her seat.
  • How might I diagnose a patient with social anxiety disorder?
  • I would ask the patient how s/he feels in social situations.  I would ask if s/he feels judged.  I would ask if s/he ever feels stage fright or fear of public speaking.
  • Applying the Concepts
  • Social anxiety disorder involves a fear of interacting with others or being in a social situation and is one of the most common phobias people experience.  People with social anxiety disorder are afraid of being evaluated in some negative way by others, so they tend to avoid situations that could lead to something embarrassing or humiliating.  They are very self-conscious as a result (547).

Analyze
  • Weve covered some aspects of analysis when discussing What it Means.  For the CAASE strategy, you should be analyzing for accuracy and credibility.  This will involve:
  • Fact checking.  Is the authors information correct?
  • Examining dates.  Is the authors information recent enough to be useful?
  • Questioning credibility.  Is the author an expert in the subject?  Can readers trust what s/he has to say?
23
Sample:  Analyze
  • Is the authors information correct?
  • The American Psychiatric Association (APA) defines social anxiety disorder as:  Significant anxiety and discomfort about being embarrassed or looked down on in social or performance situations. Common examples are public speaking, meeting people, or using public restrooms.  The definition is very close to the definition used in the textbook.


  • Analyzing for Accuracy and Credibility
  • Social anxiety disorder involves a fear of interacting with others or being in a social situation and is one of the most common phobias people experience.  People with social anxiety disorder are afraid of being evaluated in some negative way by others, so they tend to avoid situations that could lead to something embarrassing or humiliating.  They are very self-conscious as a result (547).

Synthesize
  • Integrate the new information that you have just read into your knowledge of the topic.  This could come from your past experiences, your other reading assignments, your previous classes, etc.


  • Then, consider:
  • How does the new information relate to what I already knew?
  • What conclusions can you draw based on the new information?
25
Sample:  Synthesize
  • How does the new information relate to what I already knew?
  • I already knew that some people are shy or afraid to speak in public.  Many people are afraid to be judged by others.  I thought that these behaviors were just part of personalitysome people are shy, and some people arent.  However, the book shows that some shy people suffer from a disorder.  I wonder if this means that they cant help their behavior or feelings?  Can social anxiety disorder be treated?  I should do more reading on this disorder.
  • Synthesizing with Prior Knowledge
  • Social anxiety disorder involves a fear of interacting with others or being in a social situation and is one of the most common phobias people experience.  People with social anxiety disorder are afraid of being evaluated in some negative way by others, so they tend to avoid situations that could lead to something embarrassing or humiliating.  They are very self-conscious as a result (547).

Evaluate
  • Use the information you gained from the reading to make decisions or judgments.  You can also use the information to consider the implications of your decisions.
  • You might ask yourself:
  • Can I determine the best rationale, plan, solution, given the information?
  • What would happen if I do X?
  • In the case of our psychology example, we could try to determine if a particular person has social anxiety disorder.
27
Sample:  Evaluate
  • Do I have social anxiety disorder?
  • First, I could examine the symptoms of social anxiety disorder.  Then, I would decide if I exhibit those symptoms or not.  Finally, if I do exhibit those symptoms, I could choose to see a counselor to help me learn to manage those symptoms better.
  • Evaluating to Make a Decision
  • Social anxiety disorder involves a fear of interacting with others or being in a social situation and is one of the most common phobias people experience.  People with social anxiety disorder are afraid of being evaluated in some negative way by others, so they tend to avoid situations that could lead to something embarrassing or humiliating.  They are very self-conscious as a result (547).

Use CAASE Strategically
  • CAASE works very well for a research assignment because it asks you to fact check, consider the authors credibility, and synthesize the reading with other sources.
  • Consider using CAASE for a research project; it can help you do anything from evaluating a website to creating a literature review.

Strategy 3:  Improving Memory
31
To Remember, We Must Understand
  • In order to remember the material that you read, you must first understand that material.
  • This is why we began the workshop with comprehension strategiesif you dont comprehend the material, you wont be able to remember it well.
  • Once you understand the material, you can apply several memory strategies to help you remember it better.
32
General Memory Strategies
  • Recite the information or write it several times.
  • Break up large segments of information into smaller, easier to recall units.
  • Create Acronyms:  Write short letter combinations to help you remember key concepts.
    • Example:  PEMDAS will help you remember the order of operations in math, which is:  Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication, Division, Addition, and Subtraction.
  • Test Yourself:  Write short quizzes for so you can evaluate how well youve learned the information.
33
Memory Strategy:  Make Connections
  • You are more likely to remember information if you can relate it to something you already know or have experienced yourself.  When reading new material, try making connections to remember it better, such as:
  • Personalize the information by relating it to you.
  • Associate the information to other ideas.
34
Memory Strategy:  Use Visuals
  • Sometimes, it is helpful to use visual techniques to help us understand or remember material better. Turning the information into a picture makes it easier to remember.


  • Create a chart with the information.
  • Visualize the information in your mind.
  • Create a drawing of the information.

Strategy 4:  What Strong Readers Do
37
Use a Toolbox
  • Strong readers have a variety of strategies available in their mental toolbox.  By practicing, they know which strategies work best in which situations.


  • As you gain more and more reading experience, you will develop your own toolbox of strategies.  In addition, you will learn which strategies to activate at which time.
38
Know Your Purpose
  • Strong readers know why they are reading and what they want to get out of the reading assignment.  As you read, consider the questions:
  • How might this reading relate to choice of career?
  • How does this reading relate to your class?
  • Will you be tested on this material?  Asked to write an essay?  Asked to discuss it in class?


  • If strong readers are asked to prepare a reading for discussion, they may look for the controversial aspects of the reading and consider discussion questions in advance.  In contrast, if strong readers are asked to prepare a reading for a quiz, they may spend more time trying to memorize terms and anticipate possible quiz questions.  They can alter their strategies based on their purpose.
39
Chunk
  • A strong reader chunks a reading by breaking down a text into meaningful segments.  This can help them make sure they understand the text well.  If they do not understand the text well, chunking the test will help readers identify the portion they are struggling with.
  • Strong readers are able to explain the meaning of the different chunks in their own words, which helps them understand the overall meaning of the text better.
40
Know When You Need More Background Information
  • Some texts you read will assume that you already have some knowledge about a topic.  Strong readers can recognize when they lack that knowledge.  They respond by doing background research to help them understand the text better.
41
Review the Text
  • Periodically, stop reading to determine:
    • What do I know?
    • What do I think I know?
    • What has changed about what I thought I knew?
  • Write summaries in your own words
  • Teach what youve learned to someone else
