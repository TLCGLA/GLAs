WHEN TO QUOTE
(OR NOT)
4
WHY DO WE QUOTE?
  • Writers quote to make their arguments more convincing to their readers.  You may quote for any of the following reasons:
  • To provide specific background information that supports your generalizations
  • To explain key terms or concepts
  • To support your claims
  • To lend authority to your argument
  • To anticipate and counter objections


  • Let’s look at some examples!
5
EXAMPLE 1: PROVIDE BACKGROUND INFORMATION
  • Youth baseball players in the Dominican Republic play in poor conditions.  Sports journalist Dave Zirin gives a detailed description of the players, saying that they “play without shoes, using cut-out milk cartons for gloves, rolled-up cloth for balls, and sticks and branches for bats” (465).


  • Explanation:  The writer above makes a generalization about the conditions for young athletes.  Then, she uses a quote from an expert to help illustrate those conditions.  This makes her generalization more concrete and interesting to readers.
6
EXAMPLE 2:  EXPLAIN KEY TERMS
  • One way to purchase local food is to join a community supported agriculture program (CSA).  John Cloud explains the CSA, stating, “you join with others in your community to invest in a local farm” (246).


  • Explanation:  The writer introduces a concept that may not be familiar to many readers.  To help his readers understand the concept, he includes a definition from another author.
7
EXAMPLE 3:  TO SUPPORT CLAIMS
  • Rather than assuming that corporations are inherently anti-environment, we should explore the methods they use to increase sustainability.  Many corporations use natural resources, so it is in their best interest to preserve those resources.  For example, Coca-Cola is deeply involved in combating water scarcity and contamination.  UCLA professor Jared Diamond points out that one of Coca-Cola’s goals is “to make its plants water-neutral, returning to the environment water in quantities equal to the amount used in beverages and their production” (273).


  • Explanation:  The writer sets up her argument regarding the role of corporations in sustainability efforts, the uses the quote as an example of a corporation that is concerned with water conservation.
8
EXAMPLE 4: LEND AUTHORITY
  • The dependence of US agriculture on corn is causing serious environmental problems.  Michael Pollan, an expert on food whose book The Omnivore’s Dilemma was named one of the ten best books of 2006 by the New York Times, reminds us that corn uses “more nitrogen fertilizer than any other crop” (239).


  • Explanation:  The writer includes background information on Michael Pollan to show that he is a credible source who can be trusted to give readers accurate information about agriculture.
9
EXAMPLE 5: TO ANTICIPATE AND COUNTER OBJECTIONS
  • Pop culture affects the body image of both girls and boys.  They see unrealistic images on television and movies; they interact with those images through their toys.  Barbie and GI Joe, both common children’s toys, have unrealistic body sizes and shapes.  Constant play with these toys can saturate a child’s self-perception.  Some may argue, like Kim Franke-Folstad, that “they are toys.  We all know that” (403).  But, the events and impressions of childhood can imprint themselves on an adult.


  • Explanation:  The writer sets up his argument regarding toys and body image.  He knows that some may disagree with him, so he presents an objection to his argument.  Then, he works to overcome that objection.
10
LIMIT QUOTATIONS
  • Although quotations add support to a paper, students should be careful to only quote when necessary.  When students quote too much, their own voice gets lost and readers may not understand how the quotes relate to one another.


  • Think of yourself as the tour guide of the paper.  You may show readers quotes (like a tour guide shows a building or a piece of art), but the readers need your explanation of what quotes mean and how they relate to your point.
11
WHEN SHOULD YOU QUOTE?
  • To ensure that you’re using an appropriate amount of quotes, you should use quotes under the following circumstances:


  • When the author’s language is especially vivid or expressive
  • When exact wording is needed for technical accuracy
  • When it is important to let the debaters of an issue explain their position in their own words
  • When the words of an authority lend weight to an argument
  • When the language of a source is the topic of your discussion (as in an analysis or interpretation)


  • If the quote doesn’t meet one of the above criteria, consider summarizing or paraphrasing instead.

IN-TEXT CITATIONS FOR LITERATURE
13
IN-TEXT CITATIONS
  • The standard methods for completing in-text citations are covered in the MLA 1 Workshop.  However, this slide will provide a brief review.


  • What is an in-text citation?
  • It is the way students cite quotes within the body of their paper.


  • What does an in-text citation include?
  • For a quote from a book with page numbers, an in-text citation should include:
  • A signal phrase that includes the name of the author
  • The page number where the quote can be found
14
SAMPLE IN-TEXT CITATION
  • According to psychologist Dr. Carol Dweck, “as soon as children learn to evaluate themselves, some of them become afraid of challenges” (16).


  • Signal phrase:  According to psychologist Dr. Carol Dweck
  • Page number:  16
15
WHEN TO USE ITALICS/QUOTES FOR LITERATURE
16
IN-TEXT CITATION FOR LONG POEMS
  • If the poem is divided by part, stanza, and line numbers, list them and put periods between each section.


  • Example:
  • Homer’s Iliad begins with a description of Achilles’s rage, characterizing it as “murderous anger which condemned Achaeans / to countless agonies” (1.1.2-3).


  • Note that…
  • There is a slash mark after “Achaeans” to show where the line would break
  • The first 1 in the citation refers to the part; the quote comes from the first book of the Iliad
  • The second 1 in the citation refers to the stanza
  • The 2-3 refers to the line numbers in the poem
17
IN-TEXT CITATION FOR SHORT POEMS OR SONG LYRICS
  • If the poem/song is not divided into numbered parts or stanzas, use line numbers.  For the first reference, use the word “lines.”  After the first reference, just use the numbers.


  • Example:
  • Lucille Clifton’s poem “homage to my hips” glorifies large hips, stating “they don't fit into little / petty places” (lines 4-5).


  • Note that…
  • The in-text citation uses the word “lines.”  This indicates that it is the first quote used from that poem.
  • If the writer quotes from the poem again, s/he would use numbers only, like this:  (6-7).
  • The title of the poem is meant to be in lower case.  That’s how the author wrote the title.
18
USING BLOCK QUOTES FOR POETRY
  • If you are citing three or more lines of poetry, you should:
  • Double indent it (one inch from left margin)
  • Double space the quote
  • Do not use quotation marks


  • Beware:  Avoid over-citing poetry.  Focus your efforts on quoting the words/phrases you intend to analyze.  Sometimes, when students use multiple block quotes, it looks like they are just trying to fill space in their paper.
19
SAMPLE BLOCK QUOTE FOR POETRY
  • In her poem “Planetarium,” Adrienne Rich describes constellations as
  • A woman in the shape of a monster
  • a monster in the shape of a woman
  • the skies are full of them (lines 1-3).
20
PLEASE NOTE:  BLOCK QUOTES CAN BE USED FOR PROSE SOURCES TOO
  • For prose sources, use a block quote if you are using four or more lines of text.  As with poetry, you should:
  • Double indent it (one inch from left margin)
  • Double space the quote
  • Do not use quotation marks


  • Use block quotes sparingly.  When at all possible, use the most important words or phrases from the original quote.


  • Consider summarizing long passages rather than quoting them.
21
SAMPLE BLOCK QUOTE FOR PROSE
  • John Cloud describes the some of the critiques of organic food, stating:
  • Many chefs, food writers and politically minded eaters are outraged that “Big Organic” firms now use the same industrial-sized farming and long- distance-shipping methods as conventional agribusiness. (241)
22
IN-TEXT CITATION FOR VERSE PLAYS
  • To cite a verse play (a play written in poetry, like the works of Shakespeare), use the act, scene, and line numbers.  Separate each number with periods.


  • Example:
  • In A Midsummer Night’s Dream, Puck laments the stupidity of humans, stating, “Lord, what fools these mortals be!” (3.2.1152).


  • Note that…
  • In the citation, the 3 refers to the act number
  • The 2 refers to the scene number
  • The 1152 refers to the line number
23
IN-TEXT CITATION FOR PROSE PLAYS
  • To cite a prose play (a play not written in poetry), include the page number in quotation marks.


  • Example:
  • Walter, one of the main characters of Hansberry’s A Raisin in the Sun, expresses his frustration with his wife when he states, “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream.  His woman say: Eat your eggs” (1568).

ADVANCED QUOTING TECHNIQUES
25
WHAT ARE ADVANCED QUOTING TECHNIQUES?
  • Students can modify the quote they use to make their paper flow more smoothly or to focus on specific words or phrases.  They may do this by:
  • Removing words
  • Adding words


  • In addition, students may use quotes that include quotation marks or are quoted from another source.  These types of quotes have specific citation requirements.
26
TO REMOVE WORDS
  • Students may want to remove words from the middle of a quote to focus specifically on certain words and phrases.  To remove words and phrases, use the ellipsis mark (the grammatically correct term for three periods).


  • Cautions:
  • Don’t remove words or phrases that drastically influence the meaning of the original text
  • Be sure that your sentence is still a complete sentence.
  • Do not use the ellipsis at the beginning or end of a quote.  Your readers will understand that a quote is taken from a longer passage.
27
EXAMPLE OF QUOTE WITH REMOVED WORDS
  • Original Passage:  “U.S. track star Jeremy Wariner, 400-metre gold medal winner, will slip on customized Adidas spike shoes.”


  • Modified Quote:  Margaret Munro lists several athletes who will wear high-tech gear during the Olympics.  She states that “U.S. track star Jeremy Wariner…will slip on customized Adidas spike shoes” (447).


  • Note that…
  • The author removed the phrase “400-metre gold medal winner”
  • The meaning of the passage isn’t drastically changed by removing that phrase
28
TO ADD WORDS
  • Students may want to add words to a quote in order to improve clarity.  For example, the original quote may use an acronym (like “CIA”) and the student wants to explain the acronym (“Central Intelligence Agency”).  In order to do that, the student must add the words in [brackets].
29
EXAMPLE OF QUOTE WITH ADDED WORDS
  • Original Passage:  “Thirty-eight world records have been set by athletes wearing LZRs since its debut in February, making it the must-have suit for Beijing.”


  • Modified Quote:
  • Margaret Munro discusses the success of high-tech sports equipment, pointing out that “thirty-eight world records have been set by athletes wearing LZRs [Speedo’s racing suit] since its debut in February” (447).


  • Note that…
  • The writer added text explaining what an LZR is using brackets
  • The writer did not quote the whole sentence from the original quote
30
USING A QUOTE WITH QUOTATION MARKS
  • Some passages already have quotation marks in the text
  • In order to quote those passages, change the quotation marks to ‘apostrophes’
31
EXAMPLE OF A QUOTE WITH QUOTATION MARKS
  • Original Passage:  Add it all up and the LZR Racer suit, according to manufacturer Speedo, increases “oxygen efficiency” by five per cent, makes starts, springs and turns four per cent faster, and lessens “passive drag” by 10 per cent.


  • To Quote the Original:
  • Margaret Munro discusses the features of the LZR swimsuit, stating that it “increases ‘oxygen efficiency’ by five percent” (447).


  • Note that…
  • The quotation marks around “oxygen efficiency” in the original passage become apostrophes in the quote.
32
QUOTING A QUOTE
  • Sometimes, students want to quote a person that the author has already quoted.  For example, Margaret Munro includes a quote by Larry Katz in her article.  Students may want to quote Katz’s statement.  To do so, use the abbreviation “qtd. in,” which stands for “quoted in.”


  • Example:
  • Article Author:  Margaret Munro
  • Quote Author:  Larry Katz
  • Page Number:  448


  • Larry Katz asserts that, “instead of being athlete against athlete, you’re now in a situation where it’s technology versus technology” (qtd. in Munro 448).
33
EXAMPLE OF QUOTING A QUOTE
  • Article Author:  Andy Hinds
  • Quote Author:  Alyssa Croft
  • Croft’s Statement:  Our results suggest that when fathers espouse and enact a more equal distribution of domestic work, their daughters more easily envision balancing work with family and having a less gender-stereotypic career.


  • Doctoral candidate Alyssa Croft studies the effects of fathers on their daughters’ future careers.  She discovered that “when fathers espouse and enact a more equal distribution of domestic work, their daughters more easily envision balancing work with family and having a less gender-stereotypic career” (qtd. in Hinds).


  • Note that there is no page number because the article is published online without page numbers.
34
HOW TO INTEGRATE A QUOTE
35
TO INTEGRATE A QUOTE INTO YOUR PAPER SUCCESSFULLY…
  • Introduce it using a signal phrase
  • Blend the author’s words with your own
  • Make sure the sentence with the quote is complete and grammatically correct
  • Discuss the quote
36
SIGNAL PHRASES
  • Signal phrases let readers know that you are about to introduce a quote.  They create a smooth reading experience for readers, and allow you to set up the quote with some background information.


  • Remember the tour guide comparison we used before?  A good tour guide in an art museum wouldn’t just point out paintings.  A good tour guide would introduce you to the paintings and the artists.  You should do the same for your readers.
37
SAMPLE SIGNAL PHRASES
  • Advanced:
  • X agrees/disagrees when he writes that
  • X complicates matters further when she states
  • X’s claim that ____ rests on the questionable assumption that ___.
  • By focusing on ___, X overlooks the deeper problem of ___.
  • X’s theory of ____ is extremely useful because it sheds insight on the difficult problem of ____.
  • While X is probably wrong when she claims that ___, she is right that ___.


  • Basic:
  • According to X
  • X states
  • X argues
  • X illustrates
  • X suggests
  • X describes
  • X emphasizes
  • In her article, “Title,” X notes that
38
EXAMPLE USING AN ADVANCED SIGNAL PHRASE
  • Law enforcement officers in Albuquerque, New Mexico are searching for internet predators.  Their plan to show internet predators that they “are watching” is well-meant; however, it overlooks the fact that a child is far more likely to be harmed by someone s/he knows rather than a dangerous stranger online (Mozzone).


  • Note that…
  • The quote uses only a brief phrase to make its point
  • The in-text citation uses the author’s name because it isn’t mentioned in the signal phrase
  • The in-text citation does not use a page number because it was published online
  • The example does not use any of the advanced signal phrases exactly.  Instead, it makes modifications to fit its argument.
39
BLEND THE AUTHOR’S WORDS WITH YOUR OWN
  • By blending the author’s words, you create a smoother reading experience for the reader.  You show that you are able to manage both the author’s ideas and your own, and you can explain them to your audience.  This is a more advanced writing technique that will show off your control of the language.
40
EXAMPLE OF BLENDED TEXT
  • Original Passage:  “One of the underlying assumptions has been that females are simply a variation on a theme, that it isn’t a fundamentally different mechanism, that if you’ve learned about the male you’ve learned enough to deal with both males and females,” she [Jill Becker] said. “We’ve discovered that’s not always the case.”


  • Historically, scientists have been more likely to test pharmaceuticals on male animals rather than females because they do not want to account for female hormonal cycles.  Unfortunately, this practice has led to unexpected side effects in medications for women.  Jill Becker, a research scientist, shows the scientific community’s belief that “females are simply a variation on a theme”— a variation that they incorrectly thought was insignificant (Rabin).
41
MAKE COMPLETE, GRAMMATICALLY CORRECT SENTENCES
  • No matter how you integrate quotes in your writing, make sure your sentences are complete and grammatically correct.  Read the sentences out loud to listen for potential errors.
42
CORRECTING A COMMON ERROR
  • Common Error:  In the novel The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald shows the decadence of the Jazz Age in 1920’s New York City.


  • The above sentence has two problems.  If we correct them both, we will have a correct sentence.
    • Missing Comma
    • Missing subject
  • With Comma (Not yet correct):  In the novel The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald, shows the decadence of the Jazz Age in 1920’s New York City.
  • With Comma and Subject:  In the novel The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald, he shows the decadence of the Jazz Age in 1920’s New York City.
  • Alternative Solution (Remove “In”):  The novel The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald shows the decadence of the Jazz Age in 1920’s New York City.
43
DISCUSS THE QUOTE
  • Once you have successfully integrated a quote into your paper, be sure to discuss it.  Answer one or more of the following questions:
  • What does the quote mean?
  • How does it relate to your point?
  • How does it support your point?
  • How does it relate to other information you’ve already shared with the audience?


  • Discussing the quote gives you the opportunity to guide the audience and help them understand your point better.  It allows you to keep the role of tour guide in your own paper.
44
EXAMPLE OF A QUOTE WITH DISCUSSION
  • Doctoral candidate Alyssa Croft studies the effects of fathers on their daughters’ future careers.  She discovered that “when fathers espouse and enact a more equal distribution of domestic work, their daughters more easily envision balancing work with family and having a less gender-stereotypic career” (qtd. in Hinds).  Essentially, Croft argues that fathers who do their chores will raise more successful daughters.
