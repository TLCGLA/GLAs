PART 1: 
ARTICLE OVERVIEW
4
What Are Articles?
  • In English, we use the articles “a,” “an,” and “the.”
  • Articles are parts of speech that announce nouns.
  • They appear before a noun.
  • They give the reader information about the noun.  They tell us if the noun is specific or general.  For example, if I mention “a cookie,” I’m talking about cookies in general.  If I mention “the cookie,” I’m talking about a specific cookie.
  • In some cases, you do not need to use any article at all.
5
What Is a Noun?
  • In the previous slide, we learned that articles give information about nouns.  So, to use articles properly, we must understand what nouns are.


  • Nouns are the names of people, places, things, activities, or ideas.  Here are some examples:


  • People: Harry Potter, nurse, student
  • Places: Valencia, College of the Canyons, mall
  • Things: phone, pizza, skateboard
  • Activities: hiking, surfing, skating
  • Ideas: love, equality, democracy
6
Types of Nouns
  • Count Nouns:  It is possible to count individual items.  Count nouns can be singular or plural.  For example, “pencil” is a count noun.  If I had a box of pencils in front of me, I could count the individual pencils in the box.


  • Noncount Nouns:  It is not possible to count individual items.  Noncount nouns cannot be made plural.  For example, “gasoline” is a noncount noun.  If I had a gallon of gasoline in front of me, I could not count the individual gasolines.


  • Note:  Some nouns may be countable in another language, but not in English.


  • Gerunds:  Present participles used as a noun.  Gerunds cannot be preceded by “the.”  For example, I could tell you, “Running is difficult.”  In this sentence, “running” is used as a noun.
7
How to Use Articles Properly
  • First, you must have a clear understanding of what the three articles (a/an/the) mean.  Parts 2 and 3 of this workshop will give you information to help you.
  • Then, you will identify what type of noun you are working with (count, noncount, or gerund).
  • Finally, you will decide whether the noun you are working with is specific or general.
8
Specific or General?
  • When you are deciding what article to use, you must decide if the noun is specific or general.


  • Let’s look at an example.
  • If I say, “let’s wash the car,” I’m talking about a specific car.  The listener and I both know which car we’re going to wash.


  • However, if I say, “let’s wash a car,” I’m not talking about a specific car.  I’m suggesting that the listener wash any car rather than one specific car.

PART 2:
INDEFINITE ARTICLES (A/AN)
10
What Is an Indefinite Article?
  • The indefinite articles are “a” and “an.”  They show that the noun can refer to any member of a group.


  • Let’s look at an example:
  • My son wants a car for his eighteenth birthday.
  • This sentence refers to any car.  We don’t know which specific car that he wants.


  • Here’s another example:
  • An ant can lift twenty times its own body weight.
  • This sentence refers to any ant.  There are many ants in the world, and this sentence could describe any of them.
11
Use Indefinite Articles to Show Membership
  • We use “a” and “an” to show that an individual is a member of a group.


  • Examples:
  • Miguel is a salsa dancer.  (There is a large group of salsa dancers, and Miguel is one member.)
  • Lavpreet is a practicing Hindu.  (Lavpreet is a member of the group of people known as Hindus.)
12
Other Indefinite Article Rules
  • When we use the indefinite articles (a or an), we give our readers information about the noun.  We tell them:
  • The noun is singular
  • The noun is a member of a large group
  • The noun represents the whole group


  • Let’s look at an example:
  • A dragonfly can travel up to 60 mph.


  • In this example, we are discussing one dragonfly (singular), but it is a member of a large group of all the dragonflies in the world.
  • The dragonfly in our sentence represents all dragonflies.
13
Where Indefinite Articles DO NOT Go
  • Do not use “a” or “an” before a proper noun (the name of a person or place).
  • Incorrect: Carolina wants to take her picture in front of an Eiffel Tower.
  • Correct: Carolina wants to her picture in front of the Eiffel Tower.


  • Do not use “a” or “an” before plural or noncount nouns.
  • Incorrect: Susan bought a watermelons to make fruit salad.
  • Correct: Susan bought watermelons to make fruit salad.
  • Incorrect: The car needs a gas before we can drive to San Diego.
  • Correct: The car needs gas before we can drive to San Diego.
14
Where Indefinite Articles DO NOT Go
  • Do not use “a” or “an” before a pronoun (a word that replaces a noun).
  • Incorrect: I’ve been looking for a him.
  • Correct: I’ve been looking for him.


  • Do not use “a” or “an” before an adjective without a noun after it.
  • Incorrect: Nancy prefers the dress in a green.
  • Correct: Nancy prefers the dress in green.
15
When Do I Use “a?”
  • Use the indefinite article a before nouns that start with consonant sounds (b, d, k, t, etc.).
  • A rocket
  • A shell
  • A whale


  • Use a when a word begins with a “yoo” or “wuh” as a consonant sound.
  • A university
  • A one hundred dollar bill
16
When Do I Use “an?”
  • Use the indefinite article an before nouns that start with vowel sounds (a, e, i, o, u, etc.)
  • An iceberg
  • An eagle
  • An olive


  • Use an when using a letter of the alphabet that begins with a vowel sound
  • Draw an “x” on the paper.
  • I’m going to an NAACP-sponsored event.


  • Use an when using a word that begins with “h,” but the “h” sound is silent
  • An hour
  • An honest person
17
Here Are Some Common Phrases that Use “a” or “an.”
  • Once a day / a week / a month / a year
  • Two (or any number over one) times a day / a week
  • An hour after midnight / noon / sunrise
  • Half an hour earlier / later
  • A pair of socks / a couple of times / a number of items


  • Example:
  • Once a month, I buy a new pair of socks and a number of household cleaning items.
18
Here Are Some Common Idioms (Sayings) that Use “a” or “an.”
  • Sound like a broken record:  to repeat the same thing over and over
  • A horse of a different color:  a completely different matter.
  • A chip on one’s shoulder:  to have a bad attitude
  • Don’t judge a book by its cover:  don’t judge something by its outward appearance
  • A slap on the wrist:  a light punishment for doing something wrong
  • A piece of cake:  something very easy
  • At a glance:  to understand something almost immediately

PART 3:
DEFINITE ARTICLE (THE)
20
What Is a Definite Article?
  • The definite article is “the.”  It shows that the noun refers to a specific member of a group.


  • Let’s look at an example:
  • The house is decorated with lights.
  • The sentence refers to a specific house.  It does not apply to any house, but to one particular house.


  • Here’s another example:
  • Aziz was afraid to disturb the bee hive.
  • The sentence refers to one particular bee hive.
21
Definite Article Rules
  • The definite article “the” can be used before count OR noncount nouns.
  • Examples:
  • The textbook I bought yesterday was expensive.  (“Textbook” is a count noun.)
  • Sara wasted the money her mother gave her on candy and stickers.  (“Money” is a noncount noun.)


  • The definite article “the” can be used before singular OR plural nouns.
  • Examples:
  • Did you see the movie I recommended?  (“Movie” is singular.)
  • Did you taste the grapes I gave you? (“Grapes” are plural.)
22
Other Definite Article Rules
  • Use the definite article “the” when there is only one of something (a unique or singular object).
  • Examples:
  • The world, the sun, the earth, the moon, the sky, etc.
  • Neil Armstrong was the first man on the moon.


  • Use the definite article “the” before certain nouns.
  • Examples:
  • The movies, the theater, the radio, the internet, the post office, the bank, the station, etc.
  • I have to go to the bank before I can meet you at the movies.
23
Other Definite Article Rules
  • Use “the” before musical instruments when discussing the instrument in general.
  • I studied the piano for five years before I fell in love with the drums.
  • Jae thinks the tuba is the most difficult instrument to play in marching band because it is so heavy.


  • Use “the” when talking about a type of animal.
  • Today’s dogs are descendants of the wolf.
  • The housefly lives only two weeks.


  • Use “the” when talking about a group of people.
  • After graduating college, Sam wanted to help the homeless.
  • Lisa was surprised to see just how much the young use their cell phones.
24
Proper Nouns that Use “The”
  • Use “the” if the proper noun uses the pattern “the…of.”
  • The Fourth of July
  • The University of California


  • Use “the” with plural proper nouns.
  • The Los Angeles Dodgers
  • The Black Keys


  • Use “the” with collective proper nouns (a noun that names a group).
  • The Justice League
  • The Sierra Club
25
Geographical Rules
  • Use “the” before the names of oceans, seas, rivers, forests, mountain ranges, and deserts (but not lakes).
  • I saw the Mississippi River when I visited New Orleans.
  • Lake Havasu is located in the Mojave Desert.


  • Use “the” before points on the globe and geographical areas.
  • Peter crossed the equator to visit the South Pole.
  • My cousins from the Middle East are visiting for a month.


  • Use “the” before the names of some specific countries and cities (but not all).
  • Maria’s mother had always dreamed of moving to the United States.
  • I wanted to visit the tulip fields in the Netherlands.

PART 4:
LEAVING OUT ARTICLES
27
When Do We Leave Out Articles?
  • Do not use an article when generalizing about plural count nouns.
  • Elephants are intelligent creatures.  (“Elephants” are plural count nouns.)
  • You should always recycle used water bottles. (“Bottles” are plural count nouns.)


  • Do not use an article when generalizing about noncount nouns.
  • Time is money.  (Both “time” and “money” are noncount nouns.)
  • Furniture enhances the beauty of a room.  (“Furniture” is a noncount noun.)


  • Do not use an article before a gerund when making a general statement.
  • Skiing is a winter sport.  (“Skiing” is a gerund.)
  • The boy’s parents taught him that lying is wrong.  (“Lying” is a gerund.)
28
When Do We Leave Out Articles?
  • Do not use an article with names of languages or nationalities.
  • Sevan speaks four languages:  Armenian, Farsi, German, and English.
  • When Sally went to Seoul as an exchange student, she was looking forward to making Korean friends.


  • Do not use an article when using the name of sports.
  • He wanted to play basketball professionally.
  • Canada won the gold medal in hockey.


  • Do not use an article when using the names of academic subjects.
  • She needs to pass math before she can begin her engineering classes.
  • Geology is the study of the Earth, including rocks and volcanoes.
29
Geographical Rules
  • Do not use articles before the names of:
  • Most countries or territories (Canada, China, Puerto Rico)
  • Towns, cities, or states (Valencia, Paris, California)
  • Streets (Front Street, Lakewood Avenue, Dogwood Crescent)
  • Lakes:  (Lake Ontario, Lake Placid, Big Bear Lake)
  • Mountains (Mount Everest, K2, Mount Kilimanjaro)
  • Continents (North America, Africa, Antarctica)
  • Islands (Hawaii, Crete, Kodiak)


  • Please note:  There are some exceptions to these rules, such as “the Matterhorn” (a mountain).  You should look up unfamiliar place names before using them in a sentence.
