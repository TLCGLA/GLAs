What is a fragment?
  • A fragment is an unfinished or incomplete sentence
  • It may be missing a subject and/or a verb
  • It may have an incomplete meaning


  • Fragments are mistakes that can be corrected by adding the missing part of the sentence
4
A fragment is NOT a short sentence.
  • The following sentences are all complete and correct even though they are short.
  • Amy ran.
  • Becky bought bread.
  • Carol is tall.
  • Debbie sang.
5
In fact, fragments can be LONG groups of words.
  • The following are fragments even though they are long.
  • Although he had been recognized for his athletic abilities and won a record number of gold medals in the Summer Olympics in 2012.
  • Knowing better than anyone else the dangers of serving badly cooked food, such as disease, loss of customers, and bad reviews online.
  • But Emily’s wish of getting married in a Cinderella dress in the middle of Disneyland in front of all her friends and family wouldn’t come true.
6
Avoiding Fragments
  • The best way to know how to avoid fragments is to understand the rules for creating complete sentences.  This will help you make sure that each of your sentences is complete.
7
What is a complete sentence?
  • A complete sentence includes:
  • A subject (the noun the sentence is about)
  • A verb (what the subject does or is)
  • A complete thought (the sentence makes sense on its own)
8
Let’s look at an example!
  • Example:  Janine taught her dog to roll over.


  • Subject: Janine
  • “Janine” is a noun, and the sentence is about her.


  • Verb: taught
  • “Taught” lets readers know what Janine did


  • Complete thought:  The sentence makes sense on its own.  Readers have all the information they need to understand what the writer is trying to say.
  • This is a complete sentence, also known as an independent clause.
9
Missing Subject
  • If a sentence is missing a subject, it is a fragment.  To correct the fragment, add a subject.


  • Example:
  • The dentist looked at Dana’s teeth.  Pulled out the bad tooth.


  • Explanation:
  • The second sentence is a fragment because it is missing a subject, even though the subject is the same for both sentences.  We must include a subject in both sentences.


  • Corrected:
  • The dentist looked at Dana’s teeth.  She pulled out the bad tooth.
10
Missing Verb
  • If a sentence is missing a verb, it is a fragment.  To correct the fragment, add a verb.


  • Example:
  • Tim sitting on the steps of his grandfather’s house.


  • Explanation:
  • The sentence uses a gerund (“sitting”), but not a complete verb.  To correct the sentence, we must add a “to be” verb (either “is,” “was,” or “will be”).


  • Corrected:
  • Tim is sitting on the steps of his grandfather’s house.
11
Subordinate Clause Fragment
  • Each sentence must include an independent clause to be the main clause of the sentence.
  • An independent clause includes a subject, verb, and complete thought.
  • Clauses that begin with a subordinating conjunction (after, because, when, etc.) are dependent.  They cannot stand on their own.


  • Example:
  • When we saw something on the road.


  • Explanation:
  • This clause begins with a subordinating conjunction (“when”), which makes the clause dependent.  It cannot function on its own; it is a subordinate clause fragment.
12
Helpful Hint
  • It can help to recognize these fragments by reading the sentence aloud. When you hear it, you often know that the sentence (the idea being expressed) is unfinished.

Relative Clause Fragments
  • Like subordinate clauses, relative clauses are fragments if they are not attached to an independent clause.
  • Relative clauses begin with relative pronouns (who, whom, that, which, whoever, whomever, and whichever).


  • Example:
  • Fabio is my nine-year-old nephew.  Who loves to play video games for hours at a time.


  • Explanation:
  • The second sentence is a relative clause fragment.  It begins with the relative pronoun “who” and does not connect to any independent clauses.
16
Correcting a Relative Clause Fragment
  • To correct a relative clause fragment, connect it to an independent clause.


  • Example:
  • Fabio is my nine-year-old nephew who loves to play video games for hours at a time.


  • Explanation:
  • The relative clause is now connected to an independent clause.  The clause includes a subject (“Fabio”), a verb (“is”), and it is a complete thought.

Coordinating Conjunction Fragments
  • The coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so.  You can remember them with the acronym FANBOYS.
  • If a coordinating conjunction begins a sentence, it creates a fragment.


  • Example:
  • But Andy was disappointed that the lion was asleep.


  • Explanation:
  • This sentence is a fragment because it does not include a complete thought.  The coordinating conjunction “but” indicates that readers are missing information.  The sentence cannot stand on its own.
19
Correcting Coordinating Conjunction Fragments
  • There are two options for correcting coordinating conjunction fragments.  They are:
  • Remove the coordinating conjunction
  • Join the fragment with an independent clause


  • Example:
  • Andy was disappointed that the lion was asleep.
  • Andy couldn’t wait to see the lion, but he was disappointed that the lion was asleep.


  • Explanation:
  • In the first example, we removed the coordinating conjunction “but.”  Now, the sentence expresses a complete thought and can stand on its own.
  • In the second example, we added an independent clause.  It has a subject (“Andy”), a verb (“wait”), and it expresses a complete thought.

Infinitive Phrase Fragments
  • An infinitive is the word “to” followed by a verb.  (To ski, to bake, to run)
  • An infinitive phrase is missing a subject


  • Example:
  • To look extraordinarily beautiful at the party.


  • Explanation:
  • Readers don’t know who looks beautiful.  The phrase is missing a subject, so it must be a fragment.
22
Correcting Infinitive Phrase Fragments
  • To correct an infinitive phrase fragment, add an independent clause.


  • Example:
  • To look extraordinarily beautiful at the party, Naira spent four hours in the salon.
  • You can also change the order:
  • Naira spent four hours in the salon to look extraordinarily beautiful at the party


  • Explanation:
  • We added an independent clause.  It includes a subject (“Naira”), a verb (“spent”), and it expresses a complete thought.

Participle Phrase Fragments
  • Participles are verbs ending in –ing, -ed, or any other past participle form of the verb.  (Examples: walked, walking, written, writing, etc.)
  • A phrase that begins with a participle is dependent.  If it is not paired with an independent clause, it is a fragment.


  • Example:
  • Studying in the library for the whole day.


  • Explanation:
  • The participle phrase lacks a subject and a complete verb. It cannot stand on its own.
25
Correcting Participle Phrase Fragments
  • There are two options for correcting participle phrase fragments.  They are:
  • Add an entirely new independent clause
  • Add a subject and an auxiliary verb* to the participle phrase


  • Example:
  • Studying in the library all day, Rosie felt tired.
  • Rosie was studying in the library all day.


  • Explanation:
  • In the first option, we added an entirely new independent clause.  It includes a subject (“Rosie”) and a verb (“felt”), and it expresses a complete thought.
  • In the second option, we added the subject (“Rosie”) and the auxiliary verb (“was”) to the existing participle phrase fragment.


  • *The auxiliary verbs are: have, be, do, and will.  They help the main verb describe the subject’s action.
