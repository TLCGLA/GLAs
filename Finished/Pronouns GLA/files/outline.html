PART 1:
SUBJECT, OBJECT, REFLEXIVE, AND POSSESSIVE PRONOUNS
4
Nouns
    • Read the following sentences:


    • Colleges are for students. Valencia has a good college.
    • Mother made us some waffles for breakfast.
    • Mr. Smith is a marine biologist. He knows diving.
  • What do you see? Names of people, names of places, names of things, names of  actions.
  • Well, these are all nouns. Now look at some more noun words:

    People: Harry, Jane, Mr. Smith
  • Places: Valencia, Los Angeles
  • Things: college, waffles, phones
  • Actions: skiing, swimming, reading
  •  Ideas: truth, beauty, democracy
5
Reading without Pronouns
  • Now read this:
  • Harry is a student from Australia. Harry is planning to visit Harry’s parents after ten years. Harry did not see Harry’s parents in ten years. Harry’s parents will be happy to see Harry.


  • How did the reading sound? Funny? Odd? Why?
  • It is because the same nouns are repeated over and over again.


  • Without pronouns, the nouns will have to be repeated over and over again, making the reading sound strange.
6
Replace Nouns with Pronouns
7
Pronouns : Singular
  • Singular pronouns replace singular nouns:
  • Please meet Hina. She is a business student. This is her first semester in America.
  • ‘She’ refers to Hina (one person).
  • ‘This’ refers to the current year (the present year).


  • Singular pronouns: I, you, he, she, it, this, that, who, whom, etc.
8
Pronouns: Plural
  • Plural pronouns replace plural nouns:
  • Look at Mark and James. They are going to close the library now. Tina and I are working on a project, but we must leave.
  • ‘They’ refers to Mark and James (two people).
  • ‘We’ refers to Tina and I (two people).


  • Plural pronouns: We, you, they, these, those, who, whom, etc.

10
Subject Pronouns
  • When a noun is the subject of a sentence, replace it with a subject pronoun.


  • Sample:
  • Tesla is an American car.  Tesla runs on electricity, and Tesla was first introduced in 2008. Ever since, Tesla has gained popularity among its buyers.


  • “Tesla” is the subject in all the sentences. It needs to be replaced by the subject pronoun “it.”


  • Sample with Pronouns:
  • Tesla is an American car. It runs on electricity, and it was first introduced in 2008. Ever since, it has gained popularity among its buyers.


  • Some subject pronouns are: I, you, he, she, it, we, & they.
11
Subject Pronouns
  • When a plural noun is the subject of a sentence, replace it with a plural subject pronoun.


  • Sample:
  • Orville and Wilbur Wright are two brothers. Orville and Wilbur Wright are inventors of the first airplanes. Scientists followed what Orville and Wilbur Wright  had done for their successful aircraft.


  • “Orville and Wilbur Wright” are plural nouns. They are the subjects in the sentences. They need to be replaced by the plural pronoun “they.”


  • Sample with Pronouns:
  • Orville and Wilbur Wright are two brothers. They are inventors of the first airplanes. Scientists followed what they had done for their successful aircraft.
12
Object  Pronouns
  • When a noun is an object replace it with an object pronoun.


  • Sample:
  • Peter ate an apple. He liked it so much that he asked for one more, but his mother gave him two.


  • “It”  refers to “an apple,” which is in the object position in the first sentence.
  • “Him” refers to “Peter” which is in the object position in the second sentence.
  • Instead of saying “he liked an apple,” we say “he liked it,” and instead of saying “his mother gave Peter” we say “his mother gave him.”


  • Some object pronouns are:  me, you, him, her, it, us, them, etc.
13
Object Pronouns
14
Important Pronoun Rules!

  • Don’t mix up subject and object pronouns –
  • My brother and me went to visit our grandparent – wrong!
  • My brother and I went to visit our grandparents – correct!


  • When one of the subjects is I, place it after the other noun or pronoun.
  • I and my sister went to see the zoo – wrong!
  • My sister and I  went to see the zoo – correct!
15
Possessive Pronouns
  • Possessive pronouns show ownership. That means, they indicate that the noun belongs to them.


  • For example, if you own a car, you possess it.  It is yours and belongs to you.  This is also true for more abstract concepts, like friendship.  Grammatically speaking, it is possible to possess someone’s friendship.


  • Some possessive pronouns are: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs, etc.
16
Possessive Pronouns
  • What do you own? Do you own any clothes? If yes, the clothes are yours.


  • Samples:
  • I live in a large house. I bought this house when I moved to California in 2000. The house is mine.
  • You love to drive luxury cars. That new red Ferrari is yours.
  • We all live on this earth. The earth is ours. Let’s preserve it.
17
Possessive Pronouns
  • Let’s look at possessive pronouns in another way. Read the following:


  • Tom opened the fridge and began to eat from a lunch bag. When I saw that, I cried, “ That is my lunch. Yours is in the other fridge!”


  • “Yours” refers to Tom’s lunch bag.


  • Mine is a life of constant struggle, but yours is easy-going.
  • “Mine” refers to my life.
18
Reflexive Pronouns
  • When the subject and the object of a sentence are the same, you need a reflexive pronoun.


  • Some reflexive pronouns are: myself, yourself (singular), himself, herself, itself, yourselves (plural), ourselves, & themselves.


  • Samples:
  • I saw myself in the mirror.
  • Our  students managed themselves well in the inter-college quiz.
  • Tom hurt himself while skateboarding.
19
Reflexive Pronouns
  • In each example, the subjects and the objects are all the same.


  • I saw myself in the mirror.
  • Whom did I see? I saw “I” but we don’t say that. We must use a reflexive pronoun.


  • Our students managed themselves well in the inter-college quiz.
  • Whom did the students manage well? They managed “the students” well. We must use a reflexive pronoun.


  • Tom hurt himself while skateboarding.
  • Whom did Tom hurt? Tom hurt Tom, so we must use the reflexive pronoun “himself.”
20
Reflexive Pronouns
  • Reflexive pronouns can also give emphasis to the noun when they are placed next to the noun. This is a more advanced use but also a good one.


  • Sample:
  • The students themselves admitted to cheating on the test.


  • You could still say: The students admitted to cheating on the test.


  • Adding the reflexive pronoun simply EMPHASIZES  the subject and makes it stronger.
21
Reflexive Pronouns with ‘by’
  • When the preposition ‘by’ is used before a reflexive pronoun, it means “alone.”


  • For example:  By myself, by yourself, by herself, by itself, by themselves, by ourselves,  etc.


  • Samples:
  • The girls said they could clean the room by themselves. They did not need any help.
  • “I want to be by myself; I need some privacy.”


  • “By themselves” suggests “they can do the job alone without anybody’s help.”
  • “By myself” suggests “I want to be alone.”
22
Reflexive Pronouns: Common Mistakes
  • Watch out for mistakes. The following sentences are wrong. Can you figure out why?


  • A man injured hisself while trying to fix a TV antenna.
  • We decided among ourself to form a study group.
  • My neighbors mostly live by themself. They rarely have guests.


  • Go to the next slide for an explanation.
23
Reflexive Pronouns: Correcting Common Mistakes
  •  “Hisself” is a common error.  Correct it by writing “himself.”
  • Corrected:  A man injured himself while trying to fix a TV antenna.


  • “Ourself” and “themself,” are mistakes because they are trying to be singular when they must be plural.  Correct them by writing “ourselves” or “themselves.”
  • Corrected:
  • We decided among ourselves to form a study group.
  • My neighbors mostly live by themselves. They rarely have guests.

PART 2:
RELATIVE, INDEFINITE, DEMONSTRATIVE, AND INTERROGATIVE PRONOUNS
25

Relative Pronoun
Background Knowledge
  • A relative clause (adjective clause) is a dependent clause. It gives information about a noun.


  • Since it is a dependent clause, it cannot stand alone.  A relative clause begins with words like who, which, that, etc.


  • Sample:
  • I got an A on the essay that I turned in last week.


  • “That I turned in last week” is a relative clause. It gives information about the “essay.”
26
Relative Pronouns
  • Relative pronouns start relative (adjective) clauses.
  • Sample:
  • The lady who was talking to me last night was my new neighbor. She told me something that I didn’t believe. She said she remembered her past life, which she spent in a jungle of Indonesia.


  • “Who” (a relative pronoun) starts a relative clause. It is a relative clause because it has a subject and a verb, and it gives information about a noun, “the lady.”
  • “That” (a relative pronoun) starts another relative clause. It gives information about “something” (what the lady told me).
  • “Which” (a relative pronoun) starts yet another relative clause and gives information about the “past life” (of the lady).
  • Some relative pronouns are: who, whom, whose, that, which, whoever, whichever, etc.
27
Relative Pronouns:  Subject and Object
  • Sample:
  • This is the man whom I told you about.  He is the only person I know who can run two marathons in a month.


  • The first relative pronoun you see underlined is “whom”
  • The second relative pronoun you see underlined is “who”


  • Do you know the difference?
  • One acts as an object and one acts as a subject.


  • Let’s go over those sentences again; this time think about subjects and objects.
28
Relative Pronouns:  Who vs. Whom
  • This is the man whom I told you about.
  • “Whom” is in the object position.  One way to know this for sure is to rewrite the sentence as, “I told you about him.”  Since “him” is an object pronoun, I know I must use “whom” here.


  • He is the only person I know who can run two marathons in a month.
  • “Who” is in the subject position.  One way to know this for sure is to rewrite the sentence as, “He can run two marathons in a month.”  Since “he” is a subject pronoun, I know I must use “who” here.
29
Relative Pronouns:  Possessive
  • Samples:
  • The lady whose house we bought was a strange person. She could tell about your past life just by looking at you.


  • The student whose homework you copied hadn’t been to any classes, so you got everything wrong!


  • “Whose” starts a relative clause and it is a possessive pronoun.  In the first sentence, “whose” refers to the lady. In the second, “whose” refers to the student.
30
Indefinite Pronouns
  • Indefinite pronouns replace indefinite or unspecific nouns.
  • Sample:
  • That chocolate brownie was delicious. Can I have another? Any will do.


  • “Another” and “any” do not mean any particular brownie. They mean unspecific pieces of brownies.


  • Some indefinite pronouns are: all, another, anything, nobody, nothing, something, etc.
31
Demonstrative Pronouns
  • Demonstrative pronouns replace definite or specific nouns.


  • Now read the following and see how the meaning changes from the previous sample:


  • These chocolate brownies are delicious. Please give me this one, too. I don’t want that one; it’s broken.


  • In the above sentence, these,” “this,” and “that” indicate particular brownies.


  • The demonstrative pronouns are:  this, these, that, those


  • You probably already know:
  • “This” and “these” refer to nearby things
  • “That” and “those” refer to things that are further away
32
Interrogative Pronouns
  • Interrogative pronouns start questions about people or things.


  • Samples:
  • Who wants to go home early today?
  • What do you have in your big bag?
  • Which restaurant would you suggest for good Thai food?


  • Some interrogative pronouns are: what, who, whom, which, whose, etc.
33
Interrogative Pronouns
  • Read the below statements.  How could you change them to questions using interrogative pronouns?
  • I want to learn Spanish.
  • This scarf is mine.
  • I gave the letter to the man who sat on a big sofa.


  • Go to the next slide to see if you got them right.
34
Interrogative Pronouns

  • These are the questions:


  • Who wants to learn Spanish?
  • Whose scarf is this?
  • To whom did you give the letter?


  • Did you get them all correct? Even the last one? Because it is “give something to someone,” you have to keep the preposition “to” even when you replace the object.
