Comma Splices
  • A comma splice occurs when only a comma joins two independent clauses.
  • Comma splices are mistakes!
2
Agenda
  • This workshop will cover…
  • Independent and Dependent Clauses
  • Comma Splices
  • Methods for Correcting Comma Splices
3
Clauses
  • Example:
  • Yesterday, John came to class without doing his homework. He rarely misses homework assignments.


  • Understand this:
  • A sentence is also called a clause. It completes a thought. The above sentences are independent clauses.
  • This means that each of the sentences are complete.  They include a subject, verb, and complete thought.
4
Independent Clauses
  • Read again:
  • Yesterday, John came to class without doing his homework. He rarely misses homework assignments.


  • These are two sentences – or clauses - and each one has a subject: John, and He.
  •  Each sentence or clause has a verb: came, and misses.
  • In order to stand alone – without help from another clause – 3 things are needed:
    • A subject
    • A verb
    • A complete thought.
  • When a clause has all three features, it is an independent clause.
5
Dependent Clauses
  • Now read these:
  • Because John was sick yesterday.
  • After he got home.
  • Before he went to bed.
  • Understand this:
  • Do the sentences above make sense?
  •  Because John was sick yesterday – (what happened ?)
  • After he got home – (then what happened?)
  • Before he went to bed – (what happened ?)


  • These sentences do not include a complete thought.  Therefore, they cannot stand alone.
  • They are not independent clauses. They are dependent clauses.
6
Dependent Clauses
  • Because John was sick yesterday. After he got home. Before he went to bed.
  • Now look again:  There is a subject and a verb, but not a complete thought. Why?


  • Notice the words at the beginning of each clause: because, after, before. These words are called subordinating conjunctions.
  • When a subordinating conjunction begins a clause, it is called a dependent clause or a subordinate clause.


  • All three of the above sentences begin with subordinating conjunctions, so they are all dependent clauses.
7
Independent & Dependent Clauses
  • Example:
  • Yesterday, John came to class without doing his homework because he was sick. He rarely misses a homework assignment. Yesterday, after he got home, he felt sick to his stomach. He took some medicine before he went to bed.


  • Understand this:
  • Some additional information is necessary to make an incomplete thought complete.
  • This additional information comes in the form of independent clauses.
  • Each dependent clause must connect to an independent clause. It cannot function alone.
8
Joining an independent and a dependent clause
  • Read the following:
  • John was sick yesterday. After he got home, he had stomach cramps. Although he lay down for a while, he did not get better. He had to take some medicine before he went to bed.


  • Understand this:


  • When a dependent clause begins a sentence, you need a comma before the independent clause.  ( i.e. – ‘After he got home , he had stomach cramps.)


  • When an independent clause begins a sentence, no comma is necessary before the dependent clause. (i.e. – ‘He had to take some medicine before he went to bed. )
9
Joining Two Independent Clauses
  • In the earlier slides, you learned that a comma can join a dependent clause with an independent clause. That is fine!
  • Now, read this:
  • It rained hard last night, the roads were flooded by the unexpected rain.


  • See what happened:
  • The two clauses are joined by a comma.
  • Neither of them is a dependent clause. Each one is an independent clause.
  • This is a mistake. The mistake is called a comma splice!
10
Comma Splice!
  • When only a comma joins two independent clauses, it is a comma splice.
  • The following sentences are examples of comma splices. Read carefully to see how only a comma (,) joins two independent clauses.
  • I was hungry, I looked for an In-N-Out.
  • The clouds promised rain, it did not rain.
  • Many students are from foreign countries, they are here to study American culture.


  • What did you notice about these sentences?
  • Each sentence has 2 subjects (underlined) and two verbs (bold).
  • Each clause has a complete thought.
  • The clauses are joined only by a comma.


  • Therefore, they are comma splices.
11
Correcting Comma Splices
  • Comma splices need to be corrected.
  • Example:
  • College of the Canyons offers many interesting courses, these courses attract students from near and far.


  • Let’s separate the sentence into two clauses to see if they can stand alone.  In other words, are they independent clauses?


  • College of the Canyons offers many interesting courses.
  • These courses attract students from near and far.


  • Each clause is independent.  Therefore, the sentence is a comma splice that must be corrected.
12
Correct a Comma Splice with a Period (.)
  • College of the Canyons offers many interesting courses. These courses attract students from near and far.
  • We have created two sentences from the original comma splice.
  • The comma splice is corrected!


  • Let’s take another example:
  • People graduate from college with high hopes, they try to get good jobs after graduation.
  • Correct this comma splice by removing the comma and separating the independent clauses by a period.


  • Correction:
  • People graduate from college with high hopes.  They try to get good jobs after graduation.
13
Correct a Comma Splice with a Semicolon (;)
  • Let’s use with the same comma splice and correct it using a semicolon.
  • Example:
  • College of the Canyons offers many interesting courses  ;  these courses attract students from near and far.


  • You may remove the comma and place a semicolon in its place.
  • Understand this:
  • When you separate two independent clauses with a period (.), the period must be followed by a capital letter.


  • When you separate  two independent clauses by a semicolon (;), the semicolon must be followed by a lower case letter.
14
Coordinating Conjunctions
  • What are coordinating conjunctions?
  • They are used to connect grammatically equal elements.    The coordinating conjunctions are:
  • For Used to show a cause
  • And Used for similar ideas
  • Nor Used to show a choice
  • But Used for contrasting ideas
  • Or Used to show a choice
  • Yet Used for contrasting ideas
  • So Used to show a result


  • You can remember the coordinating conjunctions with the acronym FANBOYS.  Use coordinating conjunctions to help you correct comma splices.
15
Correct a Comma Splice with a Coordinating Conjunction
  • Example:
  • College of the Canyons offers many interesting courses, and these courses attract students from near and far.



  • In the example, the independent clauses are joined by a comma and the word “and.”
  • The comma splice is corrected by adding a coordinating conjunction.
16
Correct with Coordinating Conjunctions
  • Here is another example:
  • In California, the summer is pleasant, many tourists visit during the warm weather.
  • In California, the summer is pleasant , so many tourists visit during the warm weather.


  • The coordinating conjunction “so” corrects the comma splice.
  • Remember that there is a comma before coordinating conjunctions.
17
Subordinating Conjunctions
  • What are subordinating conjunctions?
  • They are used to introduce a subordinate clause and show how the two clauses relate to one another.


  • The most common subordinating conjunctions are:
  • after, although, as, as if, because, before, if, in order that, once, since, so that, than, that, though, unless, until, when, where, whether, while


  • Use subordinating conjunctions to help you correct comma splices.
18
Correct with Subordinating Conjunctions
  • Example:
  • Deborah checked all the doors and windows of her house, she left for a long vacation.
  • These are independent clauses that are joined by a comma. Therefore, the example is a comma splice.
  • Corrected:
  • Deborah checked all the doors and windows of her house before she left for a long vacation.
  • Before Deborah left for a long vacation, she checked all the doors and windows of her house.
  • Understand this:
  • The above sentences are joined by “before” instead of a comma.
  • “Before” is a subordinating conjunction.
  • Subordinating conjunctions are at the beginning of dependent clauses.
19
Correct with Subordinating Conjunctions
  • Example:
  • Deborah checks all the doors and windows of her house whenever she leaves for a long vacation.
  • Understand this:
  • When a dependent clause occurs after an independent clause, there is no comma.


  • Alternate Example:
  • Whenever Deborah leaves for a long vacation, she checks all the doors and windows of her house .
  • Understand this:
  • When a dependent clause occurs before an independent clause,  use a comma before the independent clause.
20
Conjunctive Adverbs
  • What are conjunctive adverbs?
  • They are used to connect independent clauses and show how the clauses relate to one another.


  • The most common conjunctive adverbs are:
  • finally, furthermore, however, moreover, nevertheless, similarly, then, therefore, thus


  • Conjunctive adverbs can be used to correct comma splices.
21
Correct with Conjunctive Adverbs
  • Example:
  • My sister makes delicious mushroom omelets, she hates eating eggs and mushrooms.
  • These are independent clauses that are joined by a comma. Therefore, the example is a comma splice.


  • Corrected:
  • My sister makes delicious mushroom omelets; however, she hates eating eggs and mushrooms.
  • To correct the sentence, add the conjunctive adverb “however.”  Notice that you must also add a semicolon before “however” and a comma after it.
22
Correct with Conjunctive Adverbs
  • Let’s look at the same example again:
  • My sister makes delicious mushroom omelets, she hates eating eggs and mushrooms.


  • We can also correct the comma splice by making two separate sentences.


  • Corrected:
  • My sister makes delicious mushroom omelets.  However, she hates eating eggs and mushrooms.
  • You can begin the second sentence with the conjunctive adverb “however.”  Notice that you must follow “however” with a comma.
