Selecting and Analyzing Literary Quotes
2
Agenda
  • This workshop will cover:
  • How to select a literary quote to analyze in an essay
  • How to summarize a literary quote
  • How to respond to a literary quote
  • How to analyze a literary quote based on
    • What it means
    • What it does
3
Purpose
  • When writing a literary analysis paper, quotes from the literary text become a key element of your evidence.
  • Without quoting the literature, you will not be able to prove your point convincingly.
  • Furthermore, analyzing literary quotes well will help you learn how to read carefully and think critically.  Both of these skills are important in the working world.  Just imagine signing a contract without reading it closely and thinking about each of the requirements critically—you could get yourself in a lot of trouble!
4
Requirements for Selecting Quotes
  • Quotes in literary essays meet the following requirements:
  • They are relevant.
  • They are economical.
  • They add value to your paper.
  • They have good potential for analysis.
5
Relevance
  • Be sure to select a quote that is relevant to the point you’re trying to prove.


  • Example:  You are writing a paragraph that argues that Miss Emily Grierson of “A Rose for Emily” is a victim of her father’s patriarchal control.
  • Irrelevant:  “Already we knew that there was one room in that region above stairs which no one had seen in forty years, and which would have to be forced” (1381).
  • Relevant:  “We had long thought of them as a tableau, Miss Emily a slender figure in white in the background, her father a spraddled silhouette in the foreground, his back to her and clutching a horsewhip” (1378).
6
Economy
  • Quote economically; in other words, use only the parts of the quote that you intend to analyze.  This may mean using a single word or short phrase rather than an entire sentence (or group of sentences).


  • Example:  You are writing about the implication of necrophilia in “A Rose for Emily.”
  • Wasteful:  Quoting the entire sentence, “One of us lifted something from it, and leaning forward, that faint and invisible dust dry and acrid in the nostrils, we saw a long strand of iron-gray hair” (1382).
  • Economical:  Quoting the key phrase, “a long strand of iron-gray hair” (1382).
7
Add Value
  • A quote adds value to your analysis.  Don’t waste that value by including a quote to establish a plot point (in other words, showing that an event occurred).  When providing background information, consider summarizing rather than quoting.
  • Example:  You mention that Miss Emily Grierson of “A Rose for Emily” ages over the course of the story.  You do not have to use this quote just to prove that she ages:  “When we next saw Miss Emily, she had grown fat and her hair was turning gray” (1380).
  • However, if you plan to do a close analysis of Emily’s aging process, you may want to use the quote.
8
Potential for Analysis
  • A strong quote has good potential for analysis.  In other words, there are elements within the quote that you (the writer) can break down.  The quote may also include a literary device (like a symbol or metaphor) that you can analyze.


  • Little Potential for Analysis:  “She fitted up a studio in one of the downstairs rooms” (Faulkner 1380).  This quote explains what Miss Emily does; it informs readers about the plot, but doesn’t give writers much to analyze.
  • Good Potential for Analysis:  “Only Miss Emily’s house was left, lifting its stubborn and coquettish decay above the cotton wagons and the gasoline pumps—an eyesore among eyesores” (Faulkner 1376).  The quote has good potential because it personifies Miss Emily’s house.  Further, writers can discuss the similarities between the house and Miss Emily herself.
9
Let’s combine the requirements all in one quote!
  • Remember, we’re looking for a quote that is relevant, economical, adds value, and has potential for analysis.


  • If you are writing about the theme of decay in “A Rose for Emily,” you might use the following phrase about Miss Emily’s house:  “lifting its stubborn and coquettish decay” (1376).
  • Relevance:  The quote directly addresses the idea of decay
  • Economy:  The writer has selected a phrase rather than an entire sentence
  • Adds Value:  The quote goes beyond merely addressing the idea of decay to describe the particular type of decay exemplified by Miss Emily’s house.
  • Potential for Analysis:  The quote includes a personification and allows the writer to draw a connection between Miss Emily and her home.
10
Summarizing a Literary Quote:
What does it say?
  • Before analyzing the quote, make sure you can explain what the quote says.
  • Try restating the quote in your own words.
  • In your paper, include some background information from the scene.  This will help readers place the quote in context.
11
Example 1:  Summarizing a Quote
  • Quote from Lorraine Hansberry’s A Raisin in the Sun:  “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream.  His woman say: Eat your eggs” (Hansberry 1568).
  • Summary:  Walter Younger shows his frustrations over his wife’s response to his ambitions, i.e. her focus on the mundane.
  • Students can write the summary to make sure they understand the quote.
  • Background Information:  In this scene, Walter Younger tries to convince his wife Ruth to support his idea of opening a liquor store.  More specifically, he wants Ruth to convince his mother to finance the store.
  • Students should include some background information in their paper to help introduce the quote.
12
Example 2:  Summarizing a Quote
  • Quote from Sandra Cisneros’s “Mericans”:  “There are so many prayers and promises and thanks-be-to-God to be given in the name of the husband and the sons and the only daughter who never attend mass.  It doesn’t matter.  Like La Virgen de Guadalupe, the awful grandmother intercedes on their behalf” (Cisneros 2094).
  • Summary:  Although the grandmother’s family does not attend church, she prays on their behalf.
  • Background Information:  “Mericans” by Sandra Cisneros is the story of three grandchildren (Micaela, Junior, Keeks) who are waiting for their grandmother outside of church.
13
Responding to a Literary Quote:
What is your reaction?
  • Consider your response to literary quotes.  It may help you determine what you’d like to write about in your paper.  The response may also guide you through your analysis of the quote.
  • Consider the following questions:
    • What is your reaction to the quote?
    • Do you have an emotional response?
    • Does the quote shock or surprise you?
    • Does it remind you of a situation you experienced before?  Or something you learned in another class?
  • Write your response without worrying about being correct.  You can use the response to help you generate ideas, but don’t copy it directly into the final draft of an essay.
14
Example 1:  Responding to a Quote
  • Quote:  “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream.  His woman say: Eat your eggs” (Hansberry 1568).
  • Sample Response:  I feel bad for Walter because I want him to be able to realize his dream.  But I don’t like how he uses the term “man” and “woman” here as if all men and women are in the same situation as he and his wife.  I also don’t like how he uses the phrase “his woman,” as if the woman is the property of the man.
  • Response Explanation:  The response is the student’s initial reaction to the quote.  However, it could lead the student to an essay topic discussing marital relations in A Raisin in the Sun.  As you can see, the language is very casual, so the student wouldn’t want to use these exact words in his/her essay, even though s/he might use some of the ideas.
15
Example 2:  Responding to a Quote
  • Quote:  “There are so many prayers and promises and thanks-be-to-God to be given in the name of the husband and the sons and the only daughter who never attend mass.  It doesn’t matter.  Like La Virgen de Guadalupe, the awful grandmother intercedes on their behalf” (Cisneros 2094).
  • Response:  The poor grandmother!  It doesn’t sound like she’s awful; the rest of the story doesn’t give any evidence that she’s awful.  I also notice some grammar issues.  The first sentence uses “and” over and over…I think that’s a literary device.  I’ll have to look that up.  And, “it” in the third sentence has an unclear antecedent—I don’t know what it refers to.
16
Analyzing a Literary Quote:
What does it mean?
  • Analysis involves looking closely at a literary quote to figure out what that quote means.  This requires students to go beyond summarizing and responding to quotes (although a response is usually a good first step to an analysis).
  • When analyzing, you can examine:
  • Diction (word choice)
  • Imagery
  • Symbolism
  • Character(s), including the narrator
  • Tone
  • Other literary devices (simile, metaphor, alliteration, etc.)
  • Any assumptions made in the text
  • The implications of the text’s (or character’s) assertions
  • The use of grammar or syntax

Example 1:  Analyzing a Quote for Meaning
  • Quote:  “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream.  His woman say: Eat your eggs” (Hansberry 1568).
  • Analysis:
  • Diction:  Almost all the words are monosyllabic (one syllable).
  • Diction:  Walter uses the phrase “man” and “woman” to make the situation more generic rather than using “I” and “you,” which is personal.
  • Tone:  Frustrated
  • Grammar:  Use of the possessive “his woman”
  • Implications:  Walter interprets Ruth’s change of subject (“eat your eggs”) to mean that she doesn’t support his dream.
19
Example 2:  Analyzing a Quote for Meaning
  • Quote:  “There are so many prayers and promises and thanks-be-to-God to be given in the name of the husband and the sons and the only daughter who never attend mass.  It doesn’t matter.  Like La Virgen de Guadalupe, the awful grandmother intercedes on their behalf” (Cisneros 2094).
  • Analysis:
  • Polysyndeton (the use of several conjunctions in close succession):  The first sentence uses “and” several times, giving it a fast pace.
  • Allusion:  The quote alludes to La Virgen de Guadalupe, an incarnation of the Virgin Mary beloved in Mexico.  In addition, the quote uses a simile to compare La Virgen to the grandmother.
  • Syntax:  The quote contrasts a long, flowing sentence with a short, terse sentence.
  • Diction:  The quote refers to the grandmother as “awful,” showing the narrator’s feelings toward the grandmother.  However, the story doesn’t necessarily show that the grandmother is awful.  This leads readers to question the narrator’s reliability.

Analyzing a Literary Quote Rhetorically:
What does it do?
  • Rhetorical Analysis examines the way a speaker/narrator presents a message to an audience.  In literature, it will look at the function of a quote.  In other words, why did the speaker say the quote in this way at that point in the text?


  • When analyzing rhetorically, you can examine:
  • What is the function of the quote?
  • How does the quote contribute to the story?
  • How does it help us understand the story better (or not)?
  • Why is it placed where it is?
  • How would the story be different if that quote was not present?
  • If the speaker is making an argument, is that argument logically sound?
21
Example 1:  Analyzing a Quote Rhetorically
  • Quote:  “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream.  His woman say: Eat your eggs” (Hansberry 1568).
  • Analysis:
  • Function:  The quote shows that Walter’s frustration doesn’t just apply to his individual situation, but to relationships between men and women in general.
  • Placement:  The quote takes place near the beginning of the play, when Walter is arguing with his wife, Ruth.  It gives us insight into Walter’s character and his relationship with his wife.
  • Logic:  Walter is pointing out his wife’s distraction technique; he believes she is deliberately avoiding an argument about his liquor store idea.  He also makes a hasty generalization, assuming that his wife’s attitude applies to other marriages.
22
Example 2:  Analyzing a Quote Rhetorically
  • Quote:  “There are so many prayers and promises and thanks-be-to-God to be given in the name of the husband and the sons and the only daughter who never attend mass.  It doesn’t matter.  Like La Virgen de Guadalupe, the awful grandmother intercedes on their behalf” (Cisneros 2094).
  • Analysis:
  • Contribution:  The quote gives us insight into the grandmother’s character; however, the grandmother never has speaking lines.  The quote serves to set the stage for the children’s story, which makes up the main part of the text.
  • Argument:  The narrator makes a theological argument that “it doesn’t matter” that the grandmother’s family doesn’t attend church because the grandmother prays for them.  The argument may not be totally sincere, though.  The tone is sarcastic.
23
Bringing All the Threads Together
  • Once you have examined a quote from multiple angles, you can present your analysis to a reader.  You can incorporate any of the steps we’ve discussed.
  • Step 1:  Summarize.  Provide some background information to set the scene
  • Step 2:  Respond.  Use your response to guide your analysis
  • Step 3:  Analyze.  Use a literary or rhetorical analysis to explain the quote and use it to support your argument.
24
Example 1:  Bringing the Threads Together
  • Walter Younger shows his frustration with his wife and her response to his idea to open a liquor store.  He elevates his own idea when he says, “Man say to his woman:  I got me a dream” (Hansberry 1568).  The word “dream” has strong positive connotations, reminding readers of the lofty ideas they want to pursue.  Further, he connects his own situation with the plight of all men by using generalized terms like “man” and “woman.”  He is not describing just his own situation, but the situation of any man whose wife questions his dreams.
25
Example 2:  Bringing the Threads Together
  • When describing the grandmother’s religious practices, the narrator uses polysyndeton to imitate the sound of liturgy.  The narrator states, “there are so many prayers and promises and thanks-be-to-God to be given in the name of the husband and the sons and the only daughter who never attend mass” (Cisneros 2094).  The repeated use of the word “and” keeps the sentence flowing in an inexorable rhythm, reminding readers of a prayer or church sermon.  However, the narrator immediately contrasts the rhythm with her next sentence:  “it doesn’t matter” (Cisneros 2094).  The brief simple sentence diverges from the previous sentence’s flowing rhythm, forcing readers into an abrupt halt.  The narrator uses the syntax of these two sentences to make her theological argument: that the family’s absence from church is inconsequential because the grandmother prays for them all.
