USE #1:  CONTRACTIONS
4
WHAT IS A CONTRACTION?
  • A contraction is a word or words with letters omitted.
  • For example:
  • Do not = don’t
  • Is not = isn’t


  • The apostrophe takes the place of the missing letter(s).
5
COMMON CONTRACTIONS
  • Have not = haven’t
  • Was not = wasn’t
  • We have = we’ve
  • They are = they’re
  • Could have = could’ve
  • Cannot = can’t
  • I am = I’m
  • You will = you’ll
  • She will = she’ll
  • I have = I’ve
  • You are = you’re
  • He had = he’d
6
SAMPLE SENTENCES
  • I’m (I am) certain you’re (you are) going to have a good time at the concert.
  • She couldn’t (could not) have committed the robbery because she isn’t (is not) strong enough to break down the door.
7
CAUTIONS
  • Many instructors ask students to avoid using contractions in more formal writing assignments (like essays or research papers).  If you’re not sure what to do, talk to your professor.
  • There are a few commonly misused contractions.  Let’s look at them together!
8
YOU’RE AND YOUR
  • “You’re” is a contraction of “you are.”
  • Example:  You’re eligible for our honors program.


  • “Your” shows possession; it means, “belongs to you.”
  • Example:  I meant to return your book, but I forgot it at home.
9
THEY’RE AND THEIR
  • “They’re” is a contraction of “they are.”
  • Example:  A group of students are cheating on the Spanish final.  I know they’re going to get caught!


  • “Their” shows possession; it means, “belongs to them.”
  • Example:  After the band drove all around California for different gigs, they realized their van was about to break down.
10
IT’S AND ITS
  • “It’s” is a contraction of “it is.”
  • Example:  It’s going to be a hectic finals week!


  • “Its” shows possession; it means, “belonging to it.”
  • Example:  I dropped coffee on my laptop; now its hard drive is ruined.
11
WHO’S AND WHOSE
  • “Who’s” is a contraction of “who is.”
  • Example:  Who’s going to bring the guacamole to the party?


  • “Whose” shows possession; it asks, “who does it belong to?”
  • Example:  Whose phone is this?
12
COULD’VE AND COULD OF
  • “Could’ve” is a contraction of “could have.”
  • “Could of” is a common writing mistake.  Be sure to write “could’ve” instead.  (The same is true for “would’ve” and “would of.”)


  • Incorrect:  He could of won the championship if he’d worked harder.
  • Correct:  He could’ve (could have) won the championship if he’d worked harder.

USE #2:  TO SHOW POSSESSION
15
POSSESSION
  • We use an apostrophe for possession when we own something.
  • Example:  The giraffe’s baby could walk almost as soon as it was born.  (The baby belongs to the giraffe.)


  • We also use it for the characteristics that belong to a person or thing.
  • Example:  The dog’s fur grows thicker in the winter.  (The fur belongs to the dog.)
16
A POSSESSION TEST
  • One way to check if your sentence includes possession is to rephrase with the word “of.”


  • Examples:
  • The giraffe’s baby = the baby of the giraffe
  • The dog’s fur = the fur of the dog
17
EXAMPLES OF POSSESSION
  • The film’s credits
  • Belinda’s frustration
  • Your money’s worth
  • Last week’s weather
18
SAMPLE SENTENCES
  • Sunday’s game was a disaster.
  • Rosemary’s baby wouldn’t stop crying.
  • Sylvester’s mohawk was red and purple this week.
19
WHEN DO I USE ‘S?
  • Use ‘s for singular possession
  • One person / place / thing owns something


  • Examples:
  • The student’s papers are all due on Thursday.
  • The rhino’s horn is made of keratin.
20
SINGULAR WORDS ENDING IN -S
  • If a word ends in “-s” but is singular, use ‘s.


  • Examples:
  • I wanted to buy James’s painting, but it was too expensive.
  • Arkansas’s capital is Little Rock.
21
IRREGULAR PLURALS
  • If the plural of the word is irregular (does not end in “s”), use ‘s.


  • Examples:
  • The children’s art projects were beautifully displayed in the classroom.
  • The women’s jewelry line sold out first.
22
WHEN DO I USE S’?
  • Use s’ for plural possession
  • More than one person / place / thing owns something


  • Examples:
  • The three chefs’ menus were equally tempting and expensive.
  • This is the elephants’ natural habitat.
23
COMMON MISTAKE 1:  SIMPLE PLURALS
  • DO NOT use apostrophes to create simple plurals.
  • A simple plural shows there is more than one person / place / thing
  • There is no possession involved.


  • Incorrect:  He bought burrito’s for all of his friend’s.
  • Correct:  He bought burritos for all of his friends.
  • Note:  The sentence discusses multiple burritos and multiple friends; however, neither the burritos nor the friends own anything.
24
COMMON MISTAKE #2:  WORDS ENDING IN -Y
  • Simple Singular:  company
  • Example:  The company sells high-end kitchen appliances.
  • Simple Plural:  companies
  • Example:  The companies are developing a product together.
  • Singular Possessive:  company’s
  • Example:  The company’s profit margins are growing.
  • Plural Possessive:  companies’
  • Example:  The two companies’ competition is becoming fiercer every day.
