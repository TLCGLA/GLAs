What is revision?
  • Revision is the process of “re-seeing” the paper.
  • It is more than correcting grammar and formatting errors.
  • It involves careful consideration of your writing (possibly including writing new material or deleting material you’ve already written).
  • It helps you ensure that you have said what you wanted to say how you wanted to say it.
4
Why is it important to revise?
  • Revision allows you to enhance and develop your ideas to create the best possible paper.
  • This can not only improve your grades, but also help you learn to be a better writer and communicator.
5
Revision Takes Time
  • Plan ahead to give yourself plenty of time to do a good job revising.  You will do your best revising when you can look at your work with fresh eyes.

Creating New Content
8
Why Create New Content?
  • Come up with good (better?) new ideas
  • Luxury of choosing the best content
  • Not just writing until you hit the bottom of the last page
9
Strategy 1:  Choose Key Sentences
  • Select a key sentence from your assignment.
  • Then, take five minutes to freewrite on that sentence.  Write everything you can about that sentence without worrying about spelling or grammar.
  • When you are done, read through your freewrite to see if there is any material you can use in your essay.
  • Repeat this process with other key sentences.
  • Click here to see a sample
10
Strategy 2:  Key Terms
  • Read through your text and look for key terms.  These terms may be important for understanding the text, words with multiple meanings, difficult terms, or terms the average reader may be unfamiliar with.
  • Highlight or underline these words.
  • Then, research the terms.  What research could you include in your draft?  What information will the audience need to understand your ideas?
  • Click here to see a sample
11
Strategy 3:  Ask Questions
  • As you read through your text, ask yourself critical questions like “how?,” “why?,” and “so what?”
  • Then, work on answering those questions.
  • When you are done, reread your answers to determine what content you might use in your draft.

Evaluating and Cutting Content
14
General Tips
15
Use the Prompt and Thesis
  • The prompt and the thesis are “bouncers.”  They decide what material belongs in the paper and what material does not belong.
  • Cut material if…
    • It doesn’t answer the prompt
    • It doesn’t support the thesis
16
Practice Audience Awareness
  • The first draft is for you
  • The second draft is for your audience
    • What does the audience need to know to support your ideas?
    • What is the best way to present your argument to the audience?
    • What should you change or cut to appeal to your audience?
  • See a sample here
17
Check for Argument Consistency
  • All paragraphs should support the same overall argument, even if you have a counterargument
  • At the end of each paragraph, ask, “how does this support my thesis?”
  • If it does support the thesis, add your answer to the question
  • If it does not, cut the paragraph
18
Author’s Voice
  • You should be the chief voice in the essay
  • Check for over-quoting or over-citing
  • Add context or analysis
  • Consider cutting (or cutting down) some quotes



  • Note:  TLC offers workshops on APA 1 and 2, MLA 1 and 2, and Avoiding Plagiarism that can help you work with quotes effectively.
19
Remember to be ruthless!
  • Deleting material can be difficult; however, removing the material that doesn’t fit in your paper will make a stronger essay.

What should go first in an essay?
  • Any information the reader needs to understand your ideas, including:
  • Background information
  • Theoretical Foundations
  • Definitions
23
What should go last?
  • The end of the paper is your last chance to capture the reader’s attention.  It should include:
  • Strong, moving, powerful ideas
  • Broad implications:  “so what?”
  • End with a bang, not a whimper
24
Organizational Principles
  • Similarity:  keep similar ideas together
  • Emphasis:  end with the strongest idea
  • Chronological:  organized by time
  • Cause/Effect:  show why a phenomenon occurred and what other events it caused
  • Audience Familiarity:  organize topics by most to least familiar
  • Controversy:  organize topics by least to most controversial
  • Problem/Solution:  explain a problem then how to solve it
  • Block Structure:  organize by examples/case studies
  • Thematic Structure:  organize by main ideas
25
Revision Strategy:  Reverse Outline
  • Number your body paragraphs.
  • Rewrite each of your topic sentences on a new piece of paper using the numbers of your body paragraphs (you can also use index cards or sticky notes).
  • Reorganize the topic sentences; try several organizational strategies.
  • When you are happy with your new organization, reorder your body paragraphs to match the new order.
  • Review your thesis.  You may have to revise the thesis based on your new organization.

General Tips for Local Revision
  • Revise your paper out of order.  Look at each paragraph individually and in a different order than they appear in the essay.
  • Divide paragraphs into sentences and revise the sentences individually.
  • Read your work out loud.
  • Ask a tutor or trusted friend to read your work and identify any unclear or awkward sentences.

Improving Clarity
30
Why is clarity important?
  • Academic readers value clarity; in other words, they want to understand your ideas without having to reread your work.


  • By developing clarity in your writing, you are more likely to earn higher grades on your assignments, and you will be better prepared to meet the needs of readers in the business world.


  • We’ll look at a few strategies to improve clarity; see your handout for additional strategies.
31
Remove vague generalizations and be specific.

  • First draft:  Smoking bans on campus are good.


  • Revised:  Public health is improved significantly by enforcing smoking bans on college campuses.
32
Remove obscure words and phrases
  • First draft:  Excuse me sir, but could I please relocate myself closer to the board so I can achieve the optimum effect of your education?


  • Revised:  Could I please move to the front row?  Changing seats will help me learn better.
33
Limit word and sentence length
  • First draft:  We arrived at the agreement that we would have a meeting at 7:00 a.m. in the morning despite the fact that it is early.


  • Revised:  We agreed to meet at 7:00 a.m. even though it is early.
34
Avoid Clichés
  • Clichés are phrases that are overused
  • They have lost their meaning
  • Readers find them boring
  • Readers may ignore them
35
Cliché Examples
  • “The children are the future.”
  • “Actions speak louder than words.”
  • “You can achieve anything if you believe in your dreams.”
  • “X is a big issue in today’s society…”
36
Case Study:  The Attention Grabber
  • Students often employ clichés when writing an attention grabber.


  • The following are phrases that have been overused:
  • “Imagine a world”
  • “Since the beginning of time”
  • “Have you ever wondered…?”
  • “Did you know…?”
37
To Avoid Clichés
  • Consider the phrase’s actual meaning and try to express the idea in a specific, concrete fashion.


  • First draft:  The grass is always greener on the other side of the street.


  • Revised:  We have a misconception that other people have a better life and better possessions than we do.

Enhancing Writing Style
40
General Tips for Enhancing Style
  • One of the last stages of revision
  • Make sure you’re happy with your content and clarity
  • Add some stylistic flare—as long as it doesn’t disrupt clarity
41
Methods for Enhancing Style
  • Anecdote
  • Unconventional types of evidence
  • Detailed description
  • Be funny (where appropriate)
  • Interesting vocabulary
  • Literary devices
  • Avoid repeating words
42
Anecdote
  • Anecdote:  A short personal story with a point.
  • Example:
  • At eighteen, I moved out of my parents’ house, got a job, and began the project of being an independent adult—or so I thought.  I believed that having and using a credit card was the thing adults did.  I believed that a credit card would let me live a happy, comfortable life with all the stuff I could ever want.  I believed that credit was extra income, just like my paycheck.  I was wrong.
43
Detailed Description
  • Example:
  • It’s midnight in November.  I’m outside, shivering, freezing in the cold air.  I’m surrounded by people, but most of them are strangers.  They’ve packed us in tightly; even so, the line loops around the building.  I hear someone behind me discussing his plans—he wants to fight his way to the TVs.  He’ll have to get through me first.  It’s Black Friday, and the doors of Best Buy are about to slide open.  Someone is going to get trampled.
44
Interesting Vocabulary
  • Example 1:  Academic Words
  • Some children successfully inveigle their parents into granting their every desire.
  • Example 2:  Informal or Slang Words
  • Relationships among young people today have grown increasingly complicated; teen girls, for example, could discover that their former bff is now their frenemy.
