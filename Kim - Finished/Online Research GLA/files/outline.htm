PART 1: OVERVIEW OF ONLINE RESEARCH
5
Traditional Research
  • It used to be that doing research involved:
    • Going to a library
    • Using a card catalog to locate sources
    • Finding books or back issues of newspapers or magazines
    • Making photocopies of the sources you plan to use
6
Online Research
  • Today, most research involves:
    • Online databases
    • Internet searches
    • Electronic copies of sources
  • You may or may not need to physically go to the library
7
Advantages of Online Research
  • Online research can be convenient and quick.
  • It also provides a large quantity of diverse sources—you are not limited to the holdings of just one library.
  • If the website is reputable, it can provide good background information about your topic.
  • You can easily access more detailed, credible information about your topic through the library databases.
8
Disadvantages of Online Research
  • At the same time, the volume of information available can be overwhelming.
  • Not all of the information online is reliable, so evaluation of sources is vital.
  • It can be difficult to confirm that the author of a website is credible.
    • Example:  Most Wikipedia pages are open for the public to write and edit.  The writers of a page might be experts, but then again, they might not.

PART 2:
ONLINE SEARCH STRATEGIES
11
Section Introduction
  • This section will cover strategies that will help you with any online research project.  They apply to either web searches or searches using our online library resources.
12
Let the research lead your ideas
  • Key Concept:  Research first, then develop your argument.
  • In order to develop strong ideas, we must do research first.
  • That way, we know that the information (well-researched data) will support our ideas.  Be sure to research all sides of the issue (not just the side you agree with).
  • Avoid the frustration of looking for “the perfect quote” for your paper.
  • Instead, learn your topic well and build your ideas based on the data (not the other way around).
13
Developing Search Terms
  • Before you begin searching, it is important to develop a list of search terms.
    • After choosing the subject you want to research, find synonyms or related topics.
    • Do preliminary research on Google to ensure that you are using the correct terms.
  • Having multiple search terms will help you find the information you want.  If you have an incorrect term, you may not find the sources you need even though the information exists.
  • Be prepared to engage in some trial-and-error testing as you research.
14
How do I develop my search terms?
  • Brainstorm:  Make a list of all the words you can think of that relate to your topic.
  • Review class materials:  Reread assigned readings from class to locate the correct vocabulary terms.
  • Do some preliminary research on Google.  This will lead you to the correct vocabulary terms or help you find recent developments to your topic.  You might also discover an interesting new angle for the topic!
  • Stay engaged:  Be aware of any recent information about your topic, such as news items or scientific breakthroughs.

Narrowing a Topic
  • Examples:
    • Too broad:  politics
    • Narrowed:  effects of third party candidates on presidential elections



    • Too broad:  visual arts
    • Narrowed:  visual arts education in elementary school
  • Sometimes topics need to be narrowed (made more specific) before you can begin searching for sources
  • By narrowing a topic, you get more focused research that will be more helpful as you start writing
17
Sample Narrowed Topic
  • One way to narrow the topic is to make a mind map.  You can choose to focus on a specific subtopic in your assignment.

PART 3:
WEBSITE EVALUATION
20
Section Introduction
  • This section will provide strategies you can use when evaluating the quality and credibility of websites.


  • Since anyone can create a website and post any information they like, it is our job as readers to select reliable websites.  Unfortunately, if we use information from unreliable websites, it makes our own work look bad.


  • Overall, we want to make sure our websites are credible.  In other words, do they include trustworthy, reliable information.  Can we believe that website?
21
What do we look for when evaluating websites?
  • Author
  • Website/Sponsoring Organization
  • Date Updated
  • Accuracy of Information
  • Use of Sources
  • Bias
  • Sarcasm/Humor
22
Author
  • Be sure that the author of the website is a credible expert in his/her field.
  • The site may have a government, corporate or organizational author—in other words, no one individual is listed as the author.  Instead, a group (like a business) is the author.  It is acceptable to use sites with organizational authors as long as the organization is credible.
  • Be sure to read the “About Us” section on a website to learn more about the author’s credentials.
  • Avoid using sites that don’t list any author at all (either individual or organizational).  You must be able to verify that the author is credible!
23
Example:  Aluminum Foil Deflector Beanie
  • Let’s try to find information about the author of the Aluminum Foil Deflector Beanie website.  His name is Lyle Zapato.
24
Example:  Aluminum Foil Deflector Beanie
  • Our first step would be to look for an About Me or About Us link, which the home page of the website does not have.
  • Our next step is to search his name, Lyle Zapato, on Google.
  • When we search his name, we can see that the first hits go directly to his own website.  There is no outside information about Zapato and no way of confirming his expertise.
  • In this case, students should not use his website as a credible source to support their arguments.
25
Website / Sponsoring Organization
  • We should also examine the website or sponsoring organization that hosts the website.
  • This is very similar to looking up information about the author.
  • We have to ensure that the website / sponsoring organization is credible; in other words, they routinely provide reliable information to readers.
26
Date Updated
  • For most topics, students should find information that is updated frequently.  For example, if you are writing a paper about unrest in the Middle East, information from 1991 will be very different from information from 2003, which will also be very different from information from 2016.
  • Some topics, however, do not need recently updated information.  For example, if you want to read Shakespeare’s Sonnet 116, a site last updated 20 years ago should have the same text as a site updated yesterday.
  • For time-specific topics, be sure to check the website for a date that shows it was updated recently.  Dates are usually listed at the top or bottom of the site.
27
Accuracy of Information
  • When you use a website, be sure to double check that the site provides accurate information.
  • Look for other credible websites or sources that have the same information.
  • It is your responsibility to confirm that the website’s information is correct.  That way, you ensure that you use the best possible information in your paper.
28

31
Use of Sources
  • When reading a website’s information, check the sources they use.
  • This is similar to checking the accuracy of the website’s information.
  • Be sure to see who (if anyone) the website quotes, links to, or refers to.  Credible sites use credible sources to support their points.
32
Bias
  • Read sites carefully to see if they have any bias.  Are they trying to get readers to believe a certain viewpoint?  Buy a particular product?
  • If you suspect that the site is biased, check all of its credentials carefully.
  • Consider some of the following questions:
    • Who does the website represent?
    • Does it provide information from both sides of an issue?
    • Can you confirm the facts of the website on other sites?
33
Example:  A Sweet Surprise
  • Let’s look for bias on A Sweet Surprise, a site about high fructose corn syrup (HCFS) hosted by the Corn Refiners Association.
34
Example:  A Sweet Surprise
  • The website looks very professional (more so than the previous examples we’ve looked at).
  • We can see from the headlines that it claims to provide the truth about HCFS.
  • However, the site is run by an organization that makes its money from growing and selling corn.
  • As critical readers, it is our responsibility to fact check any information from the site that we plan to use.  We must make sure that the information can also be found through independent sources that do not stand to gain or lose business by selling HCFS.
35
Sarcasm / Humor
  • Look out!  The site you may want to use could be a joke.
  • Some websites are used as sources of humor or sarcasm.  Before using a website for school, make sure that it is presenting information sincerely and reliably.
  • One of the most prominent examples is The Onion, an online humor site designed to look like a news source.  Let’s take a look at it!
36
Example:  The Onion
  • Here’s the Onion’s homepage.  You’ll notice that some parts of the page look almost identical to mainstream news sources.  However, there are some clues that the site isn’t a serious news source.
37
Final Question
  • Every time you do online research, you should carefully evaluate the websites to make sure that the information is credible.


  • Be sure to ask yourself one final question:


  • Is the information from this website better than anything I could find in my assigned textbook / readings or at the library?


  • If your answer to the question is no, then you should use library or class resources instead.
  • Remember, the credibility of your sources is a reflection of your own credibility.  Show readers how trustworthy you are by using good sources!

PART 4:  LIBRARY RESOURCES
40
Campus Resources
  • One of the best resources for your online research is the college’s library
  • The library has eBooks available in its catalog
  • The library also has subscriptions to many online databases
  • These databases can be accessed on campus or from home with a username and password.  This information changes every semester; ask a TLC tutor for the current username and password.
41
eBooks
  • eBooks are books where some or all of the text can be accessed online
  • To find eBooks, go to “eBooks” on the library’s home page
  • Search for books by keyword, title, or author
  • Click on the title of the book
42
To Read the eBook
  • Option 1:  Click on eBook Full Text to have access to the entire book
  • Option 2:  Click on the Table of Contents to read a specific part of the book
43
Online Databases
  • Online databases are searchable collections of research material, usually article-length sources
  • Some online databases are general and include a wide variety of topics and sources while others are specific to a particular discipline
  • A big advantage of online databases is that the sources are already screened for reliability
44
Examples of Online Databases
  • Proquest Direct:  Citations, abstracts, and full-text articles and graphics from more than 10,000 magazines, journals and newspapers


  • Lexis Nexis: Cross-disciplinary database that includes full-text articles from over 2,000 academic journals.


  • Daily Life through History:  Cross-disciplinary resource that supports history, social studies, English, and language students; gives life to history and context to current events.


  • Consumer Health Complete:  Consumer-oriented content covering all areas of health and wellness from mainstream medicine to the many perspectives of complementary, holistic and integrated medicine.
45

46
Keyword Searches
  • Keyword searches are basically the kind of search you do on Google
  • Keywords are significant or descriptive words used as a reference point for finding other words or information.
  • Often, searches begin by entering keywords into a search engine or database.
  • The search engine or database locates all sources that contain that word (or words).
  • This is a broad search strategy.  It often retrieves too many results, not all of which are relevant.
47
Using Boolean Operators
  • Boolean operators are words that search engines and databases recognize as ways to limit or expand searches
  • Common Boolean operators include:
    • AND: finds results that include both terms
      • Example:  hiring AND ethics
      • You will  only get articles that include both of these terms
    • OR: finds results that include either term
      • Example:  hiring OR ethics
      • You will get all articles that include the term “hiring” and all articles that include the term “ethics”
    • NOT: excludes term from results
      • Example:  diet NOT Atkins
      • You will get articles that discuss diet, but not those that include the word Atkins
    • *: searches multiple versions of a term
      • Example:  advertis*
      • The database will search advertise, advertisement, advertising, etc.
48
Other Methods of Narrowing a Search
  • Full text:  the library has access to the entire article
  • Peer reviewed:  the article has been reviewed by experts in the field before publication
  • Source type:  you can select the specific type of source, such as Scholarly Journal, Trade Publication, or Magazine
  • Publication date:  you can choose a specific date range.  For most research projects, you want recent articles (published in the last 5-10 years)
49

50
Sample Search with Additional Terms
  • You have decided to focus specifically on the stigma surrounding a particular mental health issue:  depression
  • You alter your search to:  depression AND stigma
  • You keep the limiters from the previous search.
  • This time, you have 425 search results.


  • Next Steps:
  • Read about 100-200 article titles; select 10-15 that seem most relevant to your topic.
  • Read all 10-15 abstracts (summaries).
  • Select the articles that best relate to your topic.
  • Read those articles entirely.
  • Take thorough notes on those articles to help you write your research paper.
51
Helpful Features
  • Many databases provide helpful features that aid students in the research process, such as:
  • An abstract, which is a brief summary of the article you can read to see if the article will help you in your research
  • The option to email, export, or save the article for easy access later
  • A sample citation (Be sure to double-check the citation using a handbook; they are not always completely accurate.)
  • The option to see similar articles
