Time Management Overview:  How Much Time Do We Have?
  • Everyone has 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.
  • This makes a budget of 168 hours each week.
  • 168 hours may seem like a lot of time.  BUT…
    • If you sleep 8 hours a day (56 hours a week), you have 112 hours left
    • And, if you are taking 12 units (about 12 hours a week),  you have 100 hours left
    • And, if you are studying sufficiently for your classes (3 hours per unit or 36 hours), you have 64 hours left.
    • This leaves only 64 hours for everything else you have to do, including:
      • Eating
      • Going to work
      • Commuting
      • Exercising / Attending Athletic Practice
      • Showering and Personal Grooming
6
Why is time management important for college students?
  • College has a much less rigid schedule than high school; first time college students must adapt to increased responsibility in managing their time.
  • According to the University at Buffalo’s counseling office, about 25% of students become chronic procrastinators.
  • It almost always takes twice as long to complete a task as what we originally thought it would take.  This means chronic procrastinators will often run out of time or complete tasks poorly.
  • Poor time management leads to lower academic performance and increased risk of dropping out.

What is a goal?
  • Many students come to college with dreams of what they want to be or do in the future.
  • In order to make those dreams come true, we have to develop goals.
  • Goals are reality-based; they consider the real steps that we need to take in order to achieve our dreams.
10
How to Set Goals
  • Start with your dreams.  In your perfect world, what will you be or do in the future?
  • Then, work to refine your dreams to make them realistic goals.  Use the acronym FOCUS to help you.
    • Fit:  Your goal must fit your values, your character, and who you are as a person.  If you are uncomfortable with conflict, for example, you may not want to be a courtroom lawyer.
    • Ownership:  Your goal should be one that you pick for yourself.  It should make you excited about your future!
    • Concreteness:  You should be able to describe your goal in specific detail.  “To run faster” isn’t very concrete.  Instead, try:  “to run a five minute mile by June 1.”
    • Usefulness:  Goals should serve a useful purpose towards helping you achieve your dream.  If you hope to work in international business, for example, you should start learning another language now.
    • Stretch:  Select goals that stretch your current abilities.  Challenge yourself!

Distinguish Between Long and Short Term Goals
  • The goal you create using FOCUS is most likely your long term goal.  It will help you achieve your dreams.
  • You should also develop short term goals.  Short term goals are intermediate steps towards achieving your long term goals.
  • For example, if your long term goal is to own your own restaurant, you may have to meet some of the following short term goals:
    • Take cooking classes to learn how to cook well and safely
    • Gain experience working in restaurants or with catering companies
    • Network with restaurant owners
    • Take business classes to learn how to start and market your own business

15
Importance of Prioritizing
  • One key aspect of time management is developing priorities.
  • In other words, you must develop a system for deciding which tasks to complete in what order.
  • In order to prioritize well, we must:
    • Break large projects into smaller tasks
    • Determine how important each task is
    • Create realistic To Do lists with deadlines
16
Breaking Down Tasks
  • In the previous section, we looked at ways to break down long-term goals into short-term goals.
  • In this section, we’ll work on breaking down large projects into smaller tasks.
17
Major Projects
  • As a college student, you will be asked to complete major projects.
  • These projects will require you to spend a great deal of time and energy.
  • In order to complete the projects well, you should plan to break them up into smaller tasks.
  • This will allow you to spread work out over a long period of time.  You’ll be able to do a better job, be less stressed out, and avoid procrastination.

  • You need to develop a system to decide what tasks to complete in what order.

Urgent
  • Urgent tasks are those that need to be completed quickly.
  • They are often unexpected problems
  • Urgent tasks cause stress and make us feel like we are in constant crisis mode.
  • They can also be dictated by someone else’s agenda; for example, it might feel urgent to respond to a text message from a best friend, even if responding interferes with your agenda.
  • Ideally, we should manage our time to prevent tasks from becoming urgent.
24
Important
  • Important tasks are ones that link directly to our future goals.
  • It is vital to complete these tasks.
  • Often, it is also fulfilling to complete these tasks; in other words, we enjoy the important tasks.
  • Important tasks also include planning for the future.

Daily Lists

PART 3:  MULTITASKING
34
What is Multitasking?
  • When we multitask, we believe that we are completing multiple tasks simultaneously.
  • We evolved to multitask during hunter/gatherer days so early humans could (for example) stalk prey and keep their eyes on their group of hunters.
  • Many students believe that multitasking is a great way to work (or, it is their preferred way to work).
35
Studies on Multitasking
  • In an experiment involving 62 undergraduate students taking a principles of accounting course, half of the cohort was allowed to text during a lecture and half had their phones turned off. After the lecture both groups took the same quiz and the students who did not text scored significantly higher on the quiz.


  • Students taking a general psychology course were asked to read a 3,828 word passage on a computer. One group used instant messaging before they started reading, another group used instant messaging while they were reading and a third group read without instant messaging. The group that used instant messaging while they read took between 22 and 59% longer to read the passage than students in the other two groups.  That was after the time spent instant messaging was subtracted from the reading times.
36
Effects of Multitasking
  • Today’s students are highly likely to multitask, especially through technology
  • In fact, one large survey found that 80 percent of college students admit to texting during class; 15 percent say they send 11 or more texts in a single class period.
  • The problem is that students overestimate their ability to pay attention to more than one thing at once.  They believe they can listen to their professor and send a text message, but the vast majority can’t do both effectively.
  • When students multitask, they generally experience the following effects:
    • Assignments take longer
    • The mental fatigue of starting and stopping a task leads to more mistakes
    • Students are less likely to remember the material well.
    • Students are more likely to get lower grades.
37
Switching Costs
  • What is the problem with multitasking?
  • For one, we don’t actually do two or more complex tasks at the same time.  Rather, we switch rapidly back and forth between those tasks.
  • When we switch tasks, we experience two different switch costs.
    • We take time to adjust to the new mental task
    • We carry over aspects of the previous mental task
  • In other words, we lose time whenever we switch between tasks.  (The more complex the task, the more time we lose.)
  • The amount of time we lose may seem small, but it adds up quickly (sometimes as much as 40% of a person’s productive time).
38
Overcoming Multitasking Habits
  • Schedule tech breaks or other breaks for yourself
  • Turn off electronics when working
  • Avoid opening multiple tabs on the computer
  • Set aside specific times for checking email and other online accounts
  • Find methods for avoiding interruptions (like working alone in the library)
  • Reset your natural rhythm during the day by taking a short walk outside or opening a window
  • When you are interrupted by something important, resist the urge to multitask.  Before switching to a new task, make note of your place in your current task so you can pick it up again easily

PART 4:  PROCRASTINATION
41
What is procrastination?
  • Researchers define procrastination as:  “needlessly delaying tasks to the point of experiencing subjective discomfort.”
  • In other words, procrastination means we put off tasks for no good reason; in addition, putting off the task makes us uncomfortable.
  • 70% of college students admit to procrastinating on their assignments.
  • The problem is that procrastination leads students to make mistakes or to not meet their full potential on assignments.
  • Keep in mind that college students often have multiple assignments due at once.  (Just think of finals week!)  Procrastinating on multiple assignments can be disastrous.
42
Why do people procrastinate?
  • Chronic procrastinators have made procrastination a lifestyle that affects all aspects of their lives.  They don’t only procrastinate on school work—they pay bills or file income tax returns late.
  • In the US, we don’t treat procrastination as a national problem.  We don’t really take it seriously, and we don’t call people on their excuses (even if we don’t believe them).
  • Procrastinators may lie to themselves.  They’ll say, for example, “I work best under pressure.”  In fact, most people don’t work best under pressure and most major assignments require time, thought, and effort in order to do well.  Unfortunately, procrastinators are squandering one of their important resources:  time.
  • Procrastinators actively look for distractions (like checking email).
  • Some procrastinators fear failure (or fear success), and procrastinate to avoid facing the consequences of completing their work.
43
The Planning Fallacy
  • Some people procrastinate because they fall into the planning fallacy, which is:
  • They underestimate how long it will take to complete a task.
  • They do not take into account how long it has taken to complete a similar task in the past.
  • They plan for everything to go smoothly.  In other words, they don’t save any time for unforeseen circumstances (like a computer crashing or changing a flat tire).
44
How do you know if you are procrastinating?
  • You might be procrastinating if…
  • You spend most of your time on low priority tasks
  • You start a high priority tasks and stop almost immediately for a distraction (like getting a cup of coffee)
  • You leave an item on your To Do list for a long time, even though you know it’s important.
  • You regularly agree to do unimportant tasks that others give you rather than completing your own important tasks.
  • You wait for the right mood or the right time to complete a task.

Addressing Procrastination:  Breaking Bad Habits
  • One way to begin addressing procrastination is to work on breaking bad habits.
  • Breaking a bad habit is difficult because a habit has become part of our cognitive script; in other words, we engage in habits unconsciously and automatically.
  • Breaking bad habits takes time; on average, you need to engage in an alternative behavior for 66 days for it to become a habit.
47
Strategies for Breaking Bad Habits
  • Create a plan.  Set a clear, measurable goal for yourself that you can divide into smaller tasks.  The tasks should have deadlines that you can incorporate onto your calendar or To Do lists.
  • Develop Self-Discipline and Self-Awareness.  Watch yourself constantly to avoid slip-ups in breaking the bad habit.  Remind yourself why you want to break that habit in the first place.
  • Choose the right approach.  Some people prefer to quit a behavior all at once.  Others prefer to limit the behavior slowly over time.  Use the method that works best for you.
  • Put obstacles in place.  Make it more difficult to engage in the bad habit.  For example, you can turn the wifi off on your computer while working on projects for school.
48
Strategies to Avoid Procrastination
  • We just looked at some general information to help us avoid bad habits.
  • Now, we’ll look at some strategies to help us avoid procrastination specifically.
49
Avoiding Procrastination
  • Keep track of your excuses.  Write down your excuses consistently; this will help you recognize them for what they are.
  • Break down.  Break your project into smaller tasks.  Facing the smaller tasks is less overwhelming than trying to do a whole project all at once.
  • Trick yourself.  If you feel like procrastinating, trick yourself by starting a small part of a larger project.  Just getting started on the project will put you into the mindset for working.
  • Resolve issues.  If there is something that’s making it difficult for you to concentrate (like your desk is messy), take care of it.  That way, you can focus on your work.
50
Avoiding Procrastination
  • Get real.  Set realistic (rather than optimistic) goals for yourself.  Start keeping track of how long tasks take you.  In the future, you’ll be better informed of the actual amount of time you need to complete tasks.
  • Think positively.  Don’t imagine everything that can go wrong for your project.  Instead, think about getting the project completed well and on time.
  • Make a deal with yourself.  Clearly articulate what you hope to accomplish that day—even if it is only fifteen minutes of work to get started on a project.
51
Avoiding Procrastination
  • Overcome fear.  We often procrastinate because we’re afraid of failing.  Face your fears and work on the task anyway (even if it is scary).
  • Get tough.  Force yourself to focus on the tasks that are high priorities, even if they aren’t fun.
  • Reward yourself.  Give yourself reasonable rewards (like going to the gym or texting your best friend) for completing tasks.    Celebrate your accomplishments to help you build up motivation for future projects.
