What is Prewriting?
  • Prewriting is the first stage of the Writing Process.  In this stage, writers develop ideas, gather research, and organize their points.


  • Prewriting can help students overcome Writer’s Block by giving them a place to begin.  Also, by thinking through ideas in advance, students write more focused, organized drafts.


  • This workshop will cover many prewriting strategies; some strategies work better than others in certain situations and f0r certain writers.


  • Think of this workshop as an opportunity to build your toolkit of strategies that you can use.

Part 1:  Overcoming Writer’s Block
5
Writer’s Block
  • Writer’s Block occurs when writers cannot think of what to write.  If you feel fear and anxiety when faced with a writing project, then you are probably experiencing writer’s block.
6
Causes of Writer’s Block
  • Low Confidence
  • New Type of Writing
  • Confused by the Prompt
  • Boring Topic
  • Lost Momentum
  • Running Out of Time
7
Low Confidence
  • Students may be unsure of their writing skills and anticipate low grades.  These negative feelings can block their thinking and motivation.
8
New Type of Writing
  • Students may experience writer’s block when doing a new type of writing, such as the first time they write a research paper.
9
Confused by the Writing Prompt
  • After going over the prompt in class and reviewing it on your own, you may still be confused by the assignment.  That confusion can make it difficult to begin writing.
10
Boring Topic
  • Sometimes, you may be assigned a topic you consider boring. This can make it difficult to motivate yourself to write.
11
Lost Momentum
  • You may have started your paper, but find yourself running out of things to say.
12
Running Out of Time
  • When you are running out of time on an assignment, you may feel a lot of pressure to finish.  This pressure can prevent you from knowing what to write about next.
13
Develop Strategies
  • Just as there are many reasons students experience writer’s block, there are many strategies to help overcome it.  We’ve gone through some strategies in this section of the presentation; we will be looking at others in upcoming sections.
14
Activity 1
  • On your worksheet, check off the causes of writer’s block you have experienced.
  • Then, briefly explain what you think is the most helpful strategy for overcoming writer’s block.

Part 2:  Narrowing a Topic
16
Narrow a Topic
  • Many college professors prefer for students to write about a narrow, specific topic.  This is how many professional academics write; for example, it would be unusual to find an academic journal article about discrimination as a whole.
  • Instead, you might find an article about gender discrimination among male pediatric nurses in Los Angeles.  This topic is much narrower, and will provide detailed, specific information to readers.
17
Benefits of Narrowing a Topic
  • Students can be specific.  With a narrow topic, students can include details that will make their work more convincing and interesting.
  • Students can go in depth.  Most college instructors would prefer their students to select a narrow topic and provide a deep, insightful analysis of that topic.  If you pick a topic that is too broad, you will not be able to discuss it with enough depth.
  • Papers will be more interesting and convincing.  Readers prefer to read work that is detailed, and will be more likely to be convinced by specific, strong evidence.
18
But won’t I run out of things to write?
  • Nope!  Students are more likely to run out of things to write if their topic is too broad.  Often times, with a broad topic, students won’t know where they can go into detail, so they don’t go into detail anywhere.  The papers can become vague, which will usually not convince readers to agree with the students’ ideas.


  • With a narrow topic, you have space to really dig in to the material.  You can include high quality research and, depending on the assignment, really interesting examples.
19
How do I narrow a topic?
  • Your instructor may or may not assign a topic to you.  If s/he does not assign a topic, select a topic that is interesting to you (maybe something that relates to your future career).
  • If s/he does assign a topic, begin with that topic.
  • Then, brainstorm potential subtopics.
  • Let’s look at the example on the next slide!

23
Topic Discussion
  • As we could see on the previous slide, even the narrow topic of Masculine Body Image could be broken up into a wide variety of topics.
  • Each of those topics could be developed into a detailed, interesting paper.
  • For example, a student writing about Masculine Body Image could write an entire paper on commercials for men’s grooming products (like Old Spice, Axe, and Gillette).
  • The narrow focus required for a paper on commercials for men’s grooming products will encourage the student to write a detailed paper that stays on topic.
  • If the student picked a larger topic, like trying to cover all of the topics in Masculine Body Image, they would spread themselves too thin and have little to say about any one topic.

Part 3:  Invention Strategies
26
What are invention strategies?
  • Invention Strategies are different exercises that writers complete to help develop their ideas for a writing project.  We will look at a variety of strategies; as a writer, you get to decide which strategy you prefer in which situation!


  • Narrowing a topic and invention strategies are closely related.  You may find yourself moving back and forth between the two.  This is completely normal, and should help you develop a strong idea for a paper.
27
Why Use Invention Strategies?

  • You can develop ideas you didn’t know you had
  • You can connect with, and evaluate, your own knowledge, past experiences, and interests
  • You can clarify your belief and attitudes
  • You can identify your purpose for writing
  • You can develop a sense of your audience
  • You can select and organize ideas to use in your paper
28
Invention Strategies
  • There are many different invention strategies to help writers develop their ideas.  For this workshop, we’ll focus specifically on:
  • Asking Questions
  • Freewriting
  • Listing
  • Talking it Out
  • Cubing
29
Asking Questions
  • After receiving your essay topic, ask yourself questions about that topic.  This can help you narrow the topic.  It can also help you find out what you don’t know about the topic, which will show you what you need to research.
30
Sample: 
Asking Questions
  • Topic:  Drought
  • What causes droughts?
  • How long do droughts last?
  • Are we in danger of running out of water?
  • What other problems do droughts cause?
  • How can we conserve water?
  • How can corporations, colleges, and other large organizations conserve water?
  • What happens to people who waste water?  Are they punished?
31
Freewriting
  • Freewriting asks students to write freely about their topic.  The key to completing a freewrite is to keep your hands writing.
  • Do not worry about spelling, grammar, or staying on topic.  If you have to write “blah blah blah,” do so—just keep writing!
  • Assign a set time limit for completing a freewrite.  Usually, 10-15 minutes is a good time limit.  You may want to complete multiple freewrites to explore the issue from different angles.
32
Sample Freewrite
  • Topic:  Domestic Violence
  • Okay, what do I think about domestic violence?  It’s a major problem these days, I know that, but maybe it ’s just because we talk about it more?     I have a friend (we met in high school)  . . . and when we got back in touch after several years, she told me her husband was physically and emotionally abusive.  I’ve heard that abusers try to isolate their victims, so I’m glad she reached out to me.  Why do they do it?  Power?  The need to control other people?  How can domestic violence be prevented?  What are the solutions?  Where can victims get help?  I think we need more education on this topic.
33
Reviewing the Freewrite
  • Once you have finished your freewrite, review it to find strong ideas that you can use in your paper.
  • These ideas don’t have to be perfectly formed—instead, look for ideas with potential.
  • When you reread your freewrite, underline the best ideas.
34
Listing
  • Listing can help you discover ideas for your paper.  It can also help you determine which are main points and which are subtopics.


  • Once you receive your topic, make a list of all possible subtopics.  As you write, do not immediately reject any idea—you never know where that idea might lead you.
35
Sample List
  • Topic:  Food Issues
  • Vegetarianism
  • Veganism
  • World Hunger
  • Organic food
  • Local food
  • Sustainable Fishing
  • Farmer’s Markets
  • Genetically-Modified Organisms
  • Treatment of Farm Animals
  • Slaughter of Animals for Meat
  • Food-Borne Disease
  • Fast Food
  • Sweeteners
  • Fad Diets
36
Review Your List
  • One key benefit of a list is that it is easy to reorganize.  Once your list is complete, you can group similar topics together.
  • The list form makes it easier to decide which ideas you’d like to keep, and which you’d like to discard.
37
Talking it Out
  • Sometimes, it helps students to discuss their ideas before writing about them.  For this strategy, plan to talk for at least three minutes.
  • Your partner will listen to your ideas and provide feedback, such as:
  • Asking you for more information or examples
  • Clarifying your ideas
  • Introducing different opinions
  • Not only will this exercise help you develop ideas, you’ll probably gain more awareness of opposing ideas!
38
Talking It Out:
Key Phrases
  • After listening to a student talk through his/her ideas, the student’s partner might use some of the following phrases to help the student refine his/her ideas:
  • I’m not sure what you mean by . . . Can you clarify that thought?
  • That sounds interesting.  Tell me more!
  • Can you give me some examples?
  • But aren’t you assuming . . . ?
  • What if someone said the opposite?  How would you respond?
39
Cubing
  • Cubing is an invention strategy that asks students to examine their topic from multiple angles.  Cubing is a more advanced strategy that works best if a student already has a fairly clear idea of what s/he will write about.  It often works best to use cubing after completing other prewriting strategies.


  • We’ll look at the process for cubing on the next slide.
40
Cubing Process:
Six Sides of the Cube
  • Describe:  How would you describe your issue or topic?
  • Compare/Contrast:  What is your topic similar to or different from?
  • Analyze:  How would you break the topic into smaller parts?
  • Apply:  How can this information be used in other situations?  Or, can you find examples to illustrate your topic?
  • Significance:  Why is this topic important?
  • Argue:  What is your position on this topic?
41
Cubing Sample
  • Topic:  Texting while Driving
  • *Note:  This is an abbreviated sample
  • Describe:  When drivers text, their eyes leave the road and they become distracted.  They are a danger to themselves, their passengers, pedestrians, and fellow drivers.
  • Compare:  Texting while driving can be compared to drunk driving.
  • Analyze:  I could write about the compulsion to text, the effects of texting while driving, the way the media discusses the issue, or strategies people use to prevent themselves from texting.
  • Apply:  I should look up famous cases of texting while driving.  Have any of them gone to court?
  • Significance:  The issue is important because texting while driving presents a significant danger to people travelling the roads.  As a society, we should strive to have safe roads and responsible drivers.
  • Argue:  I argue that fines for texting while driving should be increased.  In addition, people caught texting while driving should be required to take a special traffic school course.

Part 4:
Outlining Your Essay
44
What Is an Outline?
  • An outline is an organized list that breaks a topic into subtopics.  You can use your outline as a plan for developing your essay.
45
Benefits of Outlining
  • An outline will help make your essay more organized.  A careful plan will help keep your body paragraphs focused on the ideas in your thesis statement.
  • An outline saves time for writers.  Preparing an outline may take time, but when it is done, you’ll be able to write your essay more quickly.
46
Outline Structure
  • Essay Outline


  • Introduction Thesis: _____________________


  • Body Paragraph #1-  Topic Sentence: _____________
  • A.  supporting idea
  • B.  supporting idea
  • III. Body Paragraph #2-  Topic Sentence: ____________
  • A.  supporting idea
  • B.  supporting idea
  • IV. Body Paragraph #3-  Topic Sentence: ____________
  • A.  supporting idea
  • B.  supporting idea
  • V. Conclusion
47
Process
  • Begin with your thesis statement.  It will be your guide throughout the process of writing your essay.  (For more information, you can take the Thesis Development workshop).
  • Create topic sentences to support your thesis.  Each topic sentence should have a Roman Numeral (I, II, III, IV).
  • For each topic sentence, list supporting details, examples, or ideas.  Each support should have a capital letter (A, B, C).
48
Outline Sample
  • Thesis:  Cell phone users should avoid upgrading their phones frequently.
  • Frequent upgrades create waste
    • Old phones aren’t recycled often enough
    • Some phones are thrown in the garbage rather than being disposed of properly
  • Frequent upgrades allow companies to create a “disposable” product
    • Product doesn’t have to last a long time
    • Companies use upgrades to get higher profits from customers
  • Manufacturing new phones strains natural resources
    • Because the phones aren’t recycled, we have to manufacture phones from new materials
    • Example:  Children in Africa mining coltan for use in phones
49
Using Your Outline
  • Once you are happy with your outline, you can use it to guide your writing for the rest of the essay.  It will help you stay on topic and present your ideas in an organized fashion.
50
Activity 4
  • Use the invention work you did in Activity 3 to create a short outline on your topic.  You should develop a thesis statement and three main topic sentences.
  • Write your outline directly on your worksheet.
51
Final Tips
  • You don’t have to write your essay in order.  For example, if you’re struggling with the introduction, you can begin with one of the body paragraphs.
  • Don’t worry about making the essay perfect from the beginning.  First, get your ideas on paper.  Then you can revise them to make sure they sound how you want them to sound.
  • Write yourself notes.  If you get stuck, leave a note for yourself and move on to a different section.  You can return to the note later.
  • Take breaks.  Give yourself some time away from your essay.  Trying to write the entire paper at once can be very overwhelming.
