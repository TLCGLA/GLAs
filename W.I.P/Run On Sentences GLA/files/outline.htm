Mythbusting
  • Some students think that run-ons are sentences that are too long.
  • However, this is not the case.  It is possible to have quite long sentences that are grammatically correct.

  • Any detached observer, viewing the events of the gruff patriarch's life from a safe yet reasonably proximate distance, might have thought my father (now dearly departed, for he died of a heart attack while driving his unwieldy sedan towards the local Denny's on Route 50, where he planned to enjoy a caloric and fatty breakfast of strawberry pancakes, dripping bacon, slightly golden potatoes, and lukewarm coffee with a group of former coworkers who, like my father, had been forced by an indifferent company—recently acquired by that frugal race the Dutch—into early retirement before the age of fifty) to be a very laconic man; indeed, he was rather a recluse and never, to my knowledge, willingly sought society, although he did enter sweepstakes, fearing, however, that if by some strange glimmer of chance he should ever win, the prize-givers would bring T.V. cameras to his very doorstep and expect him to act like a fool for the amusement of thousands of commercial viewers, thereby exposing him to the world he had sought so diligently to avoid, a world of people mad enough to desire phones in their cars, when he himself could not tolerate the thought of people calling him at home.

Mythbusting
  • Similarly, some short sentences are run-ons.  For example:
  • I ran she didn’t.
  • This sentence, while only four words, is a run-on.  That is because it is made up of two independent clauses that are not separated from each other at all.

Defining “Run-On Sentence”
  • A run-on sentence is made up of two independent clauses
  • that have not been joined correctly.


  • In other words, a run-on happens when a writer has two complete thoughts but doesn’t know how to connect the two properly.  In this workshop, we’ll look at several strategies for joining complete thoughts correctly to help you avoid run-ons.


  • Before we look at strategies for avoiding run-ons, we have to understand independent clauses.

What is an independent clause?
  • An independent clause includes:
  • A subject (the noun the sentence is about)
  • A verb (what the subject does or is)
  • A complete thought (the sentence makes sense on its own)


  • It is independent because it can stand on its own.
15
Let’s look at an example
  • Example:  Janine taught her dog to roll over.


  • Subject: Janine
  • “Janine” is a noun, and the sentence is about her.


  • Verb: taught
  • “Taught” tells readers what Janine did


  • Complete thought:  The sentence makes sense on its own.  Readers have all the information they need to understand what the writer is trying to say.
  • This is an independent clause, also known as a simple sentence.
16
Let’s look at another example
  • Example:  Karl wrote his name in the sand.


  • Subject: Karl
  • “Karl” is a noun, and the sentence is about him.


  • Verb:  wrote
  • “Wrote” tells readers what Karl did.


  • Complete thought:  The sentence makes sense on its own.  Readers have all the information they need to understand what the writer is trying to say.
17
So how does this relate to Run-Ons?
  • Remember, a run-on sentence is made up of two independent clauses
  • that have not been joined correctly.


  • In other words, a run-on occurs when a writer joins two independent clauses without separating them properly.


  • This can occur in two ways:
  • Fused Sentences
  • Comma Splices
18
Fused Sentences
  • A fused sentence occurs when a writer uses two independent clauses without separating them at all.


  • Example:
  • Lorena bought a new car she needs to update her insurance.


  • Explanation:
  • The sentence has two independent clauses:
  • Lorena bought a new car.
  • She needs to update her insurance.


  • Because the clauses are independent, they both stand alone.  They cannot be combined without some form of separation.
19
Comma Splices
  • A comma splice occurs when a writer uses two independent clauses and only separates them with a comma.


  • Example:
  • Lorena bought a new car, she needs to update her insurance.


  • Explanation:
  • The sentence has two independent clauses:
  • Lorena bought a new car.
  • She needs to update her insurance.


  • Because the clauses are independent, they both stand alone.  They are separated with a comma, but a comma isn’t strong enough to separate two independent clauses by itself.  In other words, comma splices are still grammatical mistakes.

PART 3:
CORRECTING AND AVOIDING RUN-ONS
22
Section Overview
  • This section will show you a number of ways to correct run-on sentences.  These strategies will help you fix both fused sentences and comma splices.
  • There are many ways to write grammatically correct sentences; it’s up to you to choose the best strategy for each individual sentence.
23
Strategy 1
  • Separate the independent clauses with a period.


  • Incorrect: Miguel studied electrical engineering he wanted to work for Tesla.
  • Correct: Miguel studied electrical engineering.  He wanted to work for Tesla.
24
Strategy 2
  • Separate the independent clauses with a semicolon.
  • This separates the two clauses efficiently; however, it also shows that they should remain linked.  The writer wants readers to receive both pieces of information at the same time.


  • Incorrect: Natalie got her first chemistry set when she was six, she believes it sparked her interest in the sciences.


  • Correct: Natalie got her first chemistry set when she was six; she believes it sparked her interest in the sciences.
25
Strategy 3
  • Separate the independent clauses with a comma AND a coordinating conjunction.
  • The coordinating conjunctions are:  for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so.  You can remember them with the acronym FANBOYS.


  • Incorrect: Oscar wants to write screenplays for a living, he is double majoring in film and English.


  • Correct: Oscar wants to write screenplays for a living, so he is double majoring in film and English.
26
Strategy 4
  • Separate the independent clauses with a semicolon, conjunctive adverb, and a comma.
  • The conjunctive adverbs are:  accordingly, also, besides, consequently, conversely, finally, furthermore, hence, however, indeed, instead, likewise, meanwhile, moreover, nevertheless, next, nonetheless, otherwise, similarly, still, subsequently, then, therefore, thus


  • Incorrect: Pam and Quincey designed their company logo together Quincey took credit for all their work.


  • Correct:  Pam and Quincey designed their company logo together; however, Quincey took credit for all their work.
27
Strategy 5
  • Separate the independent clauses with a subordinate conjunction.
  • The subordinate conjunctions are:  after, although, as, because, before, even if, even though, if, in order that, once, provided that, rather than, since, so that, than, that, though, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whereas, wherever, whether, while, why


  • Incorrect: Rosie plays tennis on her days off it helps her relieve stress.


  • Correct: Rosie plays tennis on her days off because it helps her relieve stress.
28
Strategy 6
  • Put the subordinate conjunction at the beginning of the sentence.  Then, separate the clauses with a comma.


  • Remember, the subordinate conjunctions are:  after, although, as, because, before, even if, even though, if, in order that, once, provided that, rather than, since, so that, than, that, though, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whereas, wherever, whether, while, why


  • Incorrect: Sun developed a business plan investors showed interest in his idea.
  • Correct: After Sun developed a business plan, investors showed interest in his idea.
