Part 1: Reasons for Annotating Texts
4
What is annotation?
  • Annotation is the process of making notes / markings in a text that students are reading for class.


  • Note:  If you don’t want to write on your textbook, you can always use post-its, a notebook, or a word processing program
5
Why do students annotate?
  • To remember material longer
  • To find material more easily
  • To stay focused on the reading
  • To prepare for exams/essays
  • To identify key terms/definitions
  • To help visualize difficult concepts
  • To ask questions
6
Avoid the Foresight Bias
  • The foresight bias is a psychological phenomenon in which people overestimate their memory.
  • In other words, we believe we can’t possibly forget something—and then, we forget it.
  • Don’t let this happen when you’re reading!  Don’t assume that you’re going to remember the material.  Instead, take thorough notes to help you remember the material later.
7
Annotation does take longer
  • Many students avoid annotation because it takes a long time.  Those students aren’t entirely wrong—annotation does take time.  However, the benefits of annotating (even though it takes time) far outweigh the drawbacks.


  • Annotating a text is an investment.  You invest your time by reading and taking thorough notes.  The payoff for that investment is that you will understand and remember the material better.  You will be better prepared for exams, essays, and projects.

Part 2:  Annotation Techniques
9
Annotation Techniques: 
What should I look for?
  • When reading a text for class, you should look for material that falls into three main categories:
  • Information the author thinks is important
  • Information the reader (that’s you) thinks is important
  • Any questions you may have


  • We’ll look at each of these categories separately.
10
What does the author think is important?
  • Our first step in annotating is to find information the author thinks is important.  If we can identify that information, we know that we can comprehend the text.  When we go to class, we’ll be able to complete a quiz about the text or answer the instructor’s questions.
11
How do we know what the author thinks is important?
  • Many authors give readers clues to show what the most important information is.  Below, you’ll find a list of some of those clues.
  • Definitions and Illustrations:  If the author takes the time to define or illustrate a term, it is probably important.
  • Repetition:  If the author repeats an idea, it is probably important.
  • They tell you:  Occasionally, the author will tell you the information is important.
12
Things You Can Tell Just by Looking at the Text:  Other Clues
  • Authors give us other clues about important information in the text that we readers can see just by looking at the text.  They are:
  • Titles, subtitles, and section headers
  • Images
  • Bold or italicized text
  • Change in font color
  • Lists (either bullet point or numbered)
  • Call out boxes
  • Summaries (may appear at the beginning or end of a chapter)
  • Sample assignments/quiz questions (See Pre-Reading workshop for more information)

14
What do you (the reader) think is important?
  • Our second step in annotating is finding information that you think is important.  This will help you develop a response to the author’s ideas.  Some college-level assignments will require you to do more than understand an author’s ideas.  They will ask you to present your reaction to those ideas.  By annotating the information you think is important, you’ll be able to craft your response more easily.
15
How do you know what you think is important?
  • There are a few different ways to find the information that is important to you.  Look for:
  • Information that you have an emotional reaction to (whether the reaction is positive or negative)
  • Information that you agree or disagree with
  • Information directly related to your assignment
  • Information that connects to material you already know or your past experiences

Sample Annotation

17
What questions do you have?
  • The third step in annotating is to make note of any questions you have.  Asking questions will help keep you focused as you read because you will be looking for the author to provide an answer.  Even if the author does not provide an answer, you can either look up the information in another source or ask the question during class time.  This will ensure that you have all the information you need to understand the reading thoroughly.

Sample Annotation
19
Annotation Techniques
  • Now that you know what to look for when reading a text, how should you annotate?
  • Don’t just highlight:  It will be easy to forget why you made the highlighted marks
  • Do write notes in words/phrases:  This will help you find important information later
  • Circle unfamiliar vocabulary:  Once you are done reading, you can look those words up in the dictionary
  • Write brief summaries at the end of chapters  or major sections:  This will help  you remember what occurred in that part of the text
  • Draw:  Sometimes, it is easier to understand concepts by drawing them out.   Consider drawing visual materials, such as molecular structure or the orbits of planets.

Part 3:  Outlining Texts
21
Outlining Texts
  • Turning a reading into an outline provides several benefits for students, including:
  • Remembering what you read
  • Having a ready-made study guide for exams
  • Seeing the organization of the chapter
  • Being able to align the chapter outline with the lecture notes
22
What is an outline?
  • Sample Outline
  • Main topic 1
    • Subtopic 1
    • Subtopic 2
  • Main topic 2
    • Subtopic 1
      • Detail 1
      • Detail 2
    • Subtopic 2
  • An organized list
  • Fairly rigid format
  • Use indents to add more detailed text
  • Every time you use a 1, you must also use a 2
23
How to Outline a Textbook
  • Most textbooks are already written using an outline format, so creating an outline using a textbook is usually fairly easy once you know how.
  • Try using the following system:
  • Chapter title:  Outline Title
  • Section Headers:  Roman Numerals
  • Subheaders:  Capitalized Letters
  • Sub-subheaders:  Arabic Numerals
  • Topic Sentences:  Lower-case letters
24
Sample Outlined Textbook
As new pages appear, the outline will grow longer.
  • Managing Stress and Coping with Life’s Challenges

  • I.  What is Stress?
  • II.  Your Body’s Response to Stress
  • A.  The General Adaptation  Syndrome
  • 1.  Alarm Phase
  • 2.  Resistance Phase
25
What should I include in my outline?
  • Think of  your outline as a study guide for the chapter or reading that you’re studying.  You should include key elements from the chapter that will help you understand the meaning better, including:
  • Vocabulary words and their definitions
  • Dates and events
  • People and what they are known for
  • Processes
  • Formulas
  • Any other material that is relevant to the course or upcoming assignments
26
Characteristics of Strong Outlines
  • Parallelism:  Use parallel structure.  If the first heading is a verb, all other headings must be verbs.
  • Coordination:  Information at the same level of the outline should have the same level of importance.  The information in Roman Numeral I should be just as important as the information in Roman Numeral II.
  • Subordination:  The information in the headings should be more general than the information in the subheadings.
  • Division:  Each heading should be divided into 2 or more parts.

Part 4: Reviewing a Text
29
Reviewing a Text
  • After you have read, annotated, and outlined a text, you should review what you’ve learned.  This will help you remember the material better; you will be prepared to discuss the material in class, write about the material for an assignment, or use the material on a test or quiz.


  • Please note:  You don’t have to do all the review strategies; instead, find the strategies that suit your study style the best.  Keep in mind that different strategies work well in different situations.
30
Review Strategy 1:
What did I learn?
  • Brainstorm a list of the information you learned from reading the text.  This could include:
  • Information you found interesting
  • Information you expect to see on a test
  • Information related to an assignment
  • Information connected to your classes, field of study, or career.
31
Review Strategy 2:
Write a summary
  • Write a brief summary of the text. A summary is your explanation of what a text is about.  You will write the main points of the text.
  • Guidelines:
  • Summaries should be shorter than the original text.
  • Summaries will cover the main points of the original text.  When you write a summary, you will leave out details.
  • Summaries should be objective.  This means they will tell readers what the text is about, not what you think or feel about the text.
32
Review Strategy 3:
Write a sample exam
  • Write a sample exam for your reading.  This could include objective questions (short answer, true/false, multiple choice) or essay questions.
  • Later, you can quiz yourself to make sure you remember the answers.  In addition, sometimes instructors will use the same essay questions that you anticipate.  Guessing questions in advance will help you prepare for an essay.
33
Benefits of Reviewing the Reading
  • By annotating, outlining, and reviewing your readings carefully, you will…
  • Stay engaged in the reading
  • Retain information longer
  • Find information more quickly
  • Be better prepared for quizzes, exams, and assignments
