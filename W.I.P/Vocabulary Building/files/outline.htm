"In the previous slide"
  • In the previous slide, we were able to guess what “novice” meant.  We used context to help us guess.


  • “Context” means “the surrounding words and ideas.”  This means we use the words and ideas around an unfamiliar word to figure out the word’s meaning.


  • When you come across a new vocabulary word, do not reach for the dictionary right away.  Instead, try to make a logical guess based on the context of the new word.
8
"There are four different ways..."
  • There are four different ways to use context clues:


  • The writer tells you what a word means through definitions, explanations, and synonyms
  • The writer provides examples to illustrate the word’s meaning
  • The writer tells you what a word does not mean through contrast and antonyms
  • The reader figures out the meaning of a word through the general sense of the passage
9
"Sometimes"
  • Sometimes, sentences will include definitions for vocabulary words.  The sentences use context markers to show they are providing a definition.


  • Some context markers are:  in other words, that is, this means


  • Sample Sentence:
  • Professor Wu gets so excited about discussing history that she sometimes digresses from her lecture notes.  In other words, she strays from her main point.


  • We may not know what digress means.  But, we can use the context marker “in other words” to find a definition.  From the context marker, we see that digress means straying from the main point.
10
"Writers may include explanations of..."
  • Writers may include explanations of a word’s meaning.


  • Some context markers to show explanations include:  so that, in other words, that is, it means


  • Sample Sentence :
  • Arid regions are characterized by a severe lack of water.


  • The writer explains that arid refers to an area that experiences a severe lack of water.  As readers, we can guess that deserts are arid.
11
"Writers may use synonyms to..."
  • Writers may use synonyms to show what a word means.  Synonyms are words that mean the same or almost the same thing.


  • Some context markers for synonyms are: or, in other words.  The writer might also use dashes, parentheses, or commas


  • Sample Sentence :
  • “The children are the future” is a hackneyed or overused phrase.
12
"Instead of telling you what..."
  • Instead of telling you what a word means, some writers will show you what a word means by using examples.  Your job as a reader is to interpret those examples.


  • Some context markers for examples are:  such as, for instance, for example
13
"Sample Sentences"
  • Sample Sentences:
  • Sam was committed to showing integrity in his life.  As a child, he refused to let his friends copy his homework.  As an adult, he reported his mistakes to his boss immediately.


  • To figure out what integrity means, we must look at the examples the author gives us.  How are refusing to let a friend copy and reporting mistakes similar?


  • The examples are similar because they both relate to honesty.  So, we can guess that “integrity” means “honesty.”
14
"Sample Passage"
  • Sample Passage:  Prison reformers want to help ex-convicts avoid recidivism.  The Bureau of Justice found that about two thirds of ex-convicts were rearrested within three years of their release.  They also found that 76.9% of drug offenders were rearrested.


  • To figure out what recidivism means, we look at the writer’s examples.  The passage gives statistics about ex-convicts being rearrested.  From those statistics, we can guess that recidivism means “return to crime after release from prison.”
15
"In some cases"
  • In some cases, authors will tell you what a word does not mean by providing an opposite example.  They are showing the differences between terms.


  • Context markers include: however, in contrast, instead of, on the other hand, on the contrary, but, yet
16
"Sample Passage"
  • Sample Passage:
  • Alberto spends his money as soon as he earns it.  He loves to buy the most expensive, luxurious items that he can get his hands on—even if he can’t really afford them.  However, his brother Ernie is far more frugal.


  • The writer gives us a thorough description of Alberto’s spending habits.  The use of the word “however” suggests that Ernie is the opposite of his brother Alberto.


  • We can guess that frugal means economical, sparing, or thrifty; in other words, that Ernie doesn’t spend his money lavishly.
17
"Sample Passage"
  • Sample Passage:
  • Regina auditioned for the school play, but her performance was mediocre.  In contrast, her best friend Katie’s audition was extraordinary.


  • In the passage above, “extraordinary” is the antonym of mediocre.  (An antonym is a word that means the opposite of another word.)  This means that mediocre means ordinary.
18
"Sometimes"
  • Sometimes, readers can determine what a word means by getting a general sense of the passage.  The writer may not use context markers.  Instead, it is up to the reader to make the best guess possible.
19
"Sample Passage"
  • Sample Passage:
  • The explorer was excited to meet the denizens of the Amazon rainforest.  He wanted to learn about how they lived, what they ate, and how they crafted clothes and tools.


  • We can guess what denizen means by examining the different things the explorer wants to learn.  It is clear that he wants to learn about people.  In addition, he is interested in a particular place: the Amazon rainforest.  We can guess that denizen means “people who live in a particular place.”
20
"Sample Passage"
  • Sample Passage:
  • The cash register was missing money again.  The manager wanted to figure out which employee was culpable before contacting his district manager or the police.


  • We can guess what culpable means by thinking about the rest of the passage.  We know a crime has been committed, and the manager is trying to figure something out.  We can guess that culpable means “at fault” or “guilty.”
21
"Some words are built from..."
  • Some words are built from different parts.  We can use those parts to help determine a word’s meaning.



  • The prefix “im” means “not”
  • The base “percept” means “perceive”
  • The suffix “ible” means “able”
  • Therefore, “imperceptible” means “unable to be perceived.”

"The English language often borrows..."
  • The English language often borrows words from other languages.  If you speak another language, you can use it to help you decipher English vocabulary words.


  • Castigate = to punish
  • In Spanish, “castigar” means “to punish”
  • Diaphanous = sheer, transparent
  • In Greek, “diafanís” (διαφανής) means “transparent”
  • Carat = a unit of weight used for measuring gems
  • In Arabic, “qirat” (قيراط) is the weight of four grains
24
"Some English words sound like..."
  • Some English words sound like words in other languages, but the meanings aren’t the same.  These are called false cognates.


  • For example, many English speakers mistakenly believe the Spanish word “embarazada” means “embarrassed.”  The word actually means “pregnant.”

"Now that you know how..."
  • Now that you know how a word is pronounced and what it means, you need to own that word.  This means using the word as part of your regular vocabulary.


  • One way to begin using a word is to make a connection with your real life experience.  How does this new word relate to you?

"If I were beginning to..."
  • If I were beginning to use the word ubiquitous, I would look up the definition first, which is:  being present everywhere.


  • Then, I would try to think of an example.  For example, in urban and suburban areas Starbucks is ubiquitous.  I can find a Starbucks just about anywhere.


  • By making personal connections, you have made the word part of your life.  You aren’t going to forget it.
33
"After practicing using a word"
  • After practicing using a word, it becomes part of your life.  However, you need to store the word and use it from time to time.


  • There are several ways you can store the word:
  • Keep a vocabulary journal.  Enter all the new vocabulary in alphabetical order (using the English alphabet).
  • Make flashcards and practice with them frequently.
  • Set a weekly goal to acquire 5-7 new vocabulary words.
